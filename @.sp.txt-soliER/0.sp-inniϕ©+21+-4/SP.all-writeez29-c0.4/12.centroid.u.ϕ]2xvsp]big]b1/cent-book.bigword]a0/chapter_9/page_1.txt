It did not arrive with a bang, or
a roar, or a flash of light.
It arrived with a whisper. A slow,
silent, and inexorable creep of wrongness.
The gray sky over The Centroid, which
had been a symbol of their despair,
began to darken. Not the darkness of
night, which is full of stars and
dreams and the promise of a new
day. But a flat, absolute, and soul-crushing
blackness. A blackness that did not just
block the light, but consumed it. A
blackness that was the absence of everything.
The Nothing had arrived. The air grew
cold, not with the chill of winter,
but with the cold of the void.
A cold that seeped into the very
stones of The Centroid, into the very
hearts of its inhabitants. The creative energy
of the school, which had been a
flickering ember, was now in danger of
being extinguished completely. A silence fell over
the school. Not a peaceful silence, but
a dead silence. The silence of a
world that had forgotten how to sing.
The students and professors, who had been
living in a state of quiet despair,
now felt a new and terrifying emotion.
Fear. A primal, existential fear that came
from the very core of their being.
They could feel The Nothing. They could
feel its hunger. They could feel its
presence. A vast, ancient, and malevolent
presence that was coming for them. For
their world. For their very souls. The
fortress of harmony, the network of resonating
sculptures that Piper and Atlas had built,
began to activate. The resonators glowed with
a soft, golden light, their cosmic wind
chimes beginning to sing a gentle, harmonious
song. It was a song of defiance,
a song of hope, a tiny spark
of light in the overwhelming darkness. But
The Nothing was a storm. A hurricane
of oblivion. And the fortress of harmony
was a fragile, beautiful, and utterly inadequate
shield against its fury. The first movement
of the symphony of salvation had begun.
But it was a symphony that was
in danger of being silenced before it
had even had a chance to be
played. The initial chaos had begun. The
students and faculty, who had been listless
and apathetic, were now in a state
of panic. They ran through the halls
of The Centroid, their faces a mask
of terror, their screams swallowed by the
oppressive silence of The Nothing. They were
creators, but they had forgotten how to
create. They were dreamers, but they had
forgotten how to dream. They were children
of the light, and they were being
consumed by the darkness. The Nothing was
not just an enemy. It was a
disease. A plague of the soul. And
it was spreading fast. The final battle
had begun. And it was a battle
that they were already losing.
