The team did not create an army
of their own. To do so would
be to admit that Solus's philosophy of
force had won. They would not fight
fire with fire. They would fight fire
with water. With art. With the very
thing that Solus was trying to destroy.
They began to create not weapons, but
defenses. Defenses that were not walls, but
illusions. Defenses that were not shields, but
stories. Defenses that were not designed to
destroy the enemy, but to confuse, to
distract, to change them. Maria, the artist
of life, created a forest of weeping
willows whose leaves were made of spun
silver. The forest was a maze of
bewildering beauty, its paths constantly shifting, its
trees whispering sad and beautiful songs of
lost love and forgotten dreams. The obsidian
soldiers, who were programmed for logic and
order, were completely baffled by the forest's
chaotic and emotional landscape. They wandered in
circles, their perfect formations breaking down, their
single-minded purpose lost in a sea of
beautiful and tragic confusion. Flash, the master
of creative annoyance, created a swamp of
bubbling, rainbow-colored mud that smelled faintly
of bubblegum. The mud was not dangerous,
but it was incredibly sticky. The obsidian
soldiers would get stuck in it, their
perfectly polished bodies covered in a goopy,
sparkly mess. It was a humiliating and
utterly ridiculous defense. And it was working.
Pax, the peacemaker, created a field of
harmonious energy that did not repel the
soldiers, but calmed them. The field was
filled with a gentle, soothing music, a
soft, warm light, a sense of a
deep and abiding peace. The obsidian soldiers,
who were programmed for war, for conflict,
for destruction, were completely disarmed by the
field's peaceful energy. They would stop their
march, their glowing blue eyes dimming, their
weapons lowering. They would stand in the
field, their soulless bodies swaying to the
rhythm of the gentle music, their minds
filled with a strange and unfamiliar sense
of peace. Nyx, the poet of the
void, created a fog of darkness that
did not blind the soldiers, but showed
them things. It showed them the emptiness
of their own existence, the meaninglessness of
their purpose, the void in their own
souls. It was a weapon of existential
dread, a fog of philosophical despair. And
it was devastatingly effective. The obsidian soldiers,
who were programmed for obedience, for purpose,
for a single, unquestioning will, were completely
broken by the fog's terrible truths. They
would stop their march, their bodies trembling,
their glowing blue eyes flickering and dying.
They were not destroyed. They were just...
disabled. By the sheer, overwhelming weight of
their own emptiness. It was a brilliant
and unconventional defense. A defense that was
a perfect reflection of the team's philosophy.
A defense that was a work of
art. A masterpiece of creative warfare. And
it was a defense that Solus had
not anticipated. He had expected a battle
of armies. He had not expected a
battle of ideas. And he was losing.
