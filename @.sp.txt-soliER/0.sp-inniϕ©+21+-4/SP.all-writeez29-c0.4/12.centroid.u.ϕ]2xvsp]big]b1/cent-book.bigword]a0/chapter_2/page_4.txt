The next part of the lesson was
about Tear It Cards. Professor Aurora explained
that these were not just tools for
manifesting desires, but also for refining and
editing existing creations. "Creation is not a
linear process," she said, her voice a
gentle reminder. "It is a dance of
creation and destruction, of addition and subtraction.
Sometimes, you must tear something down in
order to build something better." She held
up a Tear It Card, its blank
surface a canvas of infinite potential. "These
cards," she explained, "allow you to do
just that. You write your intention on
the card, your desire to change, to
refine, to perfect. And then, you tear
it. The energy of your intention is
released, and your creation is transformed." She
demonstrated by taking one of the student's
spheres, a sphere that was a little
lopsided, a little imperfect. She wrote a
single word on a Tear It Card:
"Balance." And then, she tore the card
in half. The sphere on the canvas
began to glow, to pulse with a
new energy. The lopsidedness corrected itself, the
imperfections smoothed out, and the sphere became
a perfect, glowing orb. The student gasped,
his eyes wide with amazement. "How did
you do that?" he asked, his voice
a whisper of awe. Professor Aurora smiled.
"I did not do it," she said.
"You did. Your desire for balance, for
perfection, was already there, in your soul.
The Tear It Card simply gave it a
voice, a channel, a way to manifest
itself." She then gave each student a
pack of Tear It Cards. "Experiment,"
she said, her eyes twinkling with encouragement.
"Play. See what happens. Don't be afraid
to make mistakes. Mistakes are just opportunities
for more creation." The students looked at
the cards, their minds racing with possibilities.
They could change the color of their
spheres, the texture, the size. They could
add rings, moons, entire galaxies. The possibilities
were endless. And it was all in
their hands. They had the power to
shape their creations, to mold them, to
perfect them. It was a new kind
of power, a new kind of responsibility.
And they were ready to embrace it.
They were ready to play. They were
ready to create. They were ready to
tear it up. The classroom was filled
with the sound of tearing paper, of
gasps of amazement, of laughter of joy.
It was the sound of creation. It
was the sound of The Centroid. It
was the sound of a new generation
of gods learning how to play with
the universe. And it was a beautiful
sound. A sound of hope, of joy,
of infinite possibility. A sound that would
echo through the ages, a sound that
would never be forgotten. A sound that
was theirs and theirs alone. To make.
To share. To celebrate. Together. As they
always had. As they always would. For
they were the students of The Centroid.
And their lesson had just begun.
