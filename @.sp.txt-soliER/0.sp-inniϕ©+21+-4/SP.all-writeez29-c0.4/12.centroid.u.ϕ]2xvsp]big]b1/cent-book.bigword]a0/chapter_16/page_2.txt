It was Astra who first came up with
the answer. She had been spending her
days in the great library of The
Centroid, not studying the ancient texts of
cosmic power, but reading the small, personal
stories of ordinary people. The stories of
love, of loss, of joy, of sorrow.
The stories that made up the beautiful
and chaotic messiness of life. And in
those stories, she found her inspiration. "We've
been thinking too big," she said, her
voice a quiet and thoughtful melody. "We've
been so focused on saving the universe,
on creating new realities, on changing the
very fabric of existence. But we've forgotten
about the small things. The things that
make life worth living." She looked at
her friends, her eyes shining with a
new and exciting idea. "What if our
next adventure isn't a cosmic one?" she
asked. "What if it's a personal one?
What if we stop trying to save
the universe, and we start trying to
live in it?" It was a radical
idea. A strange and beautiful idea. An
idea that was so simple, so obvious,
that they had completely overlooked it. They
had been so focused on the grand,
cosmic drama that they had forgotten about
the small, human drama. They had been
so focused on being heroes that they
had forgotten how to be people. And
in that moment, they realized that their
next great adventure was not out there,
in the farthest reaches of the multiverse.
It was right here. Inside themselves. Inside
their own hearts. Their own souls. Their
own, strange, and beautiful lives. They decided
to take a break from being the
guardians of the multiverse. They decided to
take a vacation. A long and well-deserved
vacation from the business of saving the
universe. They decided to enroll in The
Centroid. Not as teachers, or mentors, or
legends. But as students. As ordinary, everyday
students. They wanted to learn how to
paint, how to sculpt, how to write
a poem. They wanted to fall in
love, to have their hearts broken, to
make new friends, to go to parties,
to do all the silly, stupid, and
wonderful things that ordinary people do. They
wanted to be normal. Or as normal
as a group of eternally young, all-powerful,
cosmic beings could be. It was a
new kind of quest. A quest for
the ordinary. A quest for the mundane.
A quest for the beautiful and chaotic
messiness of life. And it was a
quest that was just as important, just
as challenging, and just as heroic as
any they had ever been on before.
The final confrontation was not with a
monster, or a god, or a void.
It was with the simple and profound
challenge of living a happy and meaningful
life. And it was a challenge they
were ready to face. Together. As a
family. As a group of friends who
had saved the universe and were now
ready to save themselves.
