The rescue mission was a desperate, high-stakes gamble. The ‘Straylight’ was a freighter, not a warship. They were outgunned, outmanned, and outmatched. To fly into the heart of a civil war was suicide. But the crew of the ‘Straylight’ had never been ones to back down from a suicide mission.

They devised a plan that was both insane and brilliant, a plan that was pure, unadulterated ‘Straylight.’ They would not fight their way to the mining colony, but dance their way through it. Lyra, her fingers a blur on her strange, multi-stringed instrument, began to play. It was a song of pure, chaotic, and utterly infectious joy, a melody that was so out of place in the grim, brutal reality of the civil war that it was almost an act of madness.

The song, broadcast on all frequencies, had a strange, and unexpected, effect on the warring factions of the Sol Pen. They were soldiers, spies, assassins, their minds and souls hardened by a lifetime of violence and betrayal. They were not prepared for a song. They were not prepared for joy. The music, a virus of pure, unadulterated happiness, spread through their ranks, a seed of chaos in the perfect, orderly machine of their war. They faltered, their attacks becoming clumsy, their strategies dissolving into a confused, contradictory mess. The war, for a single, beautiful, and utterly chaotic moment, ground to a halt.
