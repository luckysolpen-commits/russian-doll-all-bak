The crew of the ‘Straylight’ was faced with a new, and dangerous, dilemma. They could intervene, try to stop the bloodshed, but to do so would be to reveal themselves, to expose their secret war to the galaxy. Or they could stand by and watch as the Sol Pen tore itself apart, a strategy that was both tempting and deeply, morally ambiguous. Could they, the self-appointed guardians of a fragile galactic peace, stand by and do nothing as a world burned?

Once again, the crew was divided. Kaelen, the pragmatist, argued for non-intervention. The Sol Pen were destroying themselves. Why should they get in the way? Lyra, the idealist, argued for intervention. They had a moral obligation to help the innocent civilians who were caught in the crossfire. Jaxon, the haunted clone, was silent, his mind a battlefield of conflicting emotions. He saw the strategic advantage in letting the Sol Pen destroy themselves, but he also saw the faces of the innocent, the victims of a war they had not chosen.

In the end, the decision was made for them. A desperate, garbled distress call from the surface of Kith reached the ‘Straylight.’ It was from a small, independent colony of miners who were trapped between the two warring factions of the Sol Pen. They were out of food, out of water, out of time. They were a small, insignificant detail in a vast, cosmic conflict. But to the crew of the ‘Straylight,’ they were everything.
