The galaxy, freed from the overt tyranny of the XVS and the insidious
manipulation of the Sol Pen, was not at peace. It was in a state
of glorious, messy, and unpredictable chaos. The Unlit Tribe, once a
rebellion, was now a guiding philosophy, a loose confederation of worlds
and individuals who embraced the balance of light and shadow. But
balance is a dynamic state, not a static one, and the
forces of the old order, though defeated, were not destroyed.

Vorith, the ambitious XVS officer who had been swept away by
the tide of revolution, had not vanished into the unlit void.
He had retreated, regrouped, and was now plotting his return. He saw
the galaxy’s newfound freedom not as a liberation, but as an
invitation to chaos, a dangerous experiment that would inevitably lead to
self-destruction. He believed in order, in control, in a single, absolute
truth, and he was determined to restore it, by force if necessary.

His new philosophy, which he called ‘The Art of Subtle Dominion,’ was
a chilling counterpoint to Jureth’s ‘Book of Jureth.’ It was a
treatise on control, on manipulation, on the subtle art of bending
the will of others without them even realizing it. It was a
philosophy of insidious power, a weaponized form of order that promised
peace, but delivered slavery. And he was gathering his forces, not
armies, but ideas, whispers, and the lingering doubts in the hearts
of a galaxy that was still reeling from the trauma of war.

Meanwhile, the Unlit Tribe, in its embrace of chaos, was struggling
with its own internal conflicts. The very freedom they championed was
a double-edged sword. Without a central authority, without rigid rules,
the galaxy was a wild, untamed frontier, and not everyone was
ready for it. Old prejudices resurfaced, new conflicts erupted, and the
messy, beautiful chaos they had fought for was beginning to look
a lot like anarchy. The original Anglers, now scattered across the
galaxy, were becoming legends, their wisdom sought by many, but their
presence a distant memory.

Jureth, the reluctant prophet, found himself increasingly isolated. His philosophy, once
a beacon of hope, was now being misinterpreted, twisted, used to
justify acts of selfishness and chaos. He saw the seeds of
the old tyranny sprouting in the fertile ground of their new
freedom, and he knew that the war for the soul of the
galaxy was far from over. It had simply shifted, from a
battle of light against dark, to a battle of chaos against
order, a battle that was being fought not with fleets and
armies, but with ideas, with philosophies, with the very definition of
freedom itself.
