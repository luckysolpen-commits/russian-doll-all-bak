The battle for the refugee moon was not a battle of fleets and armies, but of wills and ideas. The Unlit Tribe, their small, ragtag fleet hopelessly outmatched by Vorith’s golden armada, did not fight back with weapons, but with a song. It was the same song that Jureth had sung on Apex, a song of doubt, of struggle, of the messy, beautiful, and necessary imperfection of life. It was a song that did not promise victory, but offered something far more valuable: the freedom to choose.

The song spread through the ranks of Vorith’s fleet, a virus of doubt in the perfect, orderly machine of his power. The soldiers of the golden armada, their minds and souls already touched by the words of the Book of Jureth, began to question. They looked at the small, insignificant moon, at the faces of the refugees, and they saw not an enemy, but themselves. They saw a people who were not fighting for power, or for glory, or for a single, absolute truth, but for the simple, profound, and inalienable right to exist.

The golden armada faltered. A single ship, then another, then a dozen, then a hundred, lowered their weapons. The perfect, orderly machine of Vorith’s power was grinding to a halt, its gears gummed up by the messy, beautiful, and necessary chaos of individual conscience.
