The ‘Stargazer’ slipped away from the tranquil, pastel beauty of Aether,
leaving behind a world slowly awakening from a long, beautiful nightmare.
The silence on the bridge was heavy, thick with the weight of
their recent victory and the chilling implications of the Aetherians’ final
revelation. ‘The Unlit.’ The name echoed in their minds, a stark
and terrifying counterpoint to the vibrant, light-filled worlds they had sworn
to protect. It suggested not just an absence of light, but an
active, malevolent darkness, a void with intention. The Sol Pen were
not just conquerors; they were heralds of this nothingness.

Zara was recovering in the med-bay, her mind a quiet landscape
after the psychic storm. Luna watched over her, her scientific curiosity
mingling with a deep, personal concern. The battle for Aether had
been won, but it had taken a toll on them all. The
scars were not physical, but they were deep, etched into the
very fabric of their minds. Jax felt it in the quiet moments
between commands, a new weariness that had nothing to do with
physical exhaustion. He had faced armies and monsters, but this new
enemy was something else entirely. It was a war of ideas, of
emotions, of the very definition of reality. And it was a
war they were fighting blind.

It was during this quiet, contemplative journey through the void that
a new presence made itself known. He was not a member of
the original seven, but a young Angler, a recent graduate from
the XVS academy, assigned to the ‘Stargazer’ for his first deep-space
mission. His name was Jureth, and he was a cartographer, his
angler a soft, inquisitive light that seemed to drink in the
star charts and nebulae with an insatiable thirst. He was quiet,
observant, and possessed an intellect that rivaled even Luna’s. He had
remained in the background during the previous missions, a silent apprentice
learning from the masters.

But something had changed in him after Aether. He began to
spend long hours in the observation deck, his gaze fixed not
on the brilliant, swirling galaxies, but on the empty spaces between
them. He started having visions, fleeting, fragmented images that came to
him in his sleep. He saw a vast, silent ocean of
perfect, peaceful black. He saw creatures of immense power and grace
moving through this void, their forms made not of light, but
of a beautiful, shimmering darkness. He saw a universe where life
did not need stars to flourish, where existence was not defined
by a struggle against the dark, but by an embrace of
it. He saw ‘The Unlit.’

He tried to dismiss the visions as a symptom of psychic fatigue,
an echo of the dream-eater’s influence. But they felt too real,
too coherent. They held a strange, compelling logic that resonated with
a part of him he had never known existed. He confided
in Luna, his voice a hushed whisper in the sterile confines
of the med-bay. He spoke of his visions, of the ‘unlit
void,’ of a philosophy that was the complete antithesis of everything
the XVS stood for. Luna, ever the scientist, listened with an
open mind, her curiosity piqued by this strange, new data point.
She did not dismiss him. She encouraged him to document his
visions, to explore this new, heretical line of thought. The seeds
of doubt, the first, tiny cracks in the foundation of their
cherished beliefs, had been sown.
