Jureth’s philosophy of the ‘unlit path’ began to spread, not as a formal doctrine, but as a series of quiet conversations in the corridors of the ‘Stargazer.’ His writings, which he had titled ‘The Book of Jureth,’ were passed from hand to hand, a samizdat text of heretical ideas. The crew, their faith in the old ways shaken, were hungry for a new way of understanding the universe, a way that acknowledged the shades of gray, the necessary balance of light and dark. Jureth, to his own surprise, found himself becoming the reluctant leader of a quiet, burgeoning revolution.

Vorith, ever watchful, saw the growing influence of Jureth’s ideas as a direct threat to his own authority, to the very foundation of the XVS. He could not attack Jureth directly – the young cartographer was too popular, too respected. So, he resorted to a more subtle, insidious form of warfare. He began to spread rumors, to sow seeds of doubt, to paint Jureth not as a philosopher, but as a heretic, a traitor, a sympathizer with the very forces they were fighting.

The Anglers, fiercely loyal to their young, unassuming prophet, closed ranks around him. They saw Vorith’s machinations for what they were: a desperate attempt to maintain control, to cling to a rigid, outdated ideology that was no longer fit for purpose. The ‘Stargazer’ became a microcosm of the larger conflict, a ship divided, a crew forced to choose between the old ways and the new.
