You kill the lights and scramble into a small service crawlspace behind the cockpit. It's a tight fit, and the air is thick with the smell of old wiring and ozone. You hold your breath, every muscle tensed.

The security team enters the shuttle. You can hear their boots on the metal floor. They do a quick sweep of the cockpit and cargo bay. "Clear," one of them says. "Must have been a sensor ghost."

"Alright," the team leader grunts. "Let's move."

You hear them leave. You wait for five agonizing minutes, your heart pounding in your ears. Then you slip out of the crawlspace. The hangar is empty. You have a second chance. You work with frantic, silent efficiency, your fingers flying across the controls. You bypass the final security protocols, and the shuttle's engines hum to life.

You don't bother with the hangar doors. The shuttle is small, and its kinetic barriers are rated for micrometeor impacts. You push the throttle to the max. The shuttle lurches forward, smashing through the hangar's main viewport in an explosion of transparisteel and emergency alarms.

You're out. You are in the vacuum of space, in a stolen, damaged ship, with the entire Lunar base on high alert. You have no flight plan, no destination, and very little fuel. But you are free.
