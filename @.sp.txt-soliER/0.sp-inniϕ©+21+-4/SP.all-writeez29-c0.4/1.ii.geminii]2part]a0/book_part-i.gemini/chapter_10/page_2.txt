As they rounded a particularly large pile of rubble, a
structure emerged from the gloom, a stark contrast to
the surrounding decay. It was a courthouse, just as Elias
had described, but it was pristine. Its white marble facade
gleamed, untouched by the grime and destruction that had
consumed the rest of the city. Its windows, though dark,
were intact, reflecting the faint, bruised light of the
sky. The building stood tall and proud, a defiant beacon
of order in a world consumed by chaos, an impossible anomaly
that seemed to defy the very laws of their ruined reality.

"The Nexus," Astra breathed, her voice filled with a mixture
of awe and trepidation. Her scientific mind, usually so
focused on objective facts, struggled to reconcile the
perfect symmetry of the building with the surrounding
disorder. She felt a strange pull, a magnetic force drawing
her towards its impossible perfection. "It's... it's exactly
as Elias described. Unbelievable." Her hand instinctively
reached for her tablet, as if to confirm the impossible
reality before her eyes.

Gem, usually so quick with a quip or a colorful observation,
was silent, her mouth slightly agape. Her bright pink
hair seemed to lose some of its vibrancy in the stark
white light reflecting off the marble. She felt a prickle
of unease, a sense of something deeply unnatural about
this untouched edifice. It was too perfect, too clean,
too... wrong. "It's like a ghost," she whispered, her voice
hushed, "a ghost of the old world, haunting the new."
She shivered, despite the warmth of the Steel Caravan's
cabin, a cold dread seeping into her bones.

Lucky, his face grim, brought the Steel Caravan to a slow,
controlled stop a few hundred meters from the courthouse.
He cut the engine, and the sudden silence was deafening,
broken only by the faint creak of the truck's cooling
metal and the distant, unsettling sounds of the city's
decay. He scanned the building's perimeter with his binoculars,
his eyes narrowed, searching for any hidden traps, any
sign of life. His gut, a reliable compass in this treacherous
world, screamed at him that something was profoundly wrong.
This level of preservation was not natural; it was engineered.

But as they drew closer, the reason for its pristine condition
became chillingly clear. The courthouse was not abandoned.
It was patrolled. Figures in dark, identical uniforms
marched with robotic precision along its perimeter, their
weapons held at the ready, glinting dully in the faint
light. They were heavily armed, their faces obscured by
visors that reflected the bruised sky, their movements
synchronized, unnervingly perfect, like a macabre ballet
of death. Each step was identical, each turn of the head
precisely timed. There was no hesitation, no individuality,
just a chilling, mechanical efficiency.

"Clone guards," Lucky muttered, his hand instinctively
going to the lever that controlled the flamethrower, a
familiar weight that offered a small measure of comfort.
"Just as Elias warned. He said they were... unsettlingly
uniform." He remembered Elias's frantic scrawls in the
journal, the desperate warnings about the Architect's
"perfect soldiers." The reality was far more disturbing
than the written word. These weren't just men in uniforms;
they were extensions of a single, cold will.

Gem's eyes widened, a flicker of genuine fear replacing
her usual artistic curiosity. "They're... identical," she
whispered, her voice barely audible. "Like a terrifying
army of mannequins. It's like a horror movie, but real."
She felt a profound sense of revulsion, a deep-seated
discomfort at the sight of such manufactured uniformity.
It was an affront to her very being, to her belief in
the vibrant, messy individuality of life. The air around
them seemed to grow colder, charged with an unspoken threat.
