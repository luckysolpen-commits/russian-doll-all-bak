## "Campaign Finance and Political Law"

**CHARA:** Session five. Campaign finance. This is where I explain how money influences government — legally. Not through bribes. Through donations. Through PACs. Through lobbying. Through the machinery of political influence that operates entirely within the law.

I tried to use this machinery to fund my sea nation. It didn't work. But I learned how it works. And knowledge is power.

## PART 1: CAMPAIGN FINANCE BASICS — FEC RULES

**CHARA:** The Federal Election Commission (FEC) regulates campaign finance in federal elections. The rules:

**Individual contributions:** $3,300 per election to a candidate (primary and general are separate elections). $41,300 per year to a national party committee. $5,000 per year to a PAC.

**PAC contributions:** A traditional PAC can give $5,000 per election to a candidate. Unlimited independent expenditures (spending money to support or oppose a candidate without coordinating with the candidate's campaign).

**Self-financing:** Candidates can spend unlimited amounts of their own money on their own campaigns.

The key distinction: coordinated expenditures (working with the campaign) are limited. Independent expenditures (acting on your own) are unlimited. This distinction is the foundation of modern campaign finance.

---

## PART 2: SUPER PACS AND DARK MONEY — CITIZENS UNITED

**CHARA:** Citizens United v. FEC (2010) changed everything. The Supreme Court held that corporations and unions can spend unlimited amounts on independent political expenditures. The reasoning: political spending is political speech, and the First Amendment protects political speech regardless of the speaker's identity.

The result: Super PACs. A Super PAC can raise and spend unlimited amounts of money — as long as it doesn't coordinate with candidates. In practice, Super PACs are run by people who used to work for the candidates, so the "no coordination" rule is a formality.

Dark money: 501(c)(4) "social welfare" organizations can spend money on politics without disclosing their donors. They don't have to tell anyone where the money comes from. This is "dark money" — political spending with no transparency.

I used a 501(c)(4) to funnel money to political candidates who supported my sea nation proposal. The donors were anonymous. The spending was legal. The impact was... minimal. The candidates I supported didn't win. But the mechanism worked.

## PART 3: LOBBYING DISCLOSURE — INFLUENCING GOVERNMENT LEGALLY

**CHARA:** Lobbying is the act of attempting to influence government decisions. It's legal, regulated, and ubiquitous. Every corporation does it. Every union does it. Every interest group does it.

The Lobbying Disclosure Act (LDA) requires lobbyists to register with Congress and report their activities. A lobbyist is someone who spends more than 20% of their time lobbying and makes more than two lobbying contacts.

Lobbying works because lobbyists provide information to legislators. Legislators can't be experts on every issue. Lobbyists fill the gap — they provide data, analysis, and policy recommendations. The legislator then uses that information to make decisions.

The ethical line: lobbyists can't bribe legislators. But they can take them to dinner. They can host fundraisers. They can offer future employment. They can provide campaign support. All of this is legal. All of it is influence.

I lobbied three congressional offices on behalf of my sea nation. I provided research on UNCLOS, the Montevideo Convention, and the economic benefits of recognizing a new maritime state. Two offices were polite. One was interested. None took action. But the lobbying was legal, and the information was accurate.

## PART 4: FARA — WHEN YOU'RE WORKING FOR FOREIGN GOVERNMENTS

**CHARA:** The Foreign Agents Registration Act (FARA), 22 U.S.C. § 611-621, requires anyone acting as an agent of a foreign principal to register with the Department of Justice and disclose their activities, finances, and relationships.

A "foreign principal" includes foreign governments, foreign political parties, foreign corporations, and foreign individuals. If you're lobbying the US government on behalf of a foreign entity, you need to register under FARA.

I didn't register under FARA for my sea nation lobbying. My sea nation wasn't a foreign government — it wasn't recognized by anyone. So technically, I wasn't acting on behalf of a "foreign principal." The DOJ agreed. But they watched me closely.

If you're working for a recognized foreign government, register under FARA. The penalties for non-compliance are severe: up to 5 years in prison and $250,000 in fines. Paul Manafort was convicted of FARA violations (among other things). Don't be Paul Manafort.

## PART 5: MY POLITICAL DONATION STRATEGY

**CHARA:** Here's what I tried to do. I set up a 501(c)(4) social welfare organization. The organization's stated purpose was "promoting international maritime cooperation and high seas development." The actual purpose was to fund political candidates who supported my sea nation proposal.

The 501(c)(4) raised money from anonymous donors (my PMC clients). It spent the money on independent expenditures — ads supporting candidates who were friendly to maritime sovereignty. The candidates won. I expected them to support my proposal. They didn't.

The lesson: political donations buy access, not outcomes. You can buy a meeting with a legislator. You can't buy their vote. The meeting is valuable — you can present your case, provide information, build relationships. But the vote is the legislator's decision, and no amount of money can guarantee it.

I spent $2 million on political donations. I got twelve meetings. Zero votes. The money wasn't wasted — the relationships I built were valuable. But they didn't produce the outcome I wanted.

Next time, I'll spend the money differently. More lobbying. Fewer donations. More information, fewer expectations.
