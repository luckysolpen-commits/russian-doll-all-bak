## PART 1: WHAT MAKES A NATION? — THE MONTEVIDEO CONVENTION

**CHARA:** The Montevideo Convention on the Rights and Duties of States (1933) is the definitive statement on what makes a nation a nation. Article 1 says a state must have:

1. **A permanent population** — people who live there. Not tourists. Not visitors. Residents.
2. **A defined territory** — land, water, or structures with clear boundaries.
3. **A government** — an organized political authority that exercises control over the territory and population.
4. **The capacity to enter into relations with other states** — diplomatic capability.

That's it. Four criteria. If you meet all four, you're a state under international law.

My oil rig ecosystem met all four. I had a permanent population (12 residents — me and my crew). I had a defined territory (the rig complex and its 500-meter exclusion zone). I had a government (me, as provisional president, with a written constitution). I had the capacity to enter into relations with other states (I drafted diplomatic notes, sent them to embassies, and waited for responses).

The problem wasn't the criteria. The problem was recognition.

---

## PART 2: RECOGNITION — WHY IT MATTERS

**CHARA:** Recognition is the unwritten fifth criterion. You can meet all four Montevideo criteria and still not be a state if no one recognizes you.

Recognition comes in two forms:
- **De jure recognition** — formal, legal recognition of your statehood. This is what matters.
- **De facto recognition** — practical acknowledgment that you exist and control territory. This is a stepping stone to de jure recognition.

The US recognizes 195 countries. The UN has 193 member states. If you're not on those lists, you're not a state in the eyes of the international system.

Micronations — Sealand, Liberland, Molossia, Hutt River — meet the Montevideo criteria to varying degrees. None of them are recognized. They're hobbies, not countries.

But here's the thing: recognition is political, not legal. Countries recognize other countries for political reasons, not legal ones. Kosovo is recognized by 100 countries but not by Serbia, Russia, China, or Spain. Palestine is recognized by 138 countries but not by the US or Israel. Taiwan is recognized by 12 countries but functions as a fully independent state.

Recognition is a game of politics. And I know how to play politics.

## PART 3: MICRONATIONS — WHAT WORKED, WHAT DIDN'T

**CHARA:** Let me review the most notable micronations and what we can learn from them.

**Principality of Sealand** — A WWII sea fort in the North Sea, occupied by Paddy Roy Bates in 1967. Bates claimed sovereignty under the Montevideo Convention. A British court ruled that Sealand was outside UK jurisdiction (the fort was in international waters). A German court implicitly recognized Sealand when it negotiated for the release of a detained German citizen. Sealand has its own flag, currency, passports, and government. It's the most successful micronation in history. But it's not recognized by any UN member state.

**Free Republic of Liberland** — A 7 km² parcel of unclaimed land between Croatia and Serbia, claimed by Vít Jedlička in 2015. Jedlička argued that the land was terra nullius (nobody's land) because neither Croatia nor Serbia claimed it. He met the Montevideo criteria (he had a constitution, a flag, and a few dozen residents). But Croatia arrested anyone who tried to enter. Liberland exists on paper but not in practice.

**Republic of Molossia** — Kevin Baugh's backyard in Nevada, claimed as a sovereign nation since 1977. Molossia has a constitution, a currency, a postal service, and a space program (a model rocket). It's adorable. It's not a country.

The lesson: you need territory that no existing country wants to defend. Sealand worked because the UK didn't care about a rusty sea fort. Liberland failed because Croatia cared about the border. Molossia is a joke because it's someone's backyard.

My oil rig was in international waters — beyond any country's territorial claim. That was the key. No country had a legal basis to object. They objected anyway (the US Coast Guard boarded my rig). But their objection was political, not legal. And political objections can be overcome with political pressure.

## PART 4: THE PMC LEGAL STRUCTURE — MERCENARIES WITH LAWYERS

**CHARA:** A Private Military Company (PMC) is a business that provides military services to governments, corporations, and other organizations. Blackwater (now Academi), DynCorp, G4S — these are PMCs. They're legal, regulated, and enormously profitable.

The legal structure of a PMC:
1. **Incorporation** — register as a corporation in a jurisdiction with favorable laws (Delaware, Nevada, or offshore like the British Virgin Islands).
2. **Licensing** — obtain the necessary licenses to provide military services. In the US, this means ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations) registration and State Department approval.
3. **Contracts** — negotiate contracts with clients. The contracts define the scope of services, the rules of engagement, the liability limitations, and the payment terms.
4. **Compliance** — comply with international law (the Geneva Conventions, the UN Mercenary Convention) and domestic law (the US Anti-Pinkerton Act, the War Crimes Act).

The UN Mercenary Convention (1989) defines a mercenary as someone who is specially recruited to fight in an armed conflict, motivated by private gain, and not a national of a party to the conflict. PMCs avoid this definition by providing "security services" rather than "combat services." The distinction is thin but legally significant.

My PMC was registered in the British Virgin Islands. It provided "temporal security consulting" to three governments. The contracts were classified. The payments were substantial. The legal gray area was enormous. But it was legal — or at least, not provably illegal.

## PART 5: CONTRACTING WITH GOVERNMENTS — HOW PMCs GET PAID

**CHARA:** Governments hire PMCs for three reasons:
1. **Plausible deniability** — the government can deny involvement in sensitive operations.
2. **Cost efficiency** — PMCs are cheaper than maintaining a standing military.
3. **Specialized expertise** — PMCs have skills that regular militaries don't.

The contracting process:
1. **Request for Proposal (RFP)** — the government publishes a request for military services.
2. **Bid** — the PMC submits a proposal outlining capabilities, personnel, equipment, and cost.
3. **Award** — the government selects a PMC and negotiates the contract.
4. **Execution** — the PMC performs the services.
5. **Payment** — the government pays according to the contract terms.

The money is in the execution. A typical PMC contract is worth $50 million to $500 million per year. The largest PMC contracts (Blackwater in Iraq, DynCorp in Afghanistan) were worth billions.

My PMC's contracts were smaller — $10-20 million per year per client. But I had three clients. And the work was specialized (temporal security consulting). And the clients were governments that didn't appear on any public contract database.

The money was real. The work was real. The legal framework was... flexible.
