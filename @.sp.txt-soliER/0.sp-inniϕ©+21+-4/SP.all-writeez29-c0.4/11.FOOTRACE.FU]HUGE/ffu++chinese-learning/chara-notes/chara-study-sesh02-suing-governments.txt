## "Suing Governments — Sovereign Immunity and Its Loopholes"

---

**CHARA:** Session two. Suing governments. This is where it gets hard — and where it gets interesting. Because governments have a legal shield called sovereign immunity. They can't be sued unless they agree to be sued. But they've agreed to be sued in specific ways, and those ways are the loopholes I'm going to teach you about.

I've got my tea — black, 200 degrees — and I've got the UN case file open. The one I lost. The one I'm going to win next time. Let me explain why I lost, and why the next one will be different.

## PART 1: SOVEREIGN IMMUNITY — THE SHIELD

**CHARA:** Sovereign immunity is the legal doctrine that says "the king can do no wrong" — or in modern terms, the government can't be sued without its consent. It comes from English common law. The American founders inherited it. And they kept it, mostly.

The federal government has sovereign immunity under the Eleventh Amendment and centuries of common law. State governments have it too. Foreign governments have it under the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act (FSIA).

The practical effect: you can't sue the United States government unless there's a specific law that says you can. You can't sue a state unless there's a specific law that says you can. You can't sue a foreign government unless there's a specific law that says you can.

But there are specific laws. And those are the loopholes.

---

## PART 2: BIVENS ACTIONS — SUING FEDERAL OFFICIALS

**CHARA:** Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents (1971). The Supreme Court held that you can sue federal officials personally for constitutional violations. Not the government — the individual officials.

The logic: if a federal agent violates your constitutional rights, they're acting outside the scope of their authority. They're not the government anymore — they're just a person who broke the law. And you can sue them personally.

Bivens actions cover:
- Fourth Amendment violations (unreasonable search and seizure)
- Fifth Amendment violations (due process, equal protection)
- Eighth Amendment violations (cruel and unusual punishment)

The damages come out of the official's personal pocket. Not the government's. The official's. That's why Bivens actions are so powerful — they make individual officials personally liable for constitutional violations.

The catch: the Supreme Court has been narrowing Bivens for decades. In recent cases (Ziglar v. Abbasi, 2017; Hernandez v. Mesa, 2020), the Court has refused to extend Bivens to new contexts. So Bivens is limited to the three amendments above. If your claim doesn't fit, you're out of luck.

I tried a Bivens action against a CIA official who authorized an illegal surveillance operation. The case was dismissed because the Court refused to extend Bivens to First Amendment violations. I lost. But I preserved the argument for appeal. And I learned something: Bivens is dying. You need other tools.

---

## PART 3: SECTION 1983 — SUING STATE ACTORS

**CHARA:** 42 U.S.C. § 1983 is the most powerful civil rights statute in American law. It says: anyone who, under color of state law, deprives another person of their constitutional rights is liable for damages.

"Under color of state law" means acting as a government official — police officers, prison guards, public school administrators, state agency employees. If they violate your constitutional rights while doing their job, you can sue them under Section 1983.

Section 1983 covers all constitutional rights — not just the three that Bivens covers. First Amendment (speech, religion), Fourth Amendment (search and seizure), Fifth Amendment (due process), Eighth Amendment (cruel and unusual), Fourteenth Amendment (equal protection) — all of them.

The damages come from the official personally AND from the municipality (city, county, school district) if the violation was caused by an official policy or custom. This is called a Monell claim (Monell v. Department of Social Services, 1978).

I've filed three Section 1983 cases. One against a police department for excessive force (settled for $450,000). One against a prison for medical neglect (won on summary judgment, $1.2 million). One against a school district for censorship (dismissed on qualified immunity — more on that below).

Section 1983 is the workhorse of civil rights litigation. If you're suing a state or local government, this is your statute.

## PART 4: QUALIFIED IMMUNITY — THE SHIELD THAT PROTECTS OFFICIALS

**CHARA:** Qualified immunity is the doctrine that protects government officials from personal liability unless they violated "clearly established" constitutional rights. "Clearly established" means a prior court case with essentially identical facts already ruled that the conduct was unconstitutional.

The practical effect: if no prior case exists with the exact same facts, the official gets qualified immunity — even if their conduct was obviously unconstitutional.

This is the single biggest barrier to civil rights litigation. Courts have dismissed cases because the prior cases weren't "close enough" — the suspect was running east instead of west, the officer used a taser instead of a baton, the victim was handcuffed with one hand instead of two. Absurd distinctions.

The Supreme Court has made it worse. In Ashcroft v. al-Kidd (2011), the Court said prior cases must be "sufficiently similar" that "every reasonable official" would understand the conduct was unconstitutional. In City of Escondido v. Emmons (2019), the Court reversed a Ninth Circuit decision that had denied qualified immunity, saying the lower court defined the right too broadly.

How to defeat qualified immunity:
1. Find a prior case with nearly identical facts (the harder the better)
2. Argue that the conduct was so obviously unconstitutional that no reasonable official could have thought it was legal (the "obviousness" exception — rarely successful but occasionally works)
3. Sue the municipality instead of the individual (Monell claims don't have qualified immunity)
4. Sue under state law (qualified immunity is a federal doctrine — state law may not have it)

I lost my school district case on qualified immunity. The prior cases were about student newspapers, not student websites. The court said the distinction mattered. I think it didn't. But the court's decision stands unless the Supreme Court says otherwise.

## PART 5: THE FEDERAL TORT CLAIMS ACT — WHEN THE GOVERNMENT WAIVES IMMUNITY

**CHARA:** The Federal Tort Claims Act (FTCA), 28 U.S.C. §§ 1346(b), 2671-2680, is the federal government's waiver of sovereign immunity for tort claims. It says: you can sue the United States for the negligent or wrongful acts of federal employees, acting within the scope of their employment.

Key points:
- You can only sue for negligence or wrongful acts — not intentional torts (with some exceptions)
- The employee must have been acting within the scope of employment
- You sue the United States, not the individual employee
- Damages are limited — no punitive damages, no interest
- There are exceptions — the "discretionary function exception" protects policy decisions, the "intentional tort exception" protects against assault, battery, false imprisonment, etc.

The FTCA is how you sue the federal government for things like: medical malpractice at a VA hospital, car accidents by federal employees, property damage by federal contractors, and negligence by federal law enforcement.

I filed an FTCA claim against the CIA for negligence in handling classified information that led to a contractor's fraud. The claim was administratively denied (you must exhaust administrative remedies before filing in court). I filed in federal court. The government moved to dismiss under the discretionary function exception. The court agreed — the CIA's handling of classified information was a "discretionary function" protected by sovereign immunity. I lost. Again.

But I learned: the FTCA is narrow. The exceptions are broad. You need a very specific type of claim to get through.

## PART 6: MY UN CASE — WHAT HAPPENED AND WHAT I'D DO DIFFERENTLY

**CHARA:** Okay. The UN case. Here's what happened.

I tried to sue the United Nations for damages related to a peacekeeping operation that went wrong. The UN has absolute immunity under the International Organizations Immunities Act (IOIA), 22 U.S.C. § 288. The IOIA gives international organizations "the same immunity from suit... as is enjoyed by foreign governments."

Foreign governments have absolute immunity under the FSIA, with limited exceptions (commercial activity, torts committed in the US, etc.). The UN's immunity is even broader — it's absolute, with no exceptions.

I argued that the UN's immunity should be limited — that it shouldn't cover gross negligence and human rights violations. The court disagreed. The UN's immunity is absolute. Period. Case dismissed.

What I'd do differently:
1. Sue the individual member states, not the UN. Each member state has its own immunity rules. Some are narrower than the UN's.
2. Sue in a foreign court. The US gives the UN absolute immunity. Other countries might not.
3. Use the UN's internal dispute resolution process. The UN has an administrative claims process. It's not great, but it's something.
4. Build political pressure. Sometimes the best way to beat immunity is to make the immune party settle to avoid bad publicity.

I'm not done with the UN case. I'm just waiting for the right angle. And the right court. And the right facts.
