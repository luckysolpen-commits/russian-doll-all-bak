## PART 1: WHY YOU'D WANT TO SUE SOMEONE

**CHARA:** People think lawsuits are about money. Sometimes they are. But mostly, lawsuits are about leverage. A lawsuit is a legally sanctioned threat. You're telling someone: "I believe you've wronged me, and I'm going to use the government's power to make you pay for it." The threat of a lawsuit — the cost, the discovery, the public record, the reputational damage — is often more powerful than the lawsuit itself.

I learned this at the CIA. Not in training. In practice. A contractor overbilled us by $2.3 million. My supervisor wanted to let it go — "it's just paperwork," he said. I filed a False Claims Act qui tam action. The contractor settled for $8 million plus legal fees. My supervisor got promoted. I got a target on my back. Worth it.

But you don't need to be CIA to sue someone. You just need to know the rules. And the rules are public. Anyone can read them. Anyone can use them. Most people don't. That's your advantage.

---

## PART 2: JURISDICTION — WHERE TO FILE

**CHARA:** Before you file a lawsuit, you need to figure out where to file it. This is called jurisdiction. There are two types you need to know about:

**Personal jurisdiction** — does the court have power over the defendant? Generally, you can sue someone where they live, where they work, or where the harm occurred. If a company does business in a state, you can usually sue them there. If someone defames you online, you can sue them where you live (because the harm occurred where you read the defamatory statement).

**Subject matter jurisdiction** — does the court have power over this type of case? State courts handle state law claims (contracts, torts, property). Federal courts handle federal law claims (constitutional violations, federal statutes, diversity cases where the parties are from different states and the amount exceeds $75,000).

Here's the practical advice: if you can choose between state and federal court, choose federal court. Federal judges are appointed for life (they're less political). Federal procedure is more predictable. Federal discovery is more powerful. And federal judges actually read motions — unlike some state court judges who are just trying to clear their docket.

I filed my CIA contractor case in federal court (Eastern District of Virginia — the Rocket Docket, fastest civil docket in the country). The contractor tried to remove it to state court. I fought the removal. I won. The case stayed in federal court. That was half the battle.

---

## PART 3: DRAFTING THE COMPLAINT

**CHARA:** The complaint is the document that starts the lawsuit. It has three parts:

**Part 1: Jurisdictional statement.** Tell the court why it has power over this case. "This court has jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1331 because this case arises under federal law." Or: "This court has jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1332 because the parties are citizens of different states and the amount in controversy exceeds $75,000."

**Part 2: Statement of facts.** Tell the story. Who did what to whom, when, where, and how. Be specific. "On January 15, 2024, Defendant submitted Invoice #4472 to Plaintiff for $2.3 million in services. The services were never rendered. Defendant knew the services were never rendered. Defendant submitted the invoice anyway." Every fact gets its own numbered paragraph. No argument. Just facts.

**Part 3: Causes of action.** The legal theories. "Count I: Fraud. Defendant knowingly made a false representation of material fact. Plaintiff relied on the false representation. Plaintiff was damaged as a result." Each cause of action gets its own count. Each count cites the relevant law. Each count explains how the facts satisfy the legal elements.

The golden rule of complaint drafting: plead enough facts to be plausible, but not so many that you box yourself in. The Supreme Court's standard from Bell Atlantic v. Twombly (2007) and Ashcroft v. Iqbal (2009) is "plausibility" — the complaint must allege enough facts to make the claim plausible on its face, not just possible.

My CIA complaint was 47 pages, 200 paragraphs, 4 counts (False Claims Act, fraud, breach of contract, unjust enrichment). The judge called it "a model of clarity." The contractor's attorney called it "a work of fiction." The jury called it "convincing." I got $8 million.

## PART 4: SERVICE OF PROCESS

**CHARA:** You can't just mail the complaint to the defendant. You have to "serve" them — legally deliver the complaint and a summons (the court's official notice of the lawsuit). Service must be done by someone who is not a party to the lawsuit — a process server, a sheriff, or anyone over 18 who isn't you.

Methods of service:
- **Personal service** — hand the papers to the defendant directly. Best method. Hardest to challenge.
- **Substituted service** — leave the papers with someone of suitable age at the defendant's home or office. Acceptable if personal service isn't possible.
- **Service by mail** — mail the papers with a return receipt. Only works in some jurisdictions and for some types of defendants.
- **Service on a corporation** — serve the registered agent or an officer of the corporation. Every corporation has a registered agent — you can find it through the state's business registry.

The defendant has 21 days (federal court) or 30 days (many state courts) to respond after being served. If they don't respond, you can get a default judgment — you win automatically. But courts are reluctant to grant default judgments, especially against corporations. They'll usually give the defendant extra time.

I served my CIA contractor through their registered agent in Delaware. The agent signed the receipt. The clock started ticking. 21 days later, the contractor filed a Motion to Dismiss. I was ready.

---

## PART 5: ANSWERING A LAWSUIT — WHEN YOU'RE THE DEFENDANT

**CHARA:** Sometimes you're the one being sued. Here's what to do.

First: don't panic. A lawsuit is just a claim. It's not a judgment. The plaintiff has to prove their case. You have the right to defend yourself.

Second: don't ignore it. If you don't respond within the deadline (21 days federal, varies by state), you lose by default judgment. The plaintiff can then garnish your wages, levy your bank accounts, and put liens on your property.

Third: file an Answer. The Answer responds to each paragraph of the complaint — admit, deny, or "lack sufficient knowledge to admit or deny" (which is basically a denial). You also assert affirmative defenses — reasons why you win even if the plaintiff's facts are true.

Common affirmative defenses:
- **Statute of limitations** — the plaintiff waited too long to sue
- **Failure to state a claim** — even if everything is true, there's no legal violation
- **Contributory negligence** — the plaintiff caused their own harm
- **Unclean hands** — the plaintiff acted badly too
- **Arbitration clause** — the dispute must be resolved through arbitration, not court

I've been sued twice. Once by a former employer (breach of non-compete — I won on statute of limitations). Once by a business partner (fraud — I won on unclean hands, because he was doing the same thing he accused me of). Both times, I filed my Answer within 21 days. Both times, I asserted every affirmative defense that applied. Both times, the plaintiffs eventually dropped the case.

---

## PART 6: MOTION TO DISMISS — THE NUCLEAR OPTION

**CHARA:** The most powerful tool in defensive litigation is the Motion to Dismiss under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(6) — failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted.

The argument: even if everything the plaintiff says is true, there's no legal violation. The complaint doesn't allege all the elements of a valid claim. So the case should be dismissed immediately — no discovery, no depositions, no trial.

The standard: Twombly/Iqbal plausibility. The complaint must allege enough facts to make the claim plausible on its face. Not just possible. Plausible. A claim is plausible if the court can reasonably infer that the defendant is liable based on the facts alleged.

How to file one: within 21 days of being served (or with your Answer). Brief the legal argument. Cite case law. Explain why the complaint fails the plausibility test. Attach any documents that are referenced in the complaint (the court can consider these without converting the motion to summary judgment).

If the judge grants the Motion to Dismiss, the case is over. Usually with prejudice (meaning the plaintiff can't refile). Sometimes without prejudice (meaning the plaintiff can fix the complaint and try again).

If the judge denies the Motion to Dismiss, the case proceeds to discovery. But you've still won something — you've forced the plaintiff to reveal their legal theory, and you've preserved the argument for appeal if you lose at trial.

I've filed 12 Motions to Dismiss in my career. 8 were granted. 3 were denied. 1 was partially granted. That's a 67% success rate. The key is picking your shots — only file a Motion to Dismiss when the complaint genuinely fails to state a claim. Don't file one just to delay. Judges can tell the difference.

## PART 7: DISCOVERY — WHERE CASEES ARE WON AND LOST

**CHARA:** Discovery is the fact-finding phase of a lawsuit. It's where you get evidence from the other side. It's also where most cases are won or lost — because the evidence usually favors one side, and once both sides see the evidence, they settle.

Three main discovery tools:

**Interrogatories** — written questions that the other side must answer under oath. Limited to 25 in federal court. "Identify all persons with knowledge of the facts alleged in the complaint." "Describe all documents related to Invoice #4472."

**Requests for Production** — demands for documents, emails, texts, recordings, anything relevant to the case. "Produce all communications between Defendant and Plaintiff regarding Invoice #4472." "Produce all financial records related to the services described in Invoice #4472."

**Depositions** — live questioning under oath, recorded by a court reporter. You sit the other side's witnesses in a conference room and ask them questions for 6-8 hours. Their attorney can object, but the witness still has to answer (unless the objection is about privilege). Depositions are where cases break — witnesses contradict their written answers, reveal damaging facts, or just come across as unbelievable.

In my CIA case, I deposed the contractor's CEO. He couldn't explain why Invoice #4472 described services that his own employees confirmed were never performed. He tried to blame a "clerical error." I showed him 47 emails proving he personally approved the invoice. He stopped talking. The deposition transcript was 300 pages. The jury read every page.

---

## PART 8: SUMMARY JUDGMENT — WINNING WITHOUT TRIAL

**CHARA:** After discovery, if the evidence clearly favors one side, that side can file a Motion for Summary Judgment. The argument: there's no genuine dispute of material fact, so the judge can decide the case as a matter of law. No trial needed.

The standard: would a reasonable jury find for the non-moving party? If the answer is no — if the evidence is so one-sided that no reasonable jury could reach a different conclusion — the judge grants summary judgment.

Summary judgment is more powerful than a Motion to Dismiss because it's based on evidence, not just pleadings. You're not saying "the complaint is legally insufficient." You're saying "the evidence proves my case."

I filed for summary judgment in my CIA case after the CEO's deposition. The evidence was overwhelming: the invoice was fraudulent, the CEO knew it, the services were never rendered, the government paid $2.3 million for nothing. The judge granted summary judgment. No trial. No jury. Just a judge reading the evidence and saying "you lose."

That's the dream. Win on summary judgment. Save the time, the money, the stress. But it only works when the evidence is truly one-sided. Most cases aren't. Most cases settle.

---

## PART 9: SETTLEMENT — THE REALITY OF LITIGATION

**CHARA:** 97% of federal civil cases settle before trial. Trials are expensive, unpredictable, and public. Settlement is cheaper, controlled, and private. Most plaintiffs would rather take a guaranteed check than risk losing at trial. Most defendants would rather pay a known amount than risk a larger verdict.

The key to settlement is leverage. Your leverage is your ability to win at trial. If the other side thinks you'll win, they'll offer more. If they think you'll lose, they'll offer less. Your job is to make them believe you'll win.

How to build leverage:
- File a strong complaint (plausible facts, solid legal theories)
- Win early motions (Motion to Dismiss, motions to compel discovery)
- Get damaging deposition testimony
- File for summary judgment (show the evidence is one-sided)
- Make the other side's life expensive (aggressive but lawful discovery)

I settled my CIA case for $8 million. The contractor offered $3 million. I countered at $10 million. We met at $8 million. The contractor's attorney told me later that they would have gone to $12 million if I'd held out. I don't regret it. $8 million was enough. And I avoided the risk of a jury verdict.
