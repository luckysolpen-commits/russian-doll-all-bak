"This should be a safe place to lay low for a while," Astra's voice, calm and measured as always, broke the reverent silence. Her words, transmitted through their comms, were a welcome anchor in the unsettling, ghostly ambience of the dead city. Her bio-enhanced eyes, which glowed with a faint, silver light, were already scanning the surroundings, assessing the structural integrity of the ancient, crumbling buildings, her mind processing the data with a cool, detached efficiency.

They chose one of the larger, more intact structures, a colossal edifice that must have once been a grand hall or a temple, its entrance still surprisingly clear of debris. Slipping inside, they found the water within even more still, the pressure a comforting, heavy blanket after the turbulent currents of the open ocean. The only sound was the gentle, almost imperceptible hum of their life-support systems, a steady, reassuring rhythm against the overwhelming, profound quiet of the ancient, watery tomb.

Gem, unable to sit still, her fiery energy chafing against the enforced inactivity, began to pace, her movements agitated and restless even in the water's gentle resistance. "This is a setup," she declared, her voice tight with a barely suppressed fury. "They wanted to frame you, Lucky. It's too perfect, too clean. They didn't just want you out of the way; they wanted you disgraced, turned into a pariah." She slammed a fist into her other palm, the sound a dull, muffled thud in the water.

Lucky, still grappling with the bewildering, dizzying turn of events, ran a hand through his hair, a gesture of pure, unadulterated exasperation that felt strangely, almost comically, out of place in the water. "But why?" he countered, his voice laced with a desperate, pleading confusion. "The UN got what they wanted. They have my company, Cerberus Inc. I gave it to them on a silver platter. I practically gift-wrapped it for them. Why would they need to frame me? What more could they possibly want?"

Spike, ever the pragmatist, his face a grim, unreadable mask, offered a chilling, and all too plausible, possibility. "Maybe that's not all they wanted," he said, his voice a low, gravelly rumble. "Maybe they wanted you out of the picture for good. You're a loose end, Lucky. A liability. You know too much. You created the cure. You understand the plague better than anyone. To them, you're not a hero; you're a threat. A variable they can't control." The words hung in the water, cold and heavy, chilling in their brutal, undeniable logic. Lucky felt a surge of anger, hot and bitter, rising within him, a burning tide of indignation that threatened to consume him. He had dedicated his life to curing the Carcazod plague, to making the world a better, safer place. And this was his reward? Betrayal, disgrace, and a death sentence.
