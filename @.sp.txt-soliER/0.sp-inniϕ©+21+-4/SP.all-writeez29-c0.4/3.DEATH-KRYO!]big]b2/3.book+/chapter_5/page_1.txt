**Chapter 5: The Beast Awakens**

**Page 1**

The winding, bioluminescent cave system, a natural wonder of the deep, offered a temporary, fragile respite from the chaos they had just escaped. The water, here, was impossibly clear, revealing ancient, sculpted rock formations, smoothed by millennia of relentless currents, and strange, glowing flora that pulsed with a soft, internal light, casting dancing shadows on the cavern walls. Lucky, still wincing from a bruise he'd acquired during their frantic, desperate escape from Carc-Zilla, ran a hand over his aching ribs. The encounter had been a brutal, visceral reminder of the stakes involved, of the monstrous power they were up against.

"So, the UN is weaponizing Carcazod," he muttered, the words tasting bitter, acrid, in his mouth. "That's a new low, even for them. A new level of depravity." He had dedicated his life to eradicating the plague, to bringing hope back to a world ravaged by disease, to healing the wounds of humanity. To see his life's work, his triumph, twisted into a weapon of mass destruction, a tool of genocide, was a profound, personal betrayal, a desecration of everything he believed in.

Dorchester, ever the pragmatist, ever the leader, led the way, her movements fluid and confident through the watery labyrinth. Her eyes, sharp and discerning, scanned every shadow, every crevice, ever vigilant for unseen threats. "They've been at it for years," she stated, her voice devoid of emotion, yet carrying a chilling, undeniable weight. "My intel suggests they're trying to create a new strain, something even more virulent, something that can't be cured. And they're using the clone-merpeople as their test subjects, their unwilling guinea pigs." Her words hung in the water, a dark, horrifying cloud of revelation, a truth so ugly it was almost impossible to comprehend.

"They're experimenting on them?" Gem's voice was sharp with horror, her eyes wide with disbelief, her face contorted in a mask of revulsion. The idea of such a peaceful, advanced civilization, a people who lived in harmony with the ocean, being subjected to such unspeakable atrocities, was almost unbearable. It was a violation of every moral code, every ethical boundary.

"Not directly," Dorchester clarified, her gaze fixed on the path ahead, her voice a low, grim rumble. "They're trying to provoke a conflict. A reason to 'cleanse' the oceans, to justify their extermination. And the merpeople are just collateral damage, a convenient excuse for their true agenda." Her tone was grim, a testament to the cold, hard realities of power and control, of the ruthless machinations of those who sought to dominate the world.
