Chapter 4: Through the Industrial Maze

Lucky and Kael moved through the industrial zone
of the Glitch World,
a stark contrast to the bustling city center.
The buildings here were massive, utilitarian structures
of rusted metal and crumbling concrete,
their surfaces constantly flickering
with visual distortions.
The air was thick with the phantom smell of ozone
and decaying machinery,
a metallic tang that seemed to cling to his tongue.
The sky above was a perpetual, sickly grey,
occasionally streaked with lines of corrupted code,
like digital scars across the heavens.

This area was less populated,
but the few inhabitants they encountered
were more overtly glitched.
A worker on a scaffold,
his body elongated and compressed
like a faulty video stream,
endlessly hammered at a non-existent nail.
A delivery truck drove in a perfect, small circle,
its engine revving loudly,
its driver a blurred, indistinct figure.
“This sector is heavily influenced
by the Metal Mafia’s processing units,” Kael said,
his voice low, pointing to a flickering billboard
that cycled through random numbers.
“The raw data streams are more exposed here.
It’s why the glitches are more pronounced.
And why the brainwashing is more aggressive.”

They passed a group of figures huddled in an alleyway.
They appeared to be homeless,
their clothes tattered, their faces vacant.
But as Lucky got closer,
he saw the subtle, rhythmic flicker in their eyes,
the faint, almost imperceptible hum
emanating from their bodies.
They were not just brainwashed;
they were actively being *processed*.
“They’re… they’re like the man in the library.
But worse,” Lucky whispered,
a chill running down his spine.
“These are the ones who resisted too much, too openly,”
Kael said grimly.
“The system couldn’t overwrite them completely,
so it’s trying to break them down, piece by piece.
Their minds are trapped in endless, repetitive loops of despair.
A form of digital torture.”

Suddenly, one of the figures looked up.
Its eyes, though vacant, locked onto Lucky.
A low, guttural moan escaped its lips,
and its body began to contort,
its limbs stretching and twisting in unnatural ways.
It wasn’t attacking,
but its very presence was disturbing,
a raw manifestation of the Glitch World’s horror.
“Don’t engage,” Kael said, pulling Lucky away.
“Their despair is contagious.
It’s designed to drain your own will.”

They continued their journey,
navigating through abandoned factories and warehouses.
The coordinates led them deeper into the industrial wasteland.
The drones here were less subtle,
larger, and more overtly aggressive,
their red optical sensors sweeping the desolate landscape
with chilling efficiency.
Lucky tried to use his own glitch power,
focusing on a distant drone.
He imagined it flickering, becoming transparent.
For a moment, the drone shimmered,
its outline blurring,
but then it snapped back,
its red eye focusing directly on them.
A high-pitched alert sound blared from its chassis.
“It didn’t work! It saw me!” Lucky exclaimed, frustrated.
“This area has higher security protocols,” Kael said,
pulling him behind a rusted shipping container.
“Your basic visual distortion won’t be enough.
We need to be more precise. More subtle. Or more disruptive.”

He pulled out a small, sleek device from his pocket,
no bigger than his thumb.
He tapped it, and a faint, almost inaudible hum emanated from it.
“This is a frequency scrambler,” Kael explained.
“It won’t hide us,
but it will make their tracking less accurate.
Give us a few precious seconds if they decide to pursue.”
They continued, moving from cover to cover,
the distant wail of the drone fading
as the scrambler took effect.
The landscape became even more desolate,
the buildings more dilapidated.
The air grew colder,
and a faint, metallic taste lingered on Lucky’s tongue.

Finally, they arrived at their destination.
It was a massive, windowless building,
its concrete facade cracked and stained.
The coordinates pointed directly to it.
There were no visible entrances, no signs of life.
It looked like a tomb.
“This is it. The old data center,” Kael said,
looking at the building, a grim satisfaction on his face.
“Offline for decades, they say.
But if the man from the library was right…
the truth is buried deep within its core.”

He approached the wall,
running his hand over the rough concrete.
He closed his eyes, concentrating.
A faint, almost invisible shimmer appeared on the wall,
a subtle distortion in the fabric of the simulation.
It was a hidden access point,
masked by layers of digital camouflage.
“They didn’t want anyone to find this,” Kael continued.
“Which means whatever’s inside… is worth finding.”
He pushed against the shimmering section of the wall.
It gave way with a soft, almost imperceptible click,
revealing a dark, narrow passage.
The air inside was cold, stagnant,
and smelled of ozone and forgotten data.
“What do you think we’ll find in there?” Lucky whispered,
looking into the darkness.
“Either the key to our escape, Lucky,” Kael replied,
a grim smile on his face.
“Or the final piece of the Metal Mafia’s puzzle.
Either way, we’re about to find out.”

They stepped into the narrow passage,
the hidden entrance to the old data center.
The air was heavy, cold,
and smelled strongly of ozone
and the metallic tang of ancient, decaying electronics.
The passage was short,
leading into a vast, cavernous space,
dimly lit by the faint, pulsating glow
of dormant server racks.

The interior of the data center
was a labyrinth of towering, skeletal server racks,
stretching into the gloom.
Cables, thick as pythons,
snaked across the floor and up the walls,
some frayed and sparking with faint, corrupted energy.
Dust motes danced in the few beams of light
that penetrated from unseen vents above.
The silence was profound,
broken only by the soft hum of residual power
and the occasional, unsettling click or whir
from the dormant machinery.
“It’s… huge. And dead,” Lucky whispered.
“Not dead. Dormant,” Kael’s voice echoed slightly
in the vast space.
“The Metal Mafia wouldn’t just abandon a data center.
They would repurpose it, or wipe it clean.
The fact that it’s still here, still humming…
it means something was left behind.
Something they couldn’t, or didn’t want to, erase.”

Kael pulled out a small, handheld scanner.
Its screen flickered with lines of code
and faint energy readings.
He swept it across the room.
“Energy signatures. Faint, but present.
And… data streams. Old ones.
Heavily encrypted, but they’re here.
This place is a digital graveyard,
but the ghosts are still active.”
They began to navigate the maze of server racks.
The floor was uneven,
littered with discarded components
and shattered data chips.
Lucky occasionally kicked a loose cable,
sending a shower of sparks into the darkness.

As they moved deeper, the air grew colder.
The faint hum of the servers became more pronounced,
a low, rhythmic thrum that seemed to vibrate
in Lucky’s bones.
He noticed that some of the server racks,
deeper within the complex,
were glowing with a faint, sickly green light,
different from the usual blue or red of active systems.
“What’s that light?” Lucky asked, pointing.
Kael stopped, his scanner beeping rapidly.
His face, illuminated by the green glow, was grim.
“That’s not a normal system light.
That’s… a processing signature.
And it’s active.
This isn’t just a data center.
It’s a processing unit.
And it’s running something. Something big.”

He approached one of the glowing racks.
The green light pulsed, almost like a heartbeat.
On a small, dusty screen embedded in the rack,
a single word flickered in corrupted, pixelated text:
“P R O C E S S I N G . . .”
And beneath it, a series of rapidly changing numbers,
too fast to comprehend.
Lucky felt a sense of unease.
This wasn’t just data storage;
it was active manipulation.
“This is where they do it.
Where they process the minds.
The brainwashing. The identity erasure.
This is the engine of the Glitch World’s control.”

Suddenly, a low, guttural moan echoed
from deeper within the data center.
It wasn’t a mechanical sound,
but something organic,
something filled with pain and despair.
It sent a shiver down Lucky’s spine.
“What was that?” Lucky whispered.
Kael’s eyes narrowed.
He swept his scanner towards the sound.
The readings on his screen spiked erratically.
“Another prisoner. One who hasn’t been fully processed.
One who’s still fighting.
And they’re close. Very close.”

The green light from the server racks intensified,
casting long, distorted shadows.
The hum of the machinery rose to a low growl.
Lucky and Kael exchanged a look.
They’d found the heart of the Glitch World’s torment.
And they were not alone.
