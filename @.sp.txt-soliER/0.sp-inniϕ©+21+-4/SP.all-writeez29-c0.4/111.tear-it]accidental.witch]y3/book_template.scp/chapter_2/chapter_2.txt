The final words, “Beyond the veil, where ink meets bone,” hung in the silent air of the Hollow, the ink still glistening on the parchment. The power that had flowed through the Soulpen left an echo, a tingling in Elara’s fingertips and a strange, metallic taste in her mouth. Her Inkling, still agitated, reformed itself into a quivering puddle at her feet, its usual playful light dimmed.

A floorboard creaked in the shadows behind her.

Elara spun around, her heart leaping into her throat. The Soulpen felt heavy in her hand, a weapon she didn’t know how to wield. “Who’s there?” she called out, her voice a fragile thread in the vast, dusty silence.

A figure detached itself from the gloom near the base of the spiral staircase, moving with a fluid grace that was almost unnerving. He was young, not much older than her, with dark, watchful eyes that seemed to miss nothing. A faint, shadowy wisp, like a fragment of twilight, coalesced on his shoulder, a creature of living shadow that mirrored her own Inkling. A Shadow Sprite.

“I’ve been listening, Elara Veyn,” the young man said, his voice a low murmur that seemed to be absorbed by the surrounding shelves of forgotten lore. He knew her name. The realization sent a fresh jolt of fear through her.

“How do you know my name?” Elara demanded, taking a half-step back.

“The pen told me,” he said, nodding towards the Soulpen in her hand. “It screams whenever it’s used. You just have to know how to listen.” He took a step forward, his eyes fixed on the pen. “You shouldn’t have it. It’s dangerous.”

“It was my mother’s,” she countered, her fear hardening into defiance.

“Lirien,” he said, and the name on his lips was a mix of old grief and fresh anger. “I knew her. She was reckless, just like you.”

The words stung. “You don’t know anything about me.”

“I know that you’re playing with a fire that will consume you,” he said, his voice softening slightly. “The Soulpen doesn’t create for free, Elara. It takes. It feeds on will, on memories. On the soul itself.”

Elara stared at him, a knot of doubt tightening in her stomach. His warning felt true, resonating with the strange emptiness she’d felt after the pen had answered her questions. But she couldn’t afford to believe him. This pen was her only link to her mother, her only hope.

“You’re lying,” she said, more to convince herself than him.

“Am I?” he challenged. “Try it. Write something simple. Something you know to be true. See what it costs you.”

Her gaze dropped to the pen. His words were a dare, a test she couldn’t refuse. With a trembling hand, she found a clean spot on the parchment and wrote, “The lantern lights.”

The moment the words were complete, a wave of dizziness washed over her. The flame in the nearby lantern flared, burning brighter for a heartbeat, and a sharp, stabbing pain lanced through her temple. It was as if a thread had been pulled from the tapestry of her mind, a small, insignificant memory—the taste of her morning tea, the feeling of the sun on her face—suddenly gone, leaving a hollow ache in its place. Her Inkling whimpered, its form flickering as if in sympathy.

The young man—Torren, as she would soon learn his name was—watched her with a sad, knowing look. “You felt it, didn’t you? A small truth, a small cost. But Lirien… she tried to write something big. Too big. She wrote a door and walked through it, and in doing so, she broke the veil.” He gestured to the world outside the Hollow’s grimy windows. “The chaos you feel, the strange things that happen in the shadows… that’s her legacy. That’s the price we’re all paying for her choice.”
