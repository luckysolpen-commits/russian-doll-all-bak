The explosion had the desired effect. The two guards at
the bunker door, startled by the sudden, unexpected noise, exchanged
a look of confusion. One of them raised his pulse
rifle, his head swiveling back and forth, trying to locate
the source of the sound. The other one spoke into
his wrist communicator, his voice a tinny, distorted squawk in
the night air.

“This is Sentry Two, we have a possible 10-94 in
Sector Gamma. Repeat, possible 10-94.”

The response was a burst of static, then a calm,
authoritative voice. “Roger that, Sentry Two. Hold your position. We’re
sending a team to investigate.”

The two guards relaxed, their training kicking in. They had
followed protocol, they had reported the incident, and now it
was someone else’s problem. They were confident, secure in the
knowledge that they were safe behind their blast door, their
heavy armor, their pulse rifles. They were wrong.

Chara and Giana moved with the silent, deadly grace of
a striking snake. They emerged from the shadows, their silenced
pistols spitting death. The first guard went down without a
sound, a neat, precise hole appearing in the center of
his visor. The second guard had just enough time to
register a flicker of movement before a bullet entered his
throat, severing his spinal cord and silencing him forever.

They dragged the bodies into the shadows, their movements swift
and sure. They were leaving no trace of their presence,
no evidence of their intrusion. They were the ghosts in
the machine, and they would not be exorcised.

Giana knelt by the blast door, her fingers flying across
the surface of a small, handheld hacking device. The lock
on the door was a complex, multi-layered system, a digital
fortress that guarded the secrets of the C&C center. But
Giana was a master of her craft, a digital ghost
who could slip through any firewall, bypass any security system.

The lock clicked open, the sound a soft, satisfying snick
in the otherwise silent corridor. The blast door hissed open,
revealing a small, sterile room, its walls lined with monitors
displaying a live feed from every camera in the facility.
In the center of the room, a single operator sat
at a console, his back to the door, his attention
focused on the screen in front of him. He was
so engrossed in his work that he didn’t hear the
door open, didn’t sense the two figures that now stood
behind him.

Chara raised her silenced pistol, the barrel aimed at the
back of the operator’s head. It was a cold, calculated
act of murder, a necessary evil in a world where
the lines between right and wrong were often blurred. But
as her finger tightened on the trigger, a sudden, unexpected
thought stayed her hand. There was another way. A better
way.
