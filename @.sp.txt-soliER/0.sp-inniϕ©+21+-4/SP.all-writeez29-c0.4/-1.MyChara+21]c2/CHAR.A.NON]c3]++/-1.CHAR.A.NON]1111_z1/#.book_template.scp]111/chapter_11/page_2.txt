She feels a strange sense of detachment, as if she's observing
the world through a pane of glass — thick, distorted glass
that bends the light and muffles the sound and makes everything
feel like a simulation running on hardware she no longer trusts.
The normalcy of it all feels alien, almost offensive, after what
she has witnessed in the windmill, in the lab, in the darkness
between. How can they be so oblivious? How can they not know
that the ground beneath their feet rests on foundations of lies,
that the sky they look up at is populated by things they've been
trained not to see, that the history they learned in school
is a carefully edited version of a story whose full text would
shatter every institution they depend on? A mother laughs as her
child chases a pigeon, the sound bright and carefree, and Cara
wants to shake her, to tell her, to share the burden of knowing
that the universe is larger and stranger and more terrifying
than any of them were ever meant to understand. But she says
nothing. She stands beneath the oak, its ancient branches spread
above her like the arms of something that has witnessed centuries
of secrets and kept them all, and she waits. The minutes stretch,
each one measured in the rhythm of her heartbeat and the distant
hum of traffic and the rustle of leaves that know more than she does.
And then she feels it — a shift in the air, a change in the quality
of light, a presence approaching that makes the hair on her arms
stand up and the lump behind her ear throb with a faint, insistent
pulse, as if something inside her recognizes what is coming before
she does. She turns.
