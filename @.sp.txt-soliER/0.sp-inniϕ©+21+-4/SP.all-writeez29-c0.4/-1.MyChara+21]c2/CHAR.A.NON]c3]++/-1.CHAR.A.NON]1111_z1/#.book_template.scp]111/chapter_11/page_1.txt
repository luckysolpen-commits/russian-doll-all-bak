The town square is a vibrant tableau of everyday life, so normal
it hurts. Children chase pigeons, their laughter echoing through
the air like bells, pure and unselfconscious, the sound of people
who have never had to question whether the sky above them belongs
to them. Commuters hurry past, lost in their own worlds of deadlines
and coffee and the small urgencies that make up a life untroubled
by cosmic revelation. A gentle breeze rustles the leaves of the
ancient oak tree that dominates the center of the square, its
branches spread wide like the arms of something that has witnessed
centuries of human drama and kept its own counsel. Cara stands
beneath those branches, a solitary figure amidst the bustling
activity, clutching the handwritten note like a talisman,
its simple message a stark contrast to the complex, terrifying
reality she now carries inside her skull like a secret tumor.
Her eyes scan the faces in the crowd, searching for a sign,
a flicker of recognition, anything that would tell her she's
not completely alone in a world that has become fundamentally
alien to her. Every face is a question. Every passerby is a
potential threat or a potential ally, and she has no way
of telling the difference. The note burns in her pocket.
