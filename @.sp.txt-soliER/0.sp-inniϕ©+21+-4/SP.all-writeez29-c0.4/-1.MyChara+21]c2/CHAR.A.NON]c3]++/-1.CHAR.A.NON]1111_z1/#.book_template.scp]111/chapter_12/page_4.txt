The next morning, she is gone. Not absent — gone, the kind
of gone that leaves questions instead of answers, that creates
a void where a person used to be and fills it with speculation
and official statements and the kind of careful language that
people use when they know the truth is too dangerous to speak.
Agent Thompson finds her office eerily pristine — no coffee mug,
no scattered files, no personal items, no photograph of a blurred
man, no sign that anyone has lived here, worked here, bled here.
Her ergonomic chair sits empty, pushed back slightly as if she rose
in haste, or was removed in haste. Her secure phone lies centered
on the desk, screen black, battery nearly dead, a single notification
pulsing faintly: "NO SIGNAL." Her black government sedan is missing
from the secure garage. No surveillance hits. No financial activity.
No cell tower pings after 2347. In the CIA Situation Room, officials
deliver a flat, rehearsed statement about classified assignments
and national security. Thompson leans forward, barely containing
his anger. "She wouldn't vanish. Not after you threatened her.
Not after what she saw in that windmill — the metal on the altar,
the way it didn't behave like anything on Earth. Just like the stories
from '47. The rancher finds something strange, you call it a weather
balloon, then you make the whole thing disappear." The officials
exchange a brief, unreadable glance. "Careful, Agent. History has
shown that some wreckage is better left explained as simple balloons
and radar targets. The public doesn't need another Roswell.
And neither do we." On the central monitor, red text updates:
STATUS: MISSING — PRESUMED COMPROMISED. PROJECT NIGHTINGALE ACCESS:
TERMINATED. Then, as if from an external encrypted source, a final
line types itself across every screen, green text on black,
the color of a warning, of a beginning, of an ending:
"Some truths are recovered… and some carriers are recovered with them."
