The office is pristine, almost unnervingly so, the kind of clean
that suggests not cleanliness but erasure, the removal of everything
that might indicate a human being lives and works here. The panoramic
window offers a clear, unobstructed view of the city, but Cara
sees none of its beauty. She sits at her desk, her posture rigid,
her face pale and drawn, the face of a woman who has looked into
an abyss and been looked into in return. The framed photograph
of Anon is gone, replaced by a generic landscape print that means
nothing, that was chosen by someone who doesn't know her, who doesn't
know that the only thing she cared about in this office was the one
thing that has now been removed. Two stern-faced, impeccably dressed
figures stand before her desk — a man and a woman, high-ranking
officials whose faces she has seen in hushed corridors and on
classified documents whose existence she was never supposed to confirm.
Their presence alone speaks volumes, speaks of a power that operates
above the level of directors and agencies, above the level of nations,
in the rarefied atmosphere of people who make decisions that affect
the species. "Director Cara. We trust you've had time to reflect
on your recent activities. Your unauthorized excursions. Your
unsanctioned contacts." The man's voice is devoid of warmth,
devoid of anything human, the voice of a function executing.
The woman continues, her voice like ice forming on a still lake.
"Your position is contingent upon your unwavering loyalty to this
agency, and to the security of this nation. Your recent actions
have demonstrated a concerning deviation from that principle."
