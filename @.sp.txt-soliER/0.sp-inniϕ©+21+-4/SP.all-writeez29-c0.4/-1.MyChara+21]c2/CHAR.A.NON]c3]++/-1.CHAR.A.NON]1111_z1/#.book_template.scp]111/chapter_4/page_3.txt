The blue glow appeared between two rusted shipping

containers like a wound in the fabric

of the dark, pulsing softly, rhythmically, like

a heartbeat made visible. Cara killed her

flashlight and let her eyes adjust, pressing

her back against the cold corrugated steel

of the nearest container. The light was

not steady — it breathed, expanded and contracted,

casting long wavering shadows that made the

warehouse seem to shift and breathe around

her. She moved forward in a crouch,

every sense dialed to maximum, her weapon

held low and ready. The metallic scent

had intensified, sharp and coppery, mixed with

the smell of hot electronics and something

organic that she could not place, something

that reminded her of the air after

a lightning strike, charged and alive and

slightly wrong. She reached a makeshift barrier

constructed from salvaged metal sheets bolted together

in a rough semicircle, perhaps twelve feet

across and eight feet high. It had

been assembled with care, the seams sealed

with some kind of conductive compound that

glowed faintly where the blue light touched

it. Beyond the barrier, the hum was

almost deafnow, a physical presence that vibrated

in her chest cavity. She could hear

machinery — not the clatter of conventional engines

or the whine of electric motors, but

something smoother, something that sounded almost like

breathing. She found a gap between two

overlapping sheets, narrow but sufficient, and pressed

her eye to it. What she saw

made her breath catch in her throat.

The space beyond the barrier had been

transformed into something that defied every category

she possessed. Banks of server racks lined

the walls, their indicator lights blinking in

complex patterns that suggested processing on a

scale she had only seen in agency

supercomputing facilities. Cables thick as her arm

snaked across the floor, converging on a

central structure that dominated the space — a

cylindrical chamber, perhaps ten feet tall, constructed

from materials she could not identify, translucent

in places, opaque in others, filled with

a swirling luminescence that shifted between blue

and violet and something that had no

name in any spectrum she knew. And

standing before it, his back to her,

was a man she recognized from a

dozen classified briefings and a hundred redacted

personnel files. Victor LaGraine. Declared dead three

years ago. Killed in action during an

operation that officially never happened. Except here

he was, very much alive, very much

working, very much the center of whatever

this was.
