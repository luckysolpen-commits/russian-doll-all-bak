Victor stood with his back to her,

bathed in the ethereal blue glow that

poured from the cylindrical chamber like liquid

light. He was taller than she remembered

from the photographs, broader in the shoulders,

his dark hair streaked with silver that

caught the luminescence and threw it back

in fractured halos. He wore a faded

flight jacket over a dark shirt, sleeves

rolled to the elbows, and his hands

moved across a console of his own

design — part touchscreen, part analog switches, part

something she had no reference for at

all. His fingers danced over the controls

with the practiced ease of a concert

pianist, adjusting, calibrating, coaxing something from the

machine that responded to his touch like

a living thing. She watched him work

for a long moment, forgotten rainwater dripping

from her hair onto the concrete floor,

her weapon still raised though she no

longer felt any urge to use it.

This was not a man who needed

to be confronted with force. This was

a man who needed to be understood.

And understanding required proximity. She holstered her

weapon slowly, deliberately, letting the metal click

against the holster loud enough to be

heard. Victor's hands stopped moving. He did

not turn around immediately. Instead he reached

up and adjusted a dial on the

console, and the blue glow dimmed to

a softer pulse, less aggressive, more welcoming.

Then he turned, and his face was

not what she had expected. Not the

fanatic's glare, not the cornered animal's desperation.

Instead she saw something far more dangerous

— calm intelligence, weary amusement, and beneath

it all, a profound and aching sadness

that she recognized because she carried the

same weight herself. Director Cara, he said,

and his voice was warm, resonant, the

kind of voice that made you want

to lean in and listen. I was

beginning to wonder if you would come.

She said nothing. Her hand drifted toward

her sidearm again, not drawing, just resting

there, a reminder to them both of

the choice she had made to come

here unarmed in every way that mattered

except the one that could end her

life. You're supposed to be dead, she

said finally, and the words felt inadequate,

childish, but they were all she had.

Victor smiled, and it was a genuine

smile, tinged with something like regret. That

was the idea, he said. It made

the work much easier. He gestured toward

the chamber behind him, and the blue

light flared briefly, as if in response

to his invitation. But I suspect you

did not come here to discuss my

obituary. You came because you have questions.

And I have answers. More than you

will like. More than you can unhear.
