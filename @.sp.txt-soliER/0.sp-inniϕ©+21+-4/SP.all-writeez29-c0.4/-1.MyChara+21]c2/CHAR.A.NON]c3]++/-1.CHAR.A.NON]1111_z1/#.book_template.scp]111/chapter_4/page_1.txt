Rain hammered the windshield in sheets,

turning the highway into a river of

reflected neon and fractured streetlight. Cara gripped

the wheel with both hands, knuckles white,

muscles coiled tight beneath her soaked jacket.

The wipers fought a losing battle against

the deluge, slapping back and forth in

a frantic rhythm that matched her heartbeat.

Every instinct she had honed over fifteen

years of service screamed at her to

turn around, to drive back to the

warmth of her apartment, to pretend the

encrypted files had never crossed her desk.

But she could not unsee what she

had seen. The names. The dates. The

money trails that led nowhere and everywhere

all at once. Someone had been moving

billions through accounts that did not exist

on any ledger she could access through

normal channels. Someone with clearance far above

her own. Someone who wanted to stay

hidden in the static between frequencies.

The industrial district rose from the darkness

like a graveyard of forgotten ambitions. Warehouses

stood in silent rows, their corrugated metal

sides rusted and dented, windows boarded or

shattered. Most had been abandoned years ago,

left to rot while the city grew

around them like water flowing around stone.

But one still had power. She could

see it from here — a faint, irregular

pulse of blue light bleeding through cracks

in the loading bay doors. Almost imperceptible.

Almost nothing. But she had learned to

notice the things that others dismissed as

glitches, as tricks of the eye, as

nothing worth investigating. That was how she

had survived this long. By paying attention

to the silence between the noise.

She killed the engine two blocks away

and sat for a moment in the

darkness, listening to the rain drum against

the roof. Her sidearm rested against her

ribs, cold and familiar. She checked the

chamber out of habit, not necessity. Three

rounds. Enough if she needed them. Hopefully

she would not. The address she had

been given — if you could call a

burner message from an untraceable number an

address — matched this warehouse. Or one

like it. The message had been precise

in its imprecision. Come alone. Come armed.

Come prepared to question everything.

She stepped out into the rain and

it hit her like a physical blow,

cold and relentless, soaking through her clothes

in seconds. She pulled her collar up

and moved forward, boots splashing through puddles

that reflected nothing but darkness. The warehouse

loomed ahead, a skeletal beast crouching in

the storm, waiting.
