The door resisted at first, rusted hinges

protesting with a metallic shriek that echoed

across the empty lot like a wounded

animal. Cara froze, hand on her weapon,

listening for movement inside. Nothing. Only the

rain behind her and the low, almost

subsonic hum that vibrated through the soles

of her boots. She pushed harder and

the door gave way, swinging inward on

groaning hinges, revealing a cavern of absolute

darkness that smelled of ozone and old

machine oil and something else — something sharp

and electric that made the hair on

her arms stand up despite the cold.

She clicked on her tactical flashlight and

the beam cut through the gloom like

a blade, illuminating dust motes that danced

in the light like frantic stars caught

in a gravity well. The space was

vast, far larger than the exterior suggested,

a cathedral of abandoned industry repurposed for

something she could not yet name. Concrete

pillars rose into darkness overhead, supporting a

ceiling lost in shadow. The floor was

littered with debris — broken pallets, coils of

thick cable, stacks of corroded metal sheeting

leaning against walls like tombstones in a

graveyard. She moved slowly, methodically, her training

taking over despite the tremor in her

hands. Check the corners. Watch the sightlines.

Never let anything get behind you. She

had cleared buildings like this a dozen

times in a dozen countries, always with

a team, always with backup, always with

someone watching her six. Tonight she had

only the beam of her flashlight and

the weight of the gun in her

hand and the growing certainty that she

was walking into something far beyond her

clearance level, far beyond anything in her

briefing packets or training manuals or the

carefully curated reality she had accepted for

fifteen years. The hum grew louder as

she moved deeper into the warehouse, a

vibration she could feel in her teeth,

in the fillings of her molars, in

the marrow of her bones. It was

not mechanical. Not entirely. It had a

quality to it that reminded her of

standing too close to a high-voltage transformer,

that sense of invisible power pressing against

your skin, looking for a way in.

She paused behind a stack of shipping

containers and listened. There was movement ahead.

Faint. Deliberate. The sound of someone working.

Someone who knew they were alone. Someone

who might not stay alone for long.
