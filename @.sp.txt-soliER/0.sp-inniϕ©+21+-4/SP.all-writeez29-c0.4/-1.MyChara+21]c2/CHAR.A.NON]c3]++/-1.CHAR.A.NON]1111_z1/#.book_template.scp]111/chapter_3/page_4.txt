She sat in the darkness of her office, the rain painting
rivers down the window glass, and thought about the nature
of trust in a world where everyone had an agenda. Anon had warned
her to trust no one, but Anon wasn't offering answers — only warnings.
LaGraine was offering answers, but at a price that could cost her
everything. She pulled up his file again, the man who had been
erased from official records but not from the deeper layers
of the network where data never truly dies. Victor LaGraine.
Former professor of theoretical physics. Former consultant
to agencies whose names were redacted even in the redacted files.
And then, three years ago, the erasure — sudden, complete,
almost surgical. No scandal, no investigation, no public record.
Just a man who had been everywhere suddenly being nowhere.
But ghosts leave traces. They always do. And LaGraine's traces
led into the deepest parts of the Nightingale database,
into the files about non-terrestrial material, into the world
of physical evidence that the government had collected and hidden.
He had been inside the secret, had seen it, had touched it,
and then he had been removed. The question was why.
Why erase a man who knew too much instead of silencing him permanently?
Unless the erasure wasn't punishment. Unless it was protection.
Unless LaGraine had been removed from the board not because
he was a liability but because he was a threat — a man who knew
enough to be dangerous and had the intelligence to use that knowledge
in ways that the people in the shadows couldn't control.
She checked her watch. Hours until midnight. Hours until
she would stand in an abandoned warehouse and face a man
who existed in the space between enemy and ally.
She pulled up the Nightingale files one more time, searching
for anything she had missed, any detail that might give her an edge.
And then she found it — a reference, buried deep in a footnote
of a report about recovered material, to a facility that didn't
match any official designation. Coordinates, linked to a name
that made her blood go cold: Luminis. Light. The Luminis Fragment.
A piece of something that fell from the sky, studied and classified
and locked away in a place that didn't exist. LaGraine's project.
His obsession. His reason for everything he was doing.
She memorized the coordinates, closed the files, and waited
for midnight, knowing that whatever happened next, the world
she had known was already behind her.
