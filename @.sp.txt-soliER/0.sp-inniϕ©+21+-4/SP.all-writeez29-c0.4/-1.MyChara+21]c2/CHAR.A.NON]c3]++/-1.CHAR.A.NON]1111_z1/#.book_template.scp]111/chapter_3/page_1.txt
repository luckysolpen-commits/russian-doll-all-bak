The rain had settled into a steady rhythm against Cara's office windows,
drumming like fingers tapping out an impatient code. Night had fallen
again, and the office had become a war room — screens glowing
with algorithms, encrypted data streams scrolling like waterfalls,
news feeds detailing the escalating chaos in a world coming apart.
But Cara's focus was singular, fixed on the files spread across
her desk like cards in a hand she was trying to read. Project Nightingale.
Decades of suppressed information, hushed investigations,
unexplained phenomena documented by men who had dedicated their careers
to watching the sky. The deeper she went, the more unsettling
the picture became. This wasn't just a collection of curious incidents.
It was a pattern, a history, a narrative that stretched back
to the earliest days of human flight and beyond — sightings recorded
by ancient civilizations, accounts in texts older than scripture,
descriptions of craft that moved without wings or engines.
And the government had known. Known for decades. Known and hidden,
collected and classified, studied in facilities that didn't exist
by scientists who couldn't explain what they were seeing.
The scale of the suppression was staggering, a deliberate
and sustained effort to keep the truth from the public,
to control the narrative, to manage the most profound revelation
in human history with the tools of bureaucracy and secrecy.
Her phone sat on the desk, silent, but she could feel it
like a presence, a potential energy waiting to be released.
She had been thinking about LaGraine's message, the invitation
to midnight, the warehouse on 5th and Main. Hours before
the meeting, hours to prepare, to dig deeper, to find anything
that might give her an edge. And then the phone rang.
Not the secure line. Her private line, the one that very few
people had, the one that bypassed all standard protocols
and appeared on her display as an unknown number, encrypted,
untraceable. She stared at it, her hand hovering above the receiver,
every instinct telling her that this was the moment everything changed.
She answered. "Director Cara." The voice on the other end was smooth,
cultured, carrying a faint European accent that suggested a man
who had lived in many countries and belonged to none. Calm, confident,
utterly devoid of hesitation. "A pleasure to finally make your
acquaintance. My name is Victor LaGraine. And I believe I have
information that will be enlightening regarding your current predicament."
