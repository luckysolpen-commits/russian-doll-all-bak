She pushes herself up, wincing as a wave of dizziness washes
over her like a tide, pulling her toward an edge she can't see.
She's in her ergonomic chair, the same chair she sat in yesterday
or a lifetime ago, her suit rumpled, her hair disheveled,
her body carrying the stiffness of someone who has been unconscious
for longer than a nap and shorter than a coma. The tablet on her
desk is dark, the holographic displays gone, the screens black
and reflective, showing her a distorted version of her own face —
pale, drawn, eyes wide with a confusion that borders on panic.
The office is exactly as she left it, pristine and orderly,
a stark contrast to the chaos she remembers, the chaos she lived
through, the chaos that she knows was real even if this room
suggests otherwise. "Ariana? Victor?" she calls out, her voice
hoarse, a whisper scraped raw by screams she doesn't remember
screaming. No answer. Only the persistent beeping, patient
and indifferent. She looks around, a growing sense of unease
settling in her stomach like a stone dropped in a well. There's
no sign of the alien technology, no lingering scent of ozone,
no shattered crystals, no green light, no humming consoles.
It's as if the entire experience was a vivid, terrifying dream,
the kind that feels more real than reality and then dissolves
upon waking like sugar in rain. But her fingers brush against
a small, tender lump behind her ear, and the pain is immediate,
sharp, undeniable. It's real. The pain is real. The memory
of the blinding flash, the overwhelming energy, the feeling
of falling through layers of reality — it was all real. She knows
it was real. The knowledge sits in her chest like a weight.
