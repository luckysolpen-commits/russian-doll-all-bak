Cara's eyes flutter open, heavy and disoriented, like doors
swinging inward after years of being sealed shut. The first thing
she registers is a faint, persistent beeping — electronic,
rhythmic, the sound of a machine doing its job in a room
where no one is listening. Her head throbs, a dull ache
behind her eyes that pulses in time with the beeping, as if
her brain and the machine have synchronized without her consent.
She blinks, trying to clear the fog from her mind, a fog that
feels less like sleep and more like absence, like a gap in the
record of her life where hours or days have been excised with
surgical precision. She finds herself staring at the familiar
ceiling of her office at CIA Headquarters, the same acoustic tiles,
the same recessed lighting, the same mundane architecture that
she has looked at a thousand times before. But nothing about
this moment is mundane. Sunlight, bright and unforgiving,
streams through the panoramic window, illuminating dust motes
dancing in the air — tiny worlds spinning in tiny orbits,
each one a universe indifferent to the catastrophe of her memory.
The hum of office activity filters through the walls, the distant
murmur of voices, the rhythmic click of keyboards — it's all
so normal, so mundane, so aggressively ordinary after the
terrifying intensity of the windmill, the lab, the alien metal,
the light, the darkness. She tries to sit up and the world tilts.
