She reaches for her secure phone, lying innocently on the desk
like a paperweight, like an artifact from a civilization that
still believed in the stability of its own institutions.
She picks it up, her fingers trembling slightly — not from fear,
exactly, but from the neurological aftershock of something
that rewired her while she wasn't looking. No missed calls,
no messages from unknown numbers, no encrypted texts,
no green text from Anon. The last call logged is from Agent
Thompson, hours ago, a routine check-in that belongs to a world
that no longer exists. She tries to access her personal,
encrypted files, the ones containing the video LaGraine sent,
the data she and Victor had poured over through the night,
the evidence that was supposed to be the foundation of everything.
Access denied. The words are flat, final, the kind of message
that a system generates when it has been instructed to forget.
Every file, every piece of evidence, gone. Wiped clean.
Not deleted — erased, the difference being that deletion leaves
traces and erasure leaves nothing, a perfect void where data
used to live. Panic begins to set in, not the hot panic of
immediate danger but the cold panic of realization — that someone
has the power to reach into her most secure files and remove
them, that someone has the authority to bring her back to this
office, to this chair, to this normalcy, and place a version
of her here that may or may not be the version that left.
Was she drugged? Hypnotized? Transported? Or worse — was she
compromised, altered, rewritten, the way a file gets overwritten
with zeros until nothing of the original remains? She doesn't know.
She may never know. The not-knowing is its own kind of torture.
