"Give me the full picture," Cara said, planting herself
at the head of the central display. Thompson pulled up
a holographic projection that filled the space between them
with a three-dimensional web of light and data.
"It's coordinated, Director. Military-grade precision,
state-level resources, and a target list that reads like
a who's who of sensitive research." He gestured, and the hologram
shifted, highlighting specific nodes — defense contractors,
aerospace giants, biotech firms with government contracts,
all of them bleeding data in real time. Dr. Patel turned
from her console, her expression grave. "There's something else.
Every compromised system, every single one, had one thing in common.
They all had recent access to the same classified database.
A research initiative called Project Nightingale."
The name meant nothing to Cara. Thompson and Patel exchanged
a glance, the kind of look that passes between people who share
a secret they'd rather not. "It's sensitive, Director,"
Thompson said reluctantly. "A black-book operation.
Compartmentalized above top secret. It monitors and analyzes
anomalous aerial phenomena. UFO sightings, essentially."
The words landed in the room like stones dropped in still water.
UFOs. Cara felt the absurdity of it rise in her throat,
a laugh that wasn't really a laugh, because nothing about this
was funny. Anon's warning echoed in her mind, cold and precise:
The stakes are everything. "Who has access to Nightingale data?"
she asked. Patel pulled up a list. "Seventeen corporations,
nine government agencies, three international partners.
It's not a small pool, Director, but it's not public either.
Whoever planned this had inside knowledge. Detailed inside knowledge."
Inside knowledge. The phrase sent a cold thread through Cara's mind.
She thought of Anon's message, the way it had bypassed every firewall,
the way it had arrived on her tablet like a letter slipped under a door.
"I want everything on Nightingale," she said. "Every file, every report,
every piece of data that's been touched by these attacks.
I want to know who initiated the project, who's involved,
and what exactly they've been collecting." Thompson nodded,
already moving. "And Thompson," she added. "This stays in this room.
No one outside this lab knows about the Nightingale connection. Not yet."
He met her eyes, understood, and left. Patel was already deep
in the files, pulling up decades of classified observations —
grainy photographs of impossible craft, redacted transcripts
of hushed conversations between men who spoke about the sky
as if it belonged to someone else. Cara stood at the center
of the lab, surrounded by the hum of servers and the frantic
energy of her team, and felt the ground shift beneath her feet.
She had spent her entire career fighting wars in the digital realm,
clean wars, wars of code and logic, where the enemy was human
and the stakes were measurable. But this was something else entirely.
This was a war she didn't understand, against an enemy she couldn't see,
over secrets that belonged to a world she had never believed existed.
