"I want a full deep dive," Cara said, her voice gaining an edge
that made both Thompson and Patel straighten. "Every file, every report,
every piece of data related to non-terrestrial material. I want to know
what we have, where it came from, and who else knows about it."
The deep dive began, a digital excavation that pulled layers
of classified data into the light of the lab's monitors. Cara worked
alongside her team, reading, analyzing, connecting dots that spanned
decades and continents. The Nightingale database was vast, a repository
of everything the government had collected about unexplained aerial
phenomena since the earliest days of aviation. But it was the physical
samples that drew her attention — the files that dealt not with lights
in the sky but with things that could be held, measured, tested.
The reports were clinical, detached, written by scientists who had
approached the impossible with the tools of the possible and found
their tools wanting. "Sample exhibits properties inconsistent with
known metallurgy," one report read. "Atomic structure suggests
non-terrestrial origin. Strength-to-weight ratio exceeds any known
alloy by factor of ten. No visible seams, joints, or manufacturing marks."
She read on, each report more unsettling than the last. Energy signatures
that defied conventional physics. Propulsion systems that left no exhaust,
produced no heat, generated no sound. Materials that seemed to exist
in a state between solid and liquid, between matter and energy.
"Director," Patel said, and there was something in her voice that made
Cara turn quickly. On Patel's screen, a new file had appeared,
one that hadn't been there moments before. It hadn't been requested,
hadn't been searched for. It had simply arrived, like a letter
slipped under a door. "I didn't pull that up," Patel said, her eyes wide.
"It just... appeared." The file was encrypted, but not with standard
government protocols. The encryption was older, stranger, built on
mathematical principles that Cara recognized from a different context.
Someone else was sending her a message. And this time, it wasn't Anon.
Back in her office, with the door closed and the blinds drawn,
Cara examined the file more closely. The encryption was brilliant —
a work of art, in its way, the kind of code that only a handful
of people on earth could write. And then she saw it, buried deep
in the file's metadata, a signature so subtle that most analysts
would never notice it: two letters, V and L. Victor LaGraine.
