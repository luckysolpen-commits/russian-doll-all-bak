The heavy wooden door groaned shut behind

her, and the sound echoed through the

interior like the closing of a vault,

sealing her inside a space that was

far larger than the exterior dimensions should

have allowed. Moonlight filtered through cracks in

the ancient timbers, casting thin silver lines

across a central chamber that rose into

darkness overhead, the windmill's great shaft ascending

like the trunk of a mechanical tree,

its surface scarred with markings that caught

the light and held it, glyphs or

symbols or something that might have been

wear patterns from centuries of operation but

looked too deliberate, too precise, too much

like language. She raised her weapon and

swept the beam of her tactical flashlight

across the space, methodical, trained, her breathing

steady despite the cold knot of unease

that had settled in her stomach the

moment she crossed the threshold. The interior

was circular, perhaps thirty feet across, the

curved walls formed by the windmill's original

structure reinforced with additions she could not

date — sections of newer timber, patches of

brick, areas where the original wood had

been replaced with something darker, denser, that

absorbed the light rather than reflecting it.

The floor was packed earth, worn smooth

by generations of feet, and in the

center, directly beneath the ascending shaft, the

ground had been excavated into a shallow

depression, perhaps three feet deep, its edges

lined with the same dark material she

had seen in the walls. She called

out, her voice echoing in the confined

space, bouncing off the curved walls and

returning to her distorted and multiplied, as

if the windmill itself was speaking back

to her in a language of reflections.

Hello. I'm here. Show yourself. Silence answered. Not

the silence of emptiness, but the silence

of presence held in check, of something

waiting just beyond the edge of perception,

observing, assessing, deciding. She moved to the

edge of the depression and looked down.

The dark material lining its edges was

warm to the touch, not with the

heat of machinery but with something closer

to body temperature, and it pulsed, faintly,

rhythmically, in time with the beating of

her own heart or perhaps in time

with something much larger and much older

that she could feel but could not

name. Her flashlight beam caught something at

the bottom of the depression — a object,

small, geometric, reflecting the light in colors

that shifted as she watched, and she

felt the same electric scent intensify, sharp

and coppery and alive, and she knew

with a certainty that bypassed thought entirely

that she was not alone in this

space and had not been alone since

the moment she opened the door.
