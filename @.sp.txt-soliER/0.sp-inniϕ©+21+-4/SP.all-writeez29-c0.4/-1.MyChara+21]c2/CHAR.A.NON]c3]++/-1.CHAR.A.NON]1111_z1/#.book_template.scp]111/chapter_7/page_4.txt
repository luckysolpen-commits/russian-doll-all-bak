She emerged from the deepest shadows the

way water emerges from a spring — without

sound, without warning, simply present where a moment

before there had been nothing. Cara's weapon

swung toward her instinctively, the beam of

her flashlight catching a face that made

her breath catch, not from fear but

from the sheer impossibility of what she

was seeing. The woman was striking in

a way that transcended conventional beauty, her

features arranged with a precision that suggested

design rather than chance, her skin carrying

a faint luminescence that might have been

moonlight or might have been something emanating

from within. Her eyes were the color

of the blue glow in Victor's warehouse,

but deeper, older, holding a knowledge that

made Cara feel like a child standing

at the edge of an ocean she

had only seen in photographs. She moved

with an unnerving grace, each step placed

with an economy of motion that spoke

of complete awareness of her body in

space, of an efficiency that no human

training could achieve. I have been expecting

you, Director Cara, she said, and her

voice was like her movement — precise, economical,

carrying a resonance that seemed to come

from somewhere deeper than her vocal cords.

Or should I say — the one who

seeks the truth. She did not ask

Cara to lower her weapon. She did

not seem concerned by it at all.

Instead she stepped into the moonlight fully,

and Cara saw that she wore

clothes that defied categorization — not modern, not

historical, but something that existed outside the

timeline she understood, constructed from materials that

caught the light and scattered it in

the same shifting colors she had seen

in the object at the bottom of

the depression. My name is Ariana, the

woman said. And I have been waiting

for you for a very long time.

Not since tonight. Not since you received

the message. Since before you were born.

Before Victor LaGraine was recruited. Before General

Armstrong made his first impossible choice. Before

the first recovery operation in the New

Mexico desert pulled fragments of impossible technology

from the scorched earth and began the

long, slow process of trying to understand

what they had found. Cara's weapon hand

trembled, just slightly, the tremor of someone

whose entire framework of understanding is being

dismantled not by documents and data but

by the presence of a person who

should not exist, who could not exist,

and yet stood before her in moonlight,

breathing, speaking, watching her with eyes that

held the patience of centuries. Who are

you? Cara asked, and the question felt

inadequate, childish, the only words she had

for something that existed beyond words. Ariana

smiled, and it was neither warm nor

cold but simply true, the expression of

something that had no need to perform

or deceive. I am what they found,

she said. And what they could not

explain. And what they have been trying

to understand ever since. She extended her

hand, palm up, and in the center

of it, a faint blue light began

to glow.
