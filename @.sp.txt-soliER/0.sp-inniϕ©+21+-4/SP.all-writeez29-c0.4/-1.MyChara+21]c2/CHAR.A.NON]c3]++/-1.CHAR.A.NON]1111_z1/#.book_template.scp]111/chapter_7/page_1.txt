Three nights later, Cara sat in her

car, parked a quarter-mile from the windmill,

engine off, lights off, watching the ancient

structure silhouette itself against a sky choked

with clouds that refused to rain. The

secure phone buzzed against her thigh, vibrating

against the data drive she had not

removed from her pocket since that night

in the warehouse, as if the two

objects were communicating, resonating at some frequency

she could not detect but could feel

in the tension that had taken up

permanent residence in her shoulders. She pulled

the phone from her jacket and looked

at the screen. Unknown number. Encrypted beyond

anything she had seen — not agency encryption,

not military, not anything in her database

of known protocols. The call connected before

she could decide whether to answer it,

and a voice filled the car, distorted

beyond recognition, neither male nor female, processed

through so many layers of modulation that

it sounded less like a person speaking

and more like a machine attempting to

simulate human speech from a description written

in a language it did not understand.

You have seen the first layer, the

voice said, and it was not a

question. You have looked into the archive

and understood the scope of what has

been hidden. But what you have seen

is but a fragment. A single thread

in a tapestry far older than you

can imagine. The voice paused, and in

that pause Cara heard something that made

her blood go cold — not a threat, not

a promise, but a kind of vast

and ancient patience, the patience of something

that had waited centuries and could wait

centuries more. The windmill, the voice continued.

Come alone. The next piece awaits. The

line went dead, leaving only the sound

of her own breathing and the distant

wind moving through the fields outside the

car. She sat for a long moment,

the phone dark in her hand, her

mind racing through possibilities, threat assessments, contingency plans.

Anon's warnings echoed in her memory, words

typed in the dead of night by

a digital ghost who had become the

only man she had ever truly loved

without ever fully revealing himself. Trust no

one. The architecture goes deeper than you

know. They are watching. Always watching. She

had trusted Anon because she had no

choice, because the files he had sent

her had been too precise, too accurate,

too impossible to dismiss as coincidence or

manipulation. But this — this was different. This

was a voice that knew about the

archive, about Victor, about decisions she had

made in a warehouse in the rain

that no one should have known about.

She started the car.
