Victor stepped back from the console and

faced her fully, his expression shifting from

the fervent evangelist to something more human,

more vulnerable, the mask of certainty slipping

to reveal the exhausted man beneath who

had been carrying this knowledge alone for

far too long. He reached into his

flight jacket and withdrew a small data

drive, no larger than a thumbnail, matte

black, unmarked. Everything, he said, holding it

out to her. Every document Armstrong collected.

Every recording he made in the final

months, when he knew he was dying

and knew that the people he had

served with, the people who had trusted

him with the most closely guarded secrets

in human history, were planning to seal

those secrets away forever. Personal journals. Technical

analyses. Financial records tracing every dollar, every

diversion, every shell corporation and front organization

and academic institute that served as a

node in the network. The complete history

of the cover-up, from the first recovery

operation in the New Mexico desert to

the present day. He paused, and his

hand trembled, just slightly, the drive catching

the blue light and throwing it back

in fractured reflections across his palm. I

have spent three years building this archive.

Three years living as a ghost, as

a dead man, as someone who exists

outside the system I once served. Three

years waiting for someone I could trust

with it. Someone with the clearance to

understand what they are looking at and

the moral compass to know what to

do with it. He extended his hand

further, the drive resting in his open

palm like an offering, like a burden,

like a grenade with the pin already

pulled. I want you to have it,

Cara. All of it. Not just copies.

Not just summaries. Everything. The raw data,

the unredacted files, the recordings in Armstrong's

own voice, trembling with the weight of

what he had done and what he

knew was coming. Take it. Use it.

Do with it what I cannot do

alone. She looked at the drive, at

the small piece of plastic and silicon

that contained enough information to shatter governments,

to destroy careers, to upend the carefully

constructed narrative that held millions of people's

understanding of reality together, and she felt

the weight of it pressing down on

her, heavier than any weapon she had

ever carried. If I take this, she

said slowly, there is no going back.

You know that. Once I see what

is on this drive, once I know

what you know, I become part of

this. Part of whatever comes next. Victor

nodded. I know. That is the choice.

That has always been the choice. Not

whether the truth comes out — that is

inevitable now. The mechanisms are failing. The

old guard is dying. The question is

whether it comes out in a way

that helps people understand, or in a

way that destroys everything before anyone is

ready.
