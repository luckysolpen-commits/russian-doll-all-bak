Dawn arrived without announcement, a gradual graying

of the darkness filtering through the grimy warehouse

windows high above, turning the blue glow

of the cylindrical chamber from commanding to merely

persistent. Cara had not noticed the passage

of time. Hours had dissolved into a

blur of data and revelation, of names

and dates and financial trails that wound

through the global economy like veins through

a body, feeding resources to organs that

did not exist on any anatomical chart

she had ever seen. She sat on

an overturned crate beside the console, the

data drive clutched in her hand, her

eyes raw from staring at screens that

kept revealing deeper and deeper layers of

deception. Victor had been relentless, walking her

through the architecture of the cover-up with

the exhaustive precision of a professor who

had spent decades preparing a single lecture.

The financial trails led everywhere — through defense

contractors in Virginia, through banking institutions in

Zurich and Singapore, through sovereign wealth funds

in nations that officially had no connection

to the programs being funded. Billions diverted

over decades, siphoned from budgets approved by

congressional committees that had no idea where

the money actually went, funneled into research

facilities buried beneath mountains, beneath deserts, beneath

the ocean floor. And the participants were

not just American. That was the final

piece, the one that had taken Cara

longest to accept, the one that made

the scope of what she was looking

at expand beyond anything she had imagined.

Russia and China were not adversaries in

this game. They were participants. Partners. The

Cold War, the arms race, the space

race — all of it a smokescreen, a theatrical

performance staged for the benefit of populations

that were never meant to understand the

real game being played above their heads.

The superpowers had been cooperating on the

recovery programs since the beginning, maintaining the

fiction of competition while sharing data, sharing

technology, sharing the burden of knowledge that

humanity was not alone and never had

been. She stood, her joints stiff, her

mind numb with the sheer volume of

what she had absorbed. The warehouse around

her looked different in the morning light

— less like a cathedral of the impossible

and more like what it was, a

rusting industrial space filled with equipment that

would look like madness to anyone who

did not understand what it was doing.

But she understood now. Or she understood

enough. She slipped the data drive into

her pocket, feeling its small weight against

her thigh, a weight that would grow

with every step she took from this

moment forward. I will expose them, she

said, and her voice was quiet but

absolute, the voice of someone who had

made a decision that could not be

undone. All of them. Every name on

every screen. Every person who participated in

the lie. The truth comes out. Not

managed. Not guided. All of it. Victor

watched her, and his expression was unreadable

— relief, fear, pride, grief, all of it

moving across his face like clouds across

a moon. Then he nodded, once, and

turned back to his console, and the

blue light pulsed on.
