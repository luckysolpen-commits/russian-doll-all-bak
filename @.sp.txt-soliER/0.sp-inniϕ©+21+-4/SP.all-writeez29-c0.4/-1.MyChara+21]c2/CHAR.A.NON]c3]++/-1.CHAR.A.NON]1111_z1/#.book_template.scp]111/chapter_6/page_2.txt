Victor moved to a different section of

the console, and the holographic globe dissolved,

replaced by a series of schematics that

made Cara's trained eye widen despite herself.

These were not conventional engineering diagrams. The

geometries were wrong — angles that should not

hold, structures that defied standard physics, materials

listed with properties that existed in no

database she had ever accessed. The annotations

were in a hybrid of English and

symbols she did not recognize, precise and

meticulous, as if someone had spent decades

reverse-engineering technology that had no business existing

on this planet. This was recovered from

the New Mexico site, Victor said, his

voice dropping to something approaching reverence. Not all

of it. Not even most. But enough.

Enough to understand that we are not

alone in the universe and never have

been. Enough to understand that the technology

they left behind — or that we recovered

from the crash, depending on which version

of the story you prefer — contains principles

that could revolutionize everything. Energy production. Materials

science. Medicine. He tapped a section of

the schematic and it expanded, revealing layers

of complexity that made her head spin.

But the most important discovery is not

technological. It is biological. Genetic. The beings

who built this — their biology is different from

ours, yes, but not incompatible. There are

sequences in their genetic material that correspond

to dormant regions of the human genome.

Junk DNA, they used to call it.

Regions with no apparent function. But they

do have a function. They are waiting.

Dormant potential, activated by exposure to specific

energy signatures, specific frequencies, specific — he gestured

at the cylindrical chamber behind him, pulsing

its steady blue rhythm — stimuli. Expanding consciousness.

Unlocking capabilities that humanity has carried within

it since the beginning but never had

the key to access. Cara stared at

the schematics, at the impossible geometries and

the genetic sequences mapped alongside them, and

she felt the last vestiges of her

old certainty dissolve like sugar in rain.

She had been trained to deal with

human threats — terrorists, rogue states, insider threats,

cyber criminals. She had protocols for all

of those, playbooks and contingency plans and

chains of command. She had nothing for

this. Nothing for the suggestion that humanity

was sitting on the threshold of a

transformation so profound it made the industrial

revolution look like a minor software update.

You want to activate these sequences, she

said. In people. Without their knowledge. Without

their consent. Victor met her gaze without

flinching. I want to make the key

available, he said. Let people choose. That

is the difference between what Armstrong built

and what I am building. He wanted

to manage the transition. I want to

enable it. To give people the choice

he thought they were too immature to

make.
