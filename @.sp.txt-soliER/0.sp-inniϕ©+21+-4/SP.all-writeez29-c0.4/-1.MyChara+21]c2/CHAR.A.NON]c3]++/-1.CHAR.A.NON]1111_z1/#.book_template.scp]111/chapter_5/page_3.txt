Cara felt the floor shift beneath her,

not physically but conceptually, the ground of

everything she thought she understood tilting at an

angle that made her stomach clench. She

had spent her career operating within a

framework of clear objectives and measurable outcomes,

of threats that could be identified and

neutralized, of enemies who wore faces she

could recognize and motives she could predict.

What Victor was describing existed outside that

framework entirely, in a space where the

distinction between friend and enemy, between protection

and manipulation, between truth and narrative, dissolved into

something she had no training to navigate.

Show me, she said, and her voice

was steadier than she felt. Show me

the evidence. Not the story. The proof.

Victor nodded, as if he had expected

this response, as if he had prepared

for it specifically. He turned to the

console and executed a series of commands

that brought up document after document, each

one bearing classification markings that made Cara's

breath catch. Internal memoranda between officials whose

names she recognized from congressional hearings and

press briefings. Financial transactions routing billions through

shell corporations and offshore accounts, each one

traceable if you knew where to look,

and clearly Victor knew. Encrypted communications intercepted

and decoded, their contents revealing a network

of coordination so vast, so deeply embedded

in the institutions she had sworn to

protect, that she felt a moment of

vertigo so severe she had to steady

herself against the console. Names flashed across

the screens — senators, generals, directors of agencies

she had only heard referenced in the

most secure rooms of the most secure

buildings. Dates stretching back decades, connecting events

she had studied at the academy with

a hidden architecture of planning and management

that made a mockery of everything she

had been taught about how the world

worked. Decades of lies, Victor said quietly.

Not malicious lies. Not the lies of

villains twirling their mustaches in some cartoon

version of evil. The lies of people

who believed, genuinely believed, that they were

protecting something fragile and precious. The stability

of civilization itself. He pulled up another

document, this one bearing Armstrong's signature, dated

three weeks before his official death from

a heart attack that Cara now suspected

had been anything but natural. Read it,

Victor said. The final directive. The moment

he realized that management was no longer

enough. That the truth would have to

be released, not in a trickle, but

in a flood. Cara leaned forward and

read, and with each line the world

she knew crumbled a little further, until

she was standing on the edge of

something vast and terrifying and utterly inevitable.
