Lucky pushed himself away from the wreck, his muscles screaming in protest. He did a quick self-assessment. A few cuts and bruises, a pounding headache, but nothing seemed to be broken. He was lucky. The irony of his name was not lost on him. He looked back at the UNOMAN suit one last time. It was a beautiful machine, a marvel of engineering. Now, it was just a tombstone, a monument to his failure. He felt a pang of something that wasn't quite grief, but close to it. It was the feeling of losing a part of himself. He had spent years in that suit, exploring the galaxy, pushing the boundaries of human knowledge. Now, he was just a man, stripped of his technology, his purpose. He was naked in the face of this alien world. He turned his back on the wreckage and started walking. He didn't have a destination, just a general direction: away. Away from the crash, away from the memories. The jungle floor was a spongy, yielding moss that seemed to suck at his boots with every step. The air was thick with the scent of a thousand different flowers, a heady, intoxicating perfume that made his head spin. He saw plants that looked like giant, pulsating brains, and others that resembled crystalline sculptures, their delicate structures catching the light of the twin suns. He heard the chittering of unseen creatures, the rustle of leaves, the snap of a twig. Every sound was a potential threat, every shadow a potential enemy. He was a scientist, not a soldier. He was trained to observe, to analyze, to understand. But here, in this primal wilderness, his knowledge was useless. Here, only strength and cunning mattered. And he wasn't sure he had enough of either. He felt a surge of fear, cold and sharp, but he pushed it down. Fear was a luxury he couldn't afford. He had to keep moving. He had to survive. He had to find a way home. He repeated the words like a mantra, a prayer to a god he wasn't sure he believed in anymore. Survive. Find a way home. Survive. Find a way home. The words became a rhythm, a beat that matched the pounding of his heart. It was the only thing that kept him going.
