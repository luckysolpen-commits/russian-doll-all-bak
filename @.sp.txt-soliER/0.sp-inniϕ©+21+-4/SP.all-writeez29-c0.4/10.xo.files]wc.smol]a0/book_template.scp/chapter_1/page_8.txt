He stopped at the edge of the clearing, his hand tightening on his makeshift club. He was outnumbered, and he was exhausted. If they were hostile, he wouldn’t stand a chance. He waited, his heart in his throat. The villagers just stared at him, their dark eyes unblinking. The silence stretched on, thick and heavy. It was broken by the cry of a baby, a thin, reedy sound that was surprisingly human. One of the women, her skin a vibrant tapestry of blues and greens, picked up the child, rocking it gently in her arms. The sight of it, so mundane, so universal, seemed to break the tension. An elderly male, his skin a network of fine lines and faded colors, stepped forward. He was leaning on a staff carved from a single piece of gnarled, black wood. He said something in a language that sounded like a series of clicks and melodic hums. It was a question. Lucky didn’t understand the words, but he understood the tone. It was not aggressive. It was… curious. He took a deep breath and lowered his club. He held up his empty hands, a gesture of peace that he hoped was as universal as a mother’s love. “I… I mean you no harm,” he said, his voice hoarse. The villagers just stared at him, their expressions unchanged. They didn’t understand him. Of course they didn’t. He was a fool. A lost, pathetic fool. He was about to turn and run, to disappear back into the jungle, when a young girl stepped forward. She couldn’t have been more than twelve years old, her skin a pale, almost translucent white, adorned with a single, delicate spiral of silver ink. She looked at the old male, then at Lucky. And then she spoke. In perfect, unaccented English. “He says… who are you? And what do you want?”
