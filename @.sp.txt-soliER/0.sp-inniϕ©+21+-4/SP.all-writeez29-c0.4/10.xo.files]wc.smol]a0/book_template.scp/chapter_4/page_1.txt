The sky was bleeding light. The crack in reality widened, a gaping wound in the heavens, and through it, the impossible city of the Extrmophiles descended. It was not a gentle arrival. It was a violent, brutal intrusion, a violation of the natural order. The village was thrown into chaos. The Afink, who had been on their knees in a state of ecstatic terror, were now scrambling for cover, their faces masks of pure, unadulterated fear. The dance was over. The storm had arrived. And it was a storm that threatened to shatter their world. Amidst the chaos, Lucky felt a strange sense of calm. The overwhelming terror he had felt just moments before had been replaced by a cold, clinical detachment. His scientific mind, his last bastion of sanity in this world of impossible magic, had taken over. He was no longer a participant in this madness. He was an observer. A witness to a cataclysmic event that would be studied for centuries, if anyone survived to study it. He saw Lyra, her small frame buffeted by the winds that now whipped through the clearing. She was not running. She was standing her ground, her face turned towards the sky, her eyes wide with a mixture of awe and horror. And then, she turned to him, her eyes locking with his. And in their depths, he saw a silent, desperate plea. A plea for… what? Help? Understanding? Forgiveness? He didn’t know. But he knew that he could not abandon her. He ran to her side, grabbing her hand, his touch a small, human anchor in a sea of cosmic chaos. “We have to get out of here!” he yelled over the roar of the descending city. But Lyra shook her head, her gaze fixed on her family’s hut. The intricate patterns on its mud-brick walls were glowing with an intense, almost blinding light. “No,” she said, her voice barely a whisper. “We have to go… there.”
