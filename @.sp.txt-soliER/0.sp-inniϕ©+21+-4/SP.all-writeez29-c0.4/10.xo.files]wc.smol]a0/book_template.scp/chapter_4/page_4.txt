The ascent was dizzying, a kaleidoscope of colors and sensations. Encased within the pulsating column of ink, Lucky felt a profound sense of weightlessness, as if gravity itself had ceased to exist. He clung to Lyra, her small body a fragile anchor in the swirling vortex. Below them, the Afink village, once a vibrant tapestry of life, shrank into a tiny speck, then vanished altogether, swallowed by the encroaching darkness. The jungle, a vast, undulating carpet of green, stretched out to the horizon, an endless expanse of alien flora. He could see the twin suns of Planet X, now mere pinpricks of light in the inky blackness of space. The planet itself, a swirling marble of blues and greens, receded beneath them, becoming a distant memory. He was leaving it all behind. The familiar, the known, the safe. He was being propelled into the unknown, into the heart of a mystery that was both terrifying and alluring. He felt a strange mix of fear and exhilaration, a primal thrill that he hadn’t felt since his days as an explorer, pushing the boundaries of human knowledge. But this was different. This was not a journey of his own choosing. This was a forced migration, a kidnapping by an unseen force. He was a prisoner, a pawn in a game he didn’t understand. He looked at Lyra, her face pale in the eerie violet light of the ink column. Her eyes were wide, but there was no fear in them. Only a quiet, almost serene acceptance. She was not afraid. She was ready. Ready for whatever awaited them at the end of this impossible journey. He wondered if she knew something he didn’t. Something about the Extrmophiles, about their intentions, about their destiny. He wanted to ask, but the words caught in his throat. The air was thin, and the pressure was building, a silent, invisible force that squeezed his lungs, making it difficult to breathe. He closed his eyes, and surrendered to the ascent, to the unknown, to the inevitable.
