“Good morning,” he said, his voice still rough with sleep. “I hope I didn’t… intrude.” Lyra looked up from her weaving, her dark eyes sparkling in the firelight. “You are a guest, Lucky,” she said, her voice soft and melodic. “You are welcome here.” He sat down across from her, the warmth of the fire chasing away the last of the morning chill. He watched her fingers as they moved with a practiced grace, weaving the colorful threads into an intricate pattern. It was a beautiful piece of work, a tapestry of vibrant hues that seemed to tell a story. “What are you making?” he asked. “A story,” she said, her eyes still on her work. “A story of our people. A story of the jungle. A story of the gods.” He was fascinated. In his world, stories were told in books, in movies, in virtual reality simulations. But here, they were woven into the very fabric of their lives. “Tell me,” he said, his voice barely a whisper. “Tell me a story.” And so she did. She told him about the creation of their world, about the twin suns that were the eyes of their creator god, about the jungle that was the body of their earth mother. She told him about the first Afink, who had learned the secrets of the inks from the spirits of the trees. She told him about the great wars with the jungle beasts, about the heroes who had fought and died to protect their people. Her voice was mesmerizing, her words painting a vivid picture in his mind. He was no longer in a small, primitive village on a forgotten planet. He was in a world of myth and magic, a world of gods and monsters, a world of heroes and villains. He was enchanted. He was enthralled. He was… home. Or at least, it felt like it. For the first time in a long time, he felt a sense of belonging, a sense of peace. He was a part of their story now. And he couldn’t wait to see how it would end.
