“Calling to us?” Lucky repeated, the words tasting like ash in his mouth. “What are you talking about? Lyra, this is… this is impossible.” He stood up, pacing back and forth along the stream bank, his mind racing. He was a scientist. He dealt in facts, in evidence, in repeatable experiments. This… this was none of those things. This was superstition, mass hysteria, a collective delusion brought on by fear and isolation. “There has to be a rational explanation,” he insisted, more to convince himself than her. “A logical reason for all of this.” Lyra watched him, her expression a mixture of patience and sadness. “You still think in terms of your world, Lucky,” she said softly. “You try to fit our reality into your neat little boxes of logic and reason. But this world… it does not fit in a box. It is wild, and chaotic, and… alive. It has a spirit. A will. And that will is now… stirring.” “I don’t understand,” he said, his voice filled with frustration. “I don’t hear any whispers. I don’t feel any… calling.” He pressed his hands to his ears, as if to block out the ever-present hum. “I just hear… noise.” “That is because you are listening with your ears,” Lyra said, her voice gentle. “You are trying to hear a sound. But this is not a sound. It is… a feeling. A vibration. A… a memory.” She stood up and walked to the nearest hut, its mud-brick walls covered in the familiar, swirling patterns of Afink art. She placed her hand on the wall, her fingers tracing the lines of a vibrant, blue spiral. “The whispers are not in the air, Lucky,” she said, her voice barely a whisper. “They are in the earth. In the stones. In the very fabric of our world. They are the memories of the Extrmophiles, the echoes of their ancient power. And they are waking up.”
