EXT. LUCKY'S RANCH - DAY

SOUND of crickets, a gentle breeze, distant birdsong, the rhythmic THUMP of polo mallets, the whir of servos

A few years out. The sprawling ranch is a kingdom of contradictions — part rustic paradise, part cutting-edge laboratory. Lucky walked away from his empire at thirty-seven, barely two years ago, and the ranch is his deliberate escape.

A POLO FIELD cuts through the center of the property. Lucky's personal field, regulation size, immaculately maintained. Four HUMANOID ROBOTS — sleek, bipedal, with expressive faces and the logo "SHEPHERD DYNAMICS" stamped on their chest plates — are raking the field with precise, synchronized movements. They're from one of Lucky's startups: Shepherd Dynamics, the company that made humanoid robots affordable for agriculture, construction, and domestic service. These particular models are wearing tiny polo helmets, a joke someone programmed into them.

LUCKY SHEPHERD (39), young for the empire he built, but already carrying the weight of it in his eyes. His mohawk (today a subdued forest green, a concession to rural life) rises above a face that's still sharp, still handsome — barely a hint of grey at the temples. He wears designer jeans and a custom-tailored flannel shirt that costs more than most people's cars. Even in retirement, he dresses like a rich rapper who's slumming it. Gold chains peek out from his collar. Diamond rings catch the sun. The name "Lucky Shepherd" wasn't just branding — it was destiny. Born to Irish parents who named him after the luck of the Irish, he leaned into it, building an empire on the fusion of two worlds: the Asian dragon and the leprechaun.

He's not in the garden. He's on the polo field, mounted on a magnificent BAY STALLION, practicing his swing. He's good — the kind of good that comes from years of expensive lessons and natural athletic ability.

<center>LUCKY</center>
> (to the horse, patting its neck)
> That's it, Thunder. You're the only one here who doesn't talk back.

A HUMANOID ROBOT approaches, carrying a tray of iced tea. It moves with uncanny grace — almost human, but not quite. Its face is a smooth mask with projected expressions.

<center>HUMANOID ROBOT</center>
> Hydration break, Mr. Shepherd. Your vitals indicate elevated heart rate and mild dehydration.

<center>LUCKY</center>
> (dismounting, taking the glass)
> Thank you, Unit Seven. How's the crop sector looking?

<center>HUMANOID ROBOT</center>
> Genetically modified wheat yield is up twelve percent. The purple corn is producing anthocyanin levels within target parameters. The marijuana greenhouse reports optimal THC content in the latest harvest. Ms. Astra requests your presence there after your polo practice.

<center>LUCKY</center>
> (smiling)
> Of course she does. She's been breeding a new strain. Probably wants to show me the buds.

He drinks the tea, then remounts. Takes another swing. The ball flies perfectly down the field.

A MONTAGE of the ranch begins:

* THE GENETIC CROP FIELDS — Rows of impossible plants. Purple corn that shimmers in the sun. Tomatoes the size of softballs. Wheat stalks that glow faintly blue at the tips — a bioluminescent gene modification Lucky's ag-tech division developed. The crops are beautiful, alien, and productive beyond anything conventional agriculture could achieve.

* THE MARIJUANA GREENHOUSE — A massive, climate-controlled structure filled with rows of cannabis plants in various stages of growth. Some are towering sativas, others dense indicas. Astra's latest project — a strain engineered for maximum medicinal value with minimal psychoactive effect. The air inside is thick with the sweet, skunky aroma. Astra walks the rows with a tablet, taking measurements, talking to the plants like they're her children.

* THE STABLES — Twelve Arabian horses, each one a masterpiece of breeding and training. Lucky's pride and joy. The humanoid robots handle feeding and grooming, but Lucky insists on riding them himself. It's the one thing he won't automate.

* THE POLO FIELD — Lucky's personal obsession. He plays three times a week, even if his only opponent is a team of Shepherd Dynamics robots programmed to simulate intermediate-level polo. He's been trying to get Astra to play, but she refuses to "participate in aristocratic horse sports designed by colonialists."

* THE RANCH HOUSE — A sprawling, modern structure built into the hillside. Solar panels on the roof. A helipad out back. A wine cellar that rivals a small country's. Inside, the decor is a mix of rustic ranch and high-tech luxury — leather furniture next to holographic displays, cowboy boots next to robot charging stations. And on the wall, the original logo of his first company: a golden dragon coiled around a four-leaf clover. Shepherd Software started in a dorm room. ShepherdCoin — the cryptocurrency that made him a billionaire at thirty-two — was built on a blockchain architecture he designed himself. The casino, the Asian Dragon & Leprechaun, was his victory lap: a physical monument to the two myths that made his name real. Luck of the Irish. Power of the dragon.

The montage ends back on the polo field. Lucky has dismounted and is walking toward the crop fields, his boots kicking up red dust. He passes the humanoid robots, who pause their work to acknowledge him with synchronized nods.

He reaches the genetic crop fields. Astra is there, kneeling beside a row of bioluminescent tomatoes, her hands deep in the soil.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> (without looking up)
> You're late. The purple corn is ready for harvest, the blue wheat is expressing the luciferase gene beautifully, and your marijuana babies are producing cannabinoids at levels that would make a pharmaceutical company weep.

<center>LUCKY</center>
> (kneeling beside her)
> You know I'm more interested in the tomatoes. They're more reliable than quarterly reports.

Astra looks up at him, dirt on her cheeks, her hair escaping its messy bun. She's not just a friend; she's a partner in this quiet life. A genius who chose dirt over boardrooms.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> You're impossible.

<center>LUCKY</center>
> I'm retired. There's a difference.

He takes her hand. They stand together, surveying the fields — the purple corn, the blue wheat, the glowing tomatoes, the marijuana greenhouse gleaming in the distance. It's a small empire, but it's HIS empire. One he built with science and soil instead of code and cryptocurrency.

A humanoid robot approaches with two mugs of coffee. Lucky and Astra take them without looking.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> You know, you haven't looked at that thing in weeks.

She gestures subtly towards a small, sleek device on a nearby table – a high-tech monitor, a relic of his past, still capable of streaming live feeds from the Asian Dragon & Leprechaun casino. The dragon-and-clover logo glows faintly on its standby screen.

<center>LUCKY</center>
> Old habits die hard. But honestly, I barely think about it anymore. This… this is real.

He gestures to the fields, the horses, the robots, the greenhouse, the vast expanse of the ranch.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> Good. Because you deserve this, Lucky. All of it. Peace.

She sips her coffee, her gaze warm. Lucky looks at the monitor, then back at Astra, a decision forming in his eyes. He walks over to the table, picks up the device, and with a decisive click, switches it off. The screen goes black.

<center>LUCKY</center>
> There. Truly disconnected.

Astra smiles, a genuine, heartfelt expression.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> Welcome home, Lucky.

They stand together, watching the sun begin its slow descent, painting the sky in hues of orange and purple. The humanoid robots power down for their evening charge cycle. The horses settle in their pastures. The marijuana greenhouse hums with grow lights coming online.

The silence is profound, unbroken.

FADE TO BLACK.
