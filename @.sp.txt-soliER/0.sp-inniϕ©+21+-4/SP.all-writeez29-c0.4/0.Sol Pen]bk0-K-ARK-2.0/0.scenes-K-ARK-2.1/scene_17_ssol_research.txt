INT. SSOL - RESEARCH LAB/MEDICAL BAY - DAY

SOUND of humming centrifuges, the soft beep of monitoring equipment, the whisper of climate control

The research lab is a cathedral of science — rows of holographic microscopes, molecular sequencers the size of small cars, and containment fields holding specimens that defy easy categorization. The walls are lined with transparent cylinders, each containing something that defies conventional biology.

ASTRA moves through the lab with the focused intensity of someone who has found her element. Her fingers dance across holographic interfaces, pulling up data streams, cross-referencing, analyzing.

A CLONE TECHNICIAN assists her — this one female, with a green band on her collar.

<center>CLONE TECHNICIAN</center>
> The cloning process is fully automated. Each clone is grown from a base template, then differentiated through targeted epigenetic modification. They're identical at the genetic level but develop unique neural patterns through experience.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> So they're not truly identical. They diverge over time.

<center>CLONE TECHNICIAN</center>
> Yes, ma'am. After approximately six months of independent experience, a clone develops what we call... individual variance. Some clones show creativity. Others show empathy. A few show something we can only describe as... soul.

Astra pauses at one of the containment cylinders. Inside, a CLONE FETUS floats in amber fluid, its development accelerated visibly — she can watch it grow in real time.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> How long from template to active soldier?

<center>CLONE TECHNICIAN</center>
> Three weeks gestation. Two weeks neural maturation. One week training integration. Six weeks total.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> (stunned)
> Six weeks. That's... that's impossibly fast. The epigenetic programming alone should take months.

<center>CLONE TECHNICIAN</center>
> The SolPen accelerates biological processes at the molecular level. It doesn't just create objects — it creates life.

Astra moves to another station. This one holds data on the SolPen's power source. She reads, her expression growing more troubled.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> "Clone souls as power." That's what Sol said. But what does that actually mean?

She pulls up the technical schematics. The data is dense, but she's brilliant. Slowly, it becomes clear.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> (murmuring to herself)
> The clones' neural activity... their consciousness... it generates a quantum field. The SolPen taps into that field. It uses conscious observation as an energy source. The more complex the consciousness, the more power available.

She looks up at the rows of containment cylinders, at the growing clones, and a chill runs through her.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> They're not just batteries. They're the engine of reality manipulation.

The Clone Technician shifts uncomfortably.

<center>CLONE TECHNICIAN</center>
> I'm not supposed to discuss this, ma'am.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> I'm not asking you to. I'm reading the data. It's all here.

Astra moves to a third station. This one contains biological data on the CARCAZODS. She pulls up images, and her stomach turns.

Carcazods are not natural organisms. They're CORRUPTIONS — biological matter infected by something that rewrites DNA at the quantum level. They're part machine, part organism, part something else entirely. Something that shouldn't exist.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> (reading)
> "Carcazod propagation occurs through quantum-level corruption of biological matter. The infection rewrites DNA, replacing carbon-based structures with... something else. Something that consumes rather than creates."

She looks at the SolPen data, then at the Carcazod data. The connection is clear.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> The SolPen creates. Carcazods consume. They're opposites. Two forces that shouldn't coexist in the same universe.

A movement in the corner of her eye. She turns.

In the shadows between two large equipment racks, something TRANSPARENT shifts. It's reptilian — she can see that much. The shape is wrong. Too many joints. Too angular. It moves with a fluid grace that suggests it's been alive for a very long time.

It sees her seeing it.

For a moment, they lock eyes — if it has eyes. The transparency makes it hard to tell. But she FEELS its attention. Ancient. Alien. Unfathomable.

Then it's gone. Slipping into a gap in the wall that shouldn't exist.

Astra stands frozen. Her scientific mind is racing through possibilities and finding none of them adequate.

<center>CLONE TECHNICIAN</center>
> (unfazed)
> Is everything alright, ma'am?

<center>ASTRA</center>
> (carefully)
> What was that? In the shadows?

<center>CLONE TECHNICIAN</center>
> (blank)
> What was what, ma'am?

Astra studies the clone's face. Genuine confusion. No deception.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> Nothing. Never mind.

She turns back to her data, but her hands are shaking. She pulls up one more file — Type C blood composition. The analysis is extraordinary.

Type C blood doesn't just carry oxygen. It carries QUANTUM INFORMATION — encoded data that rewrites the body's relationship with its environment. It's not just nutrition. It's PROGRAMMING. Everyone who drinks it is being modified, enhanced, connected to something larger.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> (whispering)
> Sol isn't just feeding them. He's networking them.

The implications are staggering. The clones aren't just an army. They're a DISTRIBUTED CONSCIOUSNESS. A collective mind that powers the SolPen and everything it touches.

Astra sits back, overwhelmed. She needs to tell Lucky. But what would she even say?

"Your new boss is running a moon-base society powered by clone souls, haunted by transparent reptilian entities, and the blood they drink is programming us all"?

She takes a deep breath. Opens a fresh data file. Starts taking notes.

Because science is what she does. And the science here is terrifying.

FADE OUT.
