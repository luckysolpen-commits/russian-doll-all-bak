INT. MARTIAN COURIER SHIP - COMMON AREA

SOUND of low music playing from a portable speaker, the clink of vials, the loose laughter of people who've survived too much

The common area of the courier ship has been transformed into a makeshift lounge. Lucky, Astra, Gem (recovered, though still pale), LAUREN (a female clone soldier with sharp features and sharper eyes), and three other FEMALE CLONES are sprawled across the floor.

They're high. Not drunk — high. A combination of chemical compounds they've been experimenting with: Type C blood derivatives, medicinal cannabis from Lucky's ranch (Astra brought samples), and something Lauren synthesized from Martian minerals.

The effect is a mellow, philosophical euphoria. The kind that makes people stare at the ceiling and ask big questions.

Empty vials of Type C blood are scattered around. A joint burns in an ashtray. Someone has produced a white powder that may or may not be bug spray precursor chemicals.

<center>LUCKY</center>
> (lying on his back, staring at the ceiling, his mohawk a mellow lavender tonight)
> Do you think God has a SolPen?

Silence. Then laughter.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> (sitting cross-legged, eyes red)
> What?

<center>LUCKY</center>
> Think about it. If God created the universe, what did He use? Some kind of... cosmic pen. Some tool that turns thought into reality. That's literally what the SolPen does.

<center>LAUREN</center>
> (lying on her side, propped on one elbow)
> So God is... Sol?

<center>GEM</center>
> (from a corner, smiling)
> Or Sol is God. From the future.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> That's the most ridiculous thing I've ever heard.

<center>LUCKY</center>
> Is it? Sol can create matter from thought. He can rewrite reality. He built a civilization inside the Moon. If that's not godlike, what is?

<center>LAUREN</center>
> Maybe the SolPen IS the divine tool. Maybe Sol didn't invent it. Maybe he found it. Or it found him.

The clones exchange glances. They have a different relationship with creation than humans do. They were created. They know what it feels like to be someone else's thought made real.

<center>CLONE 2</center>
> (quietly)
> If Sol is God, then what are we? Angels? Demons? Or just... clay?

<center>ASTRA</center>
> You're people. You're individuals. You're not clay.

<center>CLONE 3</center>
> Are we? We were grown in tubes. Programmed with purpose. Our souls are fuel for a weapon. That sounds like clay to me.

Lucky sits up. His lavender mohawk catches the dim light.

<center>LUCKY</center>
> I built an empire of information. Sol built an empire of clones. Neither of us asked if the people in our empires wanted to be there. That's the real question. Not whether Sol is God. Whether any of us have the right to play him.

Silence. The music plays — something slow, something old, something from Earth that none of them will take for granted again.

<center>GEM</center>
> (softly)
> Is it the end of the world?

<center>LUCKY</center>
> No. It's the end of ONE world. The one we knew. The one we built. The one we thought was permanent. But worlds end all the time. New ones begin. That's the cycle.

<center>ASTRA</center>
> (leaning against him)
> You're very philosophical for someone who's currently high on bug spray.

<center>LUCKY</center>
> Bug spray is just DMT with a marketing problem.

They laugh. The kind of laugh that comes from exhaustion and survival and the absurdity of existence.

Lucky looks around the room — at Astra, at Gem, at Lauren and the clones, at the scattered vials of Type C blood that keep them alive, at the SolPen holstered at his hip.

This is his team. His family. His creation.

And he'll use every tool at his disposal to keep them alive.

FADE OUT.
