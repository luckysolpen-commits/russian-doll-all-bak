Lucky's unconsciousness was a deep, dreamless void, a welcome respite from the pain and chaos.
He floated in a timeless space, his mind free from the burdens of the physical world.
He felt a gentle rocking, a subtle hum, but it was distant, muffled, like a memory.

He slowly began to surface, a faint light piercing the darkness.
He heard muffled voices, a low murmur that seemed to surround him.
He felt a soft pressure on his arm, a gentle touch that was surprisingly comforting.

He opened his eyes, slowly, his vision blurry at first.
The light was soft, diffused, coming from a single, glowing panel above him.
He was still on the cot, in the sleek, minimalist interior of the Blue Bird's ship.
His body still ached, but the crushing weakness that had plagued him now a distant memory.

He tried to sit up, but a wave of dizziness washed over him, and he fell back against the soft mattress.
His head throbbed, a dull ache behind his eyes.
He remembered the blinding flash of light, the searing pain, the overwhelming power of the mutagen.
He remembered his voice, amplified, resonating through the cavern, carrying his message of hope.

He looked around. The room was small, functional, devoid of any personal touches.
The walls were a muted gray, and a single, soft light illuminated the space.
He saw a medical kit on a small table beside his cot, its contents neatly arranged.
Someone had tended to his wounds, cleaned him up.

He heard a faint sound, a rhythmic tapping, and turned his head.
The Blue Bird Bounty Hunter was at the helm, her back to him, her silhouette framed by the vast, star-dusted expanse of space visible through the panoramic viewport.
Her armor, a dark, iridescent material, seemed to absorb the light, making her appear as if she were made of shadows.
Her helmet, with its single, glowing blue visor, gave her an otherworldly appearance.

"Awake, are we?" her voice, calm and even, cut through the quiet.
It was a low, melodic hum, a sound that seemed to resonate in his very bones.
"Good. Wouldn't want you missing the view."

Lucky pushed himself up again, more slowly this time, and managed to sit upright.
His body still ached, but the dizziness had subsided.
He swung his legs over the side of the cot, his feet touching the cool, metallic floor.

He approached the viewport, his steps a little unsteady.
Below them, Earth was a distant, swirling marble, scarred by the robot wars, a painful reminder of the world he had left behind.
It was a beautiful, tragic sight, a testament to humanity's resilience and its capacity for self-destruction.

Ahead, a shimmering, artificial light grew larger with each passing moment – Ikarus, the Ellison Island of Earth refugees, a beacon of hope in the desolate cosmos.
It was a massive, sprawling space station, a city in the void, its lights twinkling like a thousand scattered diamonds.

"Ellison Island," the Blue Bird continued, her gaze fixed on the approaching light.
"Where the desperate and the hopeful gather. Where those who can't afford the official channels try their luck.
Viruses, hacking, Empire patrols... it's a gamble."

Lucky felt a strange sense of unease.
He had heard whispers of Ellison Island, a place where the rules were fluid, where anything could be bought or sold.
It was a place of freedom, but also a place of danger.
He wondered what kind of world awaited him there.
