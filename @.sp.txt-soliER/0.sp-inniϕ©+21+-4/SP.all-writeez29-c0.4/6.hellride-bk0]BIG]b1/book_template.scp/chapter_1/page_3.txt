The light of the Metal Sun was not just seen; it was felt.
It seeped through the hull of the *Stray Dog*,
a strange, tingling energy that made the hairs on Lucky’s arms stand up.
It was a clean, sterile energy, devoid of the chaotic warmth of a natural star.
It felt like being dissected, laid bare under a microscope.
He could almost hear the hum of it, a high, keening whine
that was more a vibration in his bones than a sound in his ears.

On the comms, the solar system was a cacophony of panicked voices.
Distress calls from freighters caught too close to the Icarus.
Confused chatter from orbital stations.
The sharp, barked orders of military fleets scrambling to respond.
But it was all just noise, the frantic buzzing of flies caught in a web.
They were all reacting, but the Architect had already acted.
He had rewritten the rules of the game, and they were all still trying to play by the old ones.

"What do we do, Lucky?" Maya’s voice was a thin thread in the chaos.
Her usual bravado was gone, stripped away by the raw power of the Architect’s creation.
She was looking at him, her dark eyes wide and searching.
They were all looking at him.

Lucky’s mind raced, but it felt like running in mud.
Every option seemed futile.
Run? Where? The Metal Sun’s light was already touching the outer planets.
Fight? With what? Their single, aging laser cannon against a man-made god?
It was like trying to punch a hurricane.

He felt a surge of helpless anger, hot and sharp.
He hated this feeling, the sense of being a piece on someone else’s board.
He had spent his life dodging the grasp of corporations, of governments,
of anyone who tried to put a leash on him.
And now this. The ultimate leash.
The Architect hadn’t just built a weapon; he had built a cage, and the entire solar system was inside it.

"We stay quiet," Lucky said, his voice low and steady, belying the turmoil in his gut.
"We watch. We learn."
He hated the words even as he said them. It felt like surrender.
But it was the only play they had.
To charge in now would be suicide.
They were insignificant, a gnat in the face of a supernova.
But gnats could still bite.
They just had to pick their moment.

He looked at the Metal Sun, its light now a searing, painful glare.
It was beautiful, in a terrible, awe-inspiring way.
Like watching a world burn.
He thought of the people on Earth, on Mars, on the orbital stations,
all looking up at this new, false dawn.
Some would be filled with wonder, with hope.
They would see the Architect as a savior, a visionary.
They wouldn’t see the bars of the cage descending around them.
Not yet.

But Lucky saw them. He saw the cold, hard lines of control
behind the beautiful, shimmering facade.
He saw the ego, the madness, the insatiable hunger for power.
He had seen it before, in the eyes of corporate CEOs and petty warlords.
But never on this scale.
This was a new level of ambition, a new definition of tyranny.

"Jax, get us out of the direct light," Lucky ordered, his voice regaining some of its old authority.
"Find us a shadow to hide in. An asteroid, a moon, anything."
"And Maya," he said, his gaze meeting hers in the reflection of the viewport.
"I want you to listen. Not to the comms, to the net.
I want to know everything about this… this thing.
Its energy signature, its composition, any weakness."
It was a fool's errand, but it was better than doing nothing.
It was a spark of defiance in the face of overwhelming power.
It was a start.

The *Stray Dog* banked, its engines groaning in protest,
and began to move, a tiny, insignificant speck fleeing the terrible, beautiful light of the Metal Sun.
Lucky watched it recede, but he knew they weren’t escaping.
They were just finding a better vantage point from which to watch the end of their world.
