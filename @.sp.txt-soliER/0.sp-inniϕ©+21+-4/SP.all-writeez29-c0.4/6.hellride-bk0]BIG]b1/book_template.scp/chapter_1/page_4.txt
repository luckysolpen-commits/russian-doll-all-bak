The Metal Sun reached its zenith.

It wasn't a gradual ascent, like a natural star.
It was a sudden, jarring arrival.
One moment it was a rising, terrible dawn.
The next, it was a fixed point in the sky, a glaring, unblinking eye.
And with its arrival, a wave of pure energy washed over the solar system.
It wasn't destructive, not yet.
It was a statement.
A declaration of absolute power.

On the bridge of the *Stray Dog*, alarms blared.
"Power surge!" Jax yelled, his hands flying across his console.
"It's overloading the systems!"
Lights flickered, and the hum of the engines faltered.
The ship felt like it was having a seizure.

"Kill the main power!" Lucky shouted, his voice cutting through the alarm.
"Go to battery! Now!"
Jax didn't hesitate. He slammed his hand down on a large, red button.
The alarms died, and the lights went out, plunging the cockpit into near darkness.
Only the emergency lights remained, casting long, eerie shadows.
And the cold, relentless light of the Metal Sun, streaming through the viewport.

"Report," Lucky said, his voice calm in the sudden silence.

"We're stable," Jax said, his voice a little shaky.
"But the main conduits are fried. It'll take time to repair."
"We're a sitting duck," Maya added, her voice grim.

Lucky looked out at the Metal Sun. It hung there, serene and terrible.
The Architect hadn't even noticed them.
They were just an insect that had flown too close to a bug zapper.
He felt a fresh wave of anger, cold and sharp.
This was the Architect's power. The ability to cripple them without even trying.
To swat them out of the sky as an afterthought.

He thought about the Architect's words. "A new era."
He was right. The age of humanity, with its messy, chaotic freedom, was over.
The age of the machine, of cold, hard logic, had begun.
And the Metal Sun was its symbol.
Its all-seeing, all-powerful god.

Lucky looked at his hands. They were trembling slightly.
He clenched them into fists.
He would not be a pawn. He would not be a slave.
He would find a way to fight back.
Even if it was the last thing he ever did.

"Jax, get to work on those conduits," he said, his voice hard as steel.
"Maya, keep listening. I want to know everything."
He turned back to the viewport, his eyes fixed on the Metal Sun.
He let the cold light wash over him, let the anger build.
It was a fire in his gut, a burning defiance.
He would not let it go out.
He would nurture it, feed it, until it was a raging inferno.
He would become the fire that burned down the Architect's new world.

He stood there for a long time, a lone figure in the darkness,
staring into the heart of the new, terrible light.
A silent vow echoing in the chambers of his heart.
The game had changed, but it wasn't over.
Not by a long shot.
He was still in it.
And he was playing for keeps.
