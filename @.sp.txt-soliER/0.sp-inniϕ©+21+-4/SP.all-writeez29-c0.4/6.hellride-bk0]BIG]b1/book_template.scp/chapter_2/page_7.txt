While Lucky briefed the pilots, Edge was a whirlwind of activity in the main war room.
He moved with a predatory grace, his cybernetic eye scanning the holoscreens,
his mind processing data at an astonishing speed.
He was in his element here, in the controlled chaos of impending battle.
This was the world he understood, the world where strength and cunning were the only currencies that mattered.

He barked orders into his comm unit, his voice a low, gravelly rasp.
"Confirm all Metal Mafia assets are in position!"
"I want real-time intel on Icarus fleet movements!"
"Any chatter from Mars or Luna? I want to know what those traitors are planning!"

Admiral Rostova watched him from across the room, her expression unreadable.
She still didn't trust him, but she respected his efficiency.
He was a blunt instrument, but sometimes, a blunt instrument was exactly what you needed.

Edge wasn't just relying on the UN fleet.
He had called in every favor, every contact, every dirty secret he had accumulated over the years.
His network of smugglers, pirates, and black marketeers was a shadow fleet,
moving in the dark, ready to strike when the moment was right.
They were the wild cards, the unpredictable element that might just tip the scales.

He saw a young UN officer struggling with a complex targeting system.
Edge strode over, his presence a sudden, intimidating force.
"What's the holdup, Ensign?"

"Sir, the algorithms for predicting the transport ship's evasive maneuvers are..." the Ensign stammered.

Edge leaned over the console, his cybernetic eye glowing faintly.
"Forget the algorithms," he growled. "They're programmed by bureaucrats.
Think like a smuggler. Where would you go if you were trying to avoid detection?
Where's the blind spot?"
His finger jabbed at a point on the screen, a seemingly innocuous patch of space.
"Here. They'll try to slip through here. Adjust your projections."

The Ensign, wide-eyed, made the adjustments.
The new trajectory appeared, a subtle but significant shift.
Admiral Rostova, who had been observing, gave a barely perceptible nod of approval.

Edge felt a surge of grim satisfaction.
This was his war too.
He had been driven underground, hunted like an animal.
He had seen his empire, his carefully constructed world, crumble under the Architect's heel.
Now, it was time for payback.

He thought of Lucky, briefing the pilots.
They were two sides of the same coin, he and Lucky.
Both outlaws, both survivors.
But Lucky still believed in something more than just survival.
He believed in freedom. In justice.
Edge had almost forgotten what those words meant.
But seeing Lucky, seeing the fire in his eyes, had rekindled a spark within him.

He looked at the countdown clock.
Less than an hour.
The air in the war room was thick with anticipation, with the unspoken question of what the next few hours would bring.
Victory or annihilation.
There was no middle ground.

Edge walked to the main holoscreen, his gaze fixed on the Metal Sun.
It hung there, serene and terrible, a monument to arrogance.
He imagined the Architect, sitting on his throne, confident in his victory.
He imagined the smug smile on his face.

"Enjoy your last moments of peace, Architect," Edge muttered, a dark promise in his voice.
"Because hell is coming for you."
He turned, his eyes hard, his jaw set.
The time for talking was over.
The time for fighting had begun.
