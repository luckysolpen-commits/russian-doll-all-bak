The war room buzzed with a frantic energy.
Officers moved with purpose, their voices sharp and efficient.
Lucky found himself in a small, sterile briefing room, facing a dozen grim-faced pilots.
They were young, most of them, barely out of the academy, their faces still smooth,
untouched by the harsh realities of war.
But their eyes held a steely resolve, a quiet determination.

"Alright, listen up," Lucky began, his voice cutting through the low hum of the room.
He wasn't a military man, but he knew how to talk to pilots.
He had flown enough hot runs, enough desperate escapes, to understand their language.
"This isn't a drill. This is the real thing.
We're going up against the Architect's World Police Bots.
These aren't your standard corporate security drones.
They're faster, smarter, and they're networked directly to the Icarus."

He pointed to a holographic projection of the bot, rotating slowly in the center of the room.
"Their primary weakness is their central processing unit, located here,"
he tapped a glowing red spot on the bot's head.
"It's heavily armored, but a direct hit with a high-energy plasma burst will disable it."

"What about their defenses?" a young pilot asked, his voice a little shaky.

"They have energy shields, standard issue," Lucky replied.
"But they're designed for crowd control, not combat.
They can deflect small arms fire, but they won't stand up to sustained fire from your fighters."
He paused, letting the information sink in.
"The biggest threat isn't their firepower. It's their numbers.
They'll try to overwhelm you. They'll try to box you in."

He looked at each pilot, meeting their gaze.
"Your mission is simple: get in, disable the transports, and get out.
Do not engage the bots unless absolutely necessary.
Your primary target is the transport ships.
Disable their engines, their navigation, anything that will stop them from reaching Earth."

He then brought up a detailed map of the Lagrange point, showing the projected approach vectors.
"Admiral Rostova's fleet will engage the main escort ships.
Your squadron will be a surgical strike force.
Hit them hard, hit them fast, and then disengage."

He could see the fear in their eyes, but also the excitement.
The thrill of the hunt, the promise of glory.
He knew that feeling. It was a dangerous drug.

"Remember," Lucky said, his voice dropping to a low, serious tone,
"every bot you disable, every transport you cripple,
is a life saved on Earth.
This isn't just a mission. It's a rescue operation."

He looked at them, at these young men and women who were about to face a force beyond their comprehension.
He saw himself in their eyes, the same reckless courage, the same desperate hope.
He wished he could tell them it would be easy.
He wished he could tell them they would all come back.
But he couldn't.

"Any questions?" he asked.

Silence.

"Good," Lucky said, a grim smile touching his lips.
"Then let's go save the world."
He turned and walked out of the briefing room, the weight of their lives heavy on his shoulders.
He had given them their orders.
Now, he had to give them a chance to execute them.
