The storm broke on a Tuesday. It arrived not
with a clap of thunder or a flash of
lightning, but with a single, piercing scream that shattered
the morning calm. The scream came from the Rose
Room. Madame Dupont, who was in the kitchen preparing
her signature lemon ricotta pancakes, dropped her spatula and
rushed to the foyer, her heart pounding in her
chest. Seraphina Monet was standing at the top of
the grand staircase, her face a mask of terror,
her hand pointing a trembling finger back towards her
room. "It's gone!" she cried, her voice a mixture
of disbelief and hysteria. "It's gone!"

Madame Dupont, her face a calm and reassuring mask,
ascended the stairs, her hand gently resting on the
actress's arm. "What is it, my dear?" she asked,
her voice a soothing balm. "What is missing?" Seraphina,
her body trembling, allowed herself to be led back
into the Rose Room. The room was in disarray.
Clothes were strewn across the floor, drawers were pulled
open, and a small, velvet jewelry box lay open
and empty on the vanity. "My necklace," Seraphina whispered,
her voice barely audible. "The Serpent's Eye. It's gone."
She sank onto the bed, her face buried in
her hands. "It was a gift," she sobbed. "From
my grandmother. It's priceless."

Madame Dupont surveyed the room, her keen eyes taking
in every detail. The window was locked from the
inside. The door showed no signs of forced entry.
Nothing else in the room seemed to be missing.
Just the necklace. A very specific, and apparently very
valuable, item. This was not a random robbery. This
was something else entirely. "I will find it," Madame
Dupont said, her voice filled with a quiet determination.
"I promise you." She then did something she hadn't
done in over a decade. She called the police.

The arrival of the San Francisco Police Department shattered
the carefully constructed tranquility of the Chateau Tivoli. Two
detectives, a man and a woman, arrived, their faces
stern and professional. The man, Detective Miles Corbin, was
a tall, lanky man with a weary, seen-it-all expression.
The woman, Detective Isabella Rossi, was younger, with sharp,
intelligent eyes that missed nothing. They interviewed Seraphina, their
questions polite but probing. They dusted for fingerprints, they
searched the room, they spoke with the other guests.
Silas Blackwood, when questioned, was cool and detached, his
alibi airtight. He had been in his room, working,
all morning. He had seen nothing, heard nothing. Mr.
Smith, on the other hand, was visibly distressed by the
commotion. He had been in the library, he said,
lost in a book. He too had seen nothing,
heard nothing. The police, after a thorough investigation, were
baffled. There were no suspects, no leads, no explanation
for how the necklace could have vanished from a
locked room. It was, they concluded, a mystery.

But Madame Dupont knew better. This was not just
a mystery. This was a message. Someone was playing
a game, a dangerous game, and the Chateau Tivoli
was the board. The theft of the Serpent's Eye
was the opening move. And as the police departed,
leaving behind a palpable sense of unease, Madame Dupont
knew that it was up to her to uncover
the truth. She would have to become a detective
in her own home, to peel back the layers
of secrets and lies that her guests had brought
with them. The goings-on at the Chateau Tivoli had
taken a sinister turn. And as she looked at
the faces of her three guests, she couldn't help
but wonder which one of them was a thief.
Or if, perhaps, they were all playing a part
in a much larger, more complex, and far more
dangerous game than she could possibly imagine. The first
piece had been moved. Now, it was her turn
to make a move.
