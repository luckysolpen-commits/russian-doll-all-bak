The investigation began in the kitchen, with Pierre's attempt
to appease the mischievous poltergeist. He prepared a feast
fit for a king, a banquet of the ghost's favorite
things: a bottle of the finest French wine, a
platter of artisanal cheeses, a basket of freshly baked
bread, and, of course, a large slice of his
famous apple tart. He placed the offering on a
silver tray and carried it to the blank wall
where the Blue Room had once been. "A little
something for you, my friend," he whispered, his voice
a mixture of reverence and fear. "A token of
our esteem. Please, eat, drink, and be merry. And
please, for the love of God, give us back
our room."

The offering sat there for a full day, untouched.
The wine remained uncorked, the cheese uneaten, the apple
tart as perfect as the moment it had come
out of the oven. The ghost, it seemed, was
not a foodie.

Next, it was Esmeralda's turn. She performed a cleansing
ritual, a ceremony that was as old as the
hills, a ritual that had been passed down to
her from her grandmother, who had been a curandera,
a healer, a woman who knew the ways of
the spirit world. She burned sage, she chanted ancient
prayers, she sprinkled holy water on the walls. She
was a force of nature, a whirlwind of spiritual
energy, a woman who was determined to drive the
evil spirits from the house.

The house, however, seemed to be unimpressed. The wall
remained stubbornly blank, the Blue Room remained stubbornly gone.
The spirits, it seemed, were not afraid of a
little smoke and holy water.

Finally, it was Alastair's turn. He began his investigation
in the library, poring over the old blueprints of
the house, searching for a clue, a hidden passage,
a secret room that might explain the impossible. He
spent hours in the dusty archives of the city,
reading old newspapers, studying old maps, looking for
any mention of the Chateau Tivoli, any story,
any legend, any myth that might shed some light
on the mystery.

And then, he found it. It was a small,
almost insignificant, article in an old, yellowed newspaper from
the 1920s. It was a story about a
magician, a famous illusionist who had once stayed at
the Chateau Tivoli. He was a man who was
known for his spectacular feats of magic, his ability
to make things disappear, to make the impossible seem
possible. And his most famous trick, the one that
had made him a legend, was called "The Vanishing
Room."

Alastair's eyes widened in disbelief. He had found it.
He had found the key to the mystery. The
disappearing room was not the work of a ghost
or a poltergeist or an angry spirit. It was
the work of a magician, a master of illusion,
a man who had left his mark on the
Chateau Tivoli in a most unusual and unforgettable
way. The question was, how had he done it?
And more importantly, how could they get the room
back?
