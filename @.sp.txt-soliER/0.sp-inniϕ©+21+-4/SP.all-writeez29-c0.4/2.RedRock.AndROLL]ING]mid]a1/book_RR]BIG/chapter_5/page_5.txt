He stepped forward, his hands raised in a gesture of peace.
“Commander,” he said, his voice calm, authoritative. “I am
Lucky Starr, CEO of Triton Biotech. I believe I can be of
-assistance.”

The UN commander looked at him, her eyes narrowed with
-suspicion. “Mr. Starr. What is your involvement in this?”

“My involvement,” Lucky replied, a charming, disarming
-smile on his face, “is that I am a concerned citizen who
-happens to have a great deal of experience in… crisis
-management. And it seems to me that you have a crisis on
your hands.”

He was playing a dangerous game, but it was the only one
he knew. He was using his reputation, his influence, his
-carefully constructed persona, as a weapon. He was a wolf
in sheep’s clothing, and he was about to lead the sheep
-into a very dangerous pasture.

He saw a flicker of uncertainty in the commander’s eyes.
She was out of her depth, and she knew it. She was a soldier,
trained to deal with physical threats. She was not prepared
for this kind of political, biological warfare.

“I need to speak with the envoy,” Lucky said, his voice
-firm, confident. “And I need to speak with the Queen. I
-believe I can help you to… de-escalate this situation.”

He was lying, of course. He had no intention of de-
-escalating anything. He was going to pour gasoline on the
-fire. But he needed to get to the Queen. She was the key.
She had the information he needed to stop Zara.

And so, the game began. A game of lies and deception, of
-half-truths and hidden agendas. A game where the fate of
two worlds hung in the balance. And Lucky Starr was right
in the middle of it, exactly where he belonged.
