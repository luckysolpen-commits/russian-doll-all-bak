As the envoy spoke, Lucky’s gaze kept drifting to Astra
and Gem. He saw the anger in Astra’s eyes, the cold, hard
-fury of a warrior who had found her cause. He saw the
-analytical detachment in Gem’s gaze, the quiet, focused
-intensity of a strategist who was already planning her
-next move.

He felt a pang of something that might have been nostalgia,
a memory of a time when they had been a team, a family.
A time before the betrayals, the lies, the regrets.

He knew he had to earn back their trust. He had to prove
to them that he was not the same man he had been all those
-years ago. He had to show them that he was worthy of their
-loyalty again.

It would not be easy. The wounds were deep, the scars were
-real. But he had to try. He needed them. He could not do
this alone.

When the envoy had finished his story, Lucky stood up.
“Thank you,” he said, his voice filled with a genuine
-sympathy. “You have done a brave thing.”

He turned to the UN commander. “I need to see the Queen,”
he said, his voice firm, resolute. “Now.”

The commander hesitated for a moment, then nodded. She had
-heard enough. She knew that this was a situation that was
-beyond her control. She needed help. And Lucky Starr, for
-all his flaws, was the only one offering it.

They were led through the sterile corridors of Triton Tower,
to a suite of rooms that had been converted into a temporary,
-gilded cage for the Red Queen. She stood by a panoramic
-window, gazing out at the shimmering cityscape of Atlantis
Prime. She was a queen in exile, a prisoner of her own
-ambition.

She turned as they entered, her eyes, once sharp and
-commanding, now held a flicker of desperation. She looked
at Lucky, a flicker of recognition, of hope, in her gaze.

“You are the one they call Starr,” she said, her voice a
-hoarse whisper.

Lucky nodded. “And you are the one who is going to help
me to save us all.”
