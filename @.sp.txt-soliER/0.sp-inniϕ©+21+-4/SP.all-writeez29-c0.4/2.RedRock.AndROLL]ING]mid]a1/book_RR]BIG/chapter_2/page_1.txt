The bass was a physical presence in Club Nova, a primal
heartbeat that throbbed against Gem’s ribs and vibrated
through the polished chrome floor. It was a relentless,
sensual assault, a sound you felt more than you heard.
Neon lights, a dizzying, chaotic kaleidoscope of electric
blues and pulsing purples, painted the faces of the dancers
in ghostly, fleeting hues. The air was thick, a humid
cocktail of synthetic jasmine, recycled oxygen, and the
faint, sweet scent of spilled bioluminescent cocktails.
It was a world away from the sterile, controlled environment
of her security office, a place of calculated risks and
-unspoken threats.

She stood at the bar, a shimmering, turquoise concoction
in her hand. It tasted vaguely of citrus and disappointment.
Her eyes, one a cool, analytical gray, the other a discreet,
cybernetic implant that glowed with a faint, internal light,
scanned the crowd. Every face was a potential threat, every
shadow a possible ambush. Old habits died hard, especially
when those habits had been forged in the crucible of a dozen
-covert operations and a hundred firefights. Her cybernetic
eye, a constant stream of data scrolling across her vision,
cross-referenced faces with known threat profiles, analyzed
body language, and monitored the club’s security systems.
It was a constant, low-level hum of paranoia, a state of
-being she had come to accept as normal.

She was a ghost in this world of vibrant, chaotic life.
A silent observer, a predator in a jungle of unsuspecting
prey. She saw the desperation in the eyes of the dancers,
the forced smiles, the frantic search for a moment of
-connection, of escape. She saw the predators, too. The
loan sharks, the information brokers, the corporate spies,
all circling, waiting for a moment of weakness. It was a
different kind of battlefield, but the rules were the same.
Survive. Adapt. Exploit.

She took a slow sip of her drink, the synthetic flavor
coating her tongue. She was waiting. Her contact was late,
which was unusual. Ratchet was a creature of habit, a man
who understood the importance of punctuality in their line
of work. His tardiness was a deviation from the baseline,
a potential indicator of a problem. And in her world,
problems were rarely small.
