“Any new intel?” Gem asked, her voice low, her gaze fixed on a
group of boisterous revelers who were drawing too much attention
to themselves. Her cybernetic eye flagged them as low-level
dealers, their pockets likely filled with cheap, synthetic drugs.
Amateurs. Cannon fodder in the grand scheme of things.

Ratchet took another long swig of his drink, his eyes scanning
the club with a practiced, weary vigilance. “More of the same,”
he said, his voice a low rumble. “The Red Queen’s losing her
grip. Her own people are starting to turn on her. Zara’s faction
is gaining ground, promising a ‘solution’ to the blood crisis.
But their solution involves… less than ethical means.”

“Less than ethical is Zara’s middle name,” Gem muttered, a cold
-knot tightening in her stomach. Zara, the ruthless corporate
-warlord, the architect of so much of the chaos that had plagued
the system for years. She had been a thorn in humanity’s side
long before the Carcazods. Now, with Mars in turmoil, she was
poised to exploit the chaos, to turn it to her advantage.

“She’s not just promising a solution,” Ratchet continued, his
voice dropping even lower, forcing Gem to lean closer to hear
him over the music. “She’s actively sabotaging the Queen’s
-efforts to find one. She’s intercepting medical supplies,
-bribing officials, spreading propaganda. She’s creating the
very crisis she claims she wants to solve.”

Gem’s mind raced, processing the information. It was a classic
Zara move. Create a problem, then offer the only solution.
A solution that would, of course, leave her in complete control.
She was a master of manipulation, a player in a game where entire
-populations were just pawns on a chessboard.

“They’re pushing for a full-scale invasion of Earth,” Ratchet
-added, his voice barely a whisper. “Claiming the UN is hoarding
-resources, that the Martian clones are being left to die. It’s
all lies, of course, but it’s gaining traction. The clones are
-desperate. They’ll believe anything that gives them a glimmer
of hope.”

Gem felt a chill that had nothing to do with the club’s air
-conditioning. An invasion. After all they had been through,
after the retreat to the oceans, the fragile rebuilding. The
-thought of another war, especially one fueled by internal
-divisions, was sickening. It was a self-destructive impulse
that seemed to be hardwired into the human psyche.
