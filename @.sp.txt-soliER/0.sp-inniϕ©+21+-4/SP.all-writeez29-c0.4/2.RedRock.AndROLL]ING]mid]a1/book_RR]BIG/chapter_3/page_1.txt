The air in the mercenary hideout was a familiar, stagnant
cocktail. It was a layered scent, a history of the room
written in molecules. The base note was stale synth-ale,
a sweet, cloying odor that clung to the worn upholstery
of the mismatched couches. Layered on top of that was the
sharp, metallic tang of gunpowder and ozone, a constant
-reminder of the tools of their trade. And beneath it all,
the faint, coppery scent of uncleaned weaponry and old,
-dried blood. It was the smell of restless, caged predators,
and Astra, for one, was getting tired of it.

She ran a calloused thumb over the worn grip of her plasma
-rifle, the cool, familiar weight of it a comforting presence
in her hand. It was a custom job, a sniper rifle she had
-built herself, its every curve and calibration as familiar
to her as her own reflection. Around her, the remnants of
her crew, a motley collection of hardened veterans and
-fresh-faced recruits, lounged in various states of disarray.
They were a pack of wolves, penned in a sheepfold, and their
-patience was wearing thin.

The hideout itself was a testament to their current fortunes.
A converted cargo bay in the forgotten underbelly of a remote
-space station. The walls were dented, scarred metal, the
-lighting a flickering, unreliable fluorescent. It was a far
-cry from the high-end, discreet safehouses they had used
-during the war. It was a place you went when you were on
your last credit, when the jobs had dried up, when the
-universe had forgotten you existed.

She felt a familiar restlessness, a gnawing hunger for
-action. The peace, fragile as it was, had made them obsolete.
The UN’s tight control over interstellar travel and weaponry
had choked off their traditional revenue streams. They were
-mercenaries without a war, soldiers without a battle. And
it was slowly, surely, driving them all mad. She could see
it in their eyes, the dull, listless boredom, the desperate
-yearning for the adrenaline and clarity of combat.
