
Chapter 3: The Mercenary's Restlessness

The air in the mercenary hideout was a familiar cocktail of stale synth-ale,
gunpowder, and the faint, metallic tang of uncleaned weaponry. Astra ran a
calloused thumb over the worn grip of her plasma rifle, the cool steel a
comforting weight in her hand. Around her, the remnants of her crew, a motley
collection of hardened veterans and fresh-faced recruits, lounged in various
states of disarray. They were restless, a pack of wolves penned in a sheepfold.

“Another day, another dollar… not,” grumbled Jax, a hulking brute with a cybernetic
arm and a perpetually sour expression. He tossed a data-pad onto a dented metal
table, the screen displaying a paltry contract for escorting a cargo freighter
through a supposedly safe sector. “This is what we’ve been reduced to? glorified
bouncers?”

Astra didn’t respond, her gaze fixed on the flickering news feed on the communal
screen. Martian unrest. The Red Queen’s grip weakening. Zara’s corporate forces
making aggressive moves. It was a powder keg, and they were sitting on the sidelines,
watching it burn.

“Remember the good old days, Astra?” chimed in Kael, a wiry scout with eyes that
missed nothing. “When we were actually *doing* something? When there were real
stakes, real fights?”

She remembered. The adrenaline, the precision of a well-executed raid, the thrill
of outsmarting an enemy. That was living. This… this was just existing. The peace,
fragile as it was, had made them obsolete. The UN’s tight control over interstellar
travel and weaponry had choked off their traditional revenue streams. They were
mercenaries without a war, soldiers without a battle.

“The Martian factions are getting bolder,” Astra finally said, her voice low.
“The Queen’s ‘Vampire’ guards are a liability, and Zara’s exploiting it. There’s
a power vacuum, and someone’s going to fill it.”

“And it won’t be us, stuck here babysitting glorified space trucks,” Jax scoffed.
“Unless, of course, someone decides to hire us for a *real* job. Like, say, a little
regime change on Mars?” He looked at Astra, a glint in his eye.

Astra considered it. The thought of action, of putting her skills to use again,
sent a jolt through her. But the risks were immense. The UN wouldn’t tolerate
any interference in Martian affairs, not with the delicate peace they’d brokered.

“Too hot,” she said, shaking her head. “Not yet, anyway. We need to be smart about
this. Wait for the right opportunity.”

“Opportunity? We’ll be collecting dust by then!” Kael exclaimed, frustrated.
“The universe is moving on, Astra. And we’re stuck in neutral.”

Suddenly, the comm unit on the table chirped, a secure, encrypted signal. Astra
raised an eyebrow. This wasn’t a standard contract offer. She tapped the screen,
and a distorted voice, familiar yet unplaceable, spoke.

“Astra. We have a situation. Mars. Urgent. Meet me at the Triton Tower Hangar Bay.
Discreetly. And bring your best.”

The line went dead. Astra looked at her crew, a slow smile spreading across her face.
“Looks like the universe just sent us an invitation,” she said, picking up her rifle.
“Pack your bags, boys. We’re going to Mars.”

Jax and Kael exchanged glances, their restlessness replaced by a spark of anticipation.
This was it. The chance they’d been waiting for. The chance to be mercenaries again.
