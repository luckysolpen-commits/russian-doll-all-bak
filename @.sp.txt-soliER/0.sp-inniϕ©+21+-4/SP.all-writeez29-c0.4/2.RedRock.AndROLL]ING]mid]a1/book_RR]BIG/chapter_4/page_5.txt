“I will consider your… proposal,” the Queen said, her voice
tight with suppressed fury. The words were a lie, a delaying
-tactic. But it was the only weapon she had left.

“But know this, Zara,” she added, a spark of defiance in
her eyes. “Mars will never truly be yours. Its spirit will
-resist you, always.”

Zara merely smiled, a chilling, triumphant expression that
-sent a shiver down the Queen’s spine. “We shall see, Your
Majesty. We shall see.”

The holo-projection flickered and died, leaving the Queen
-alone in the silent, oppressive weight of her throne room.
The silence was a physical presence, a crushing weight on
her chest.

She rose from her throne, her crimson robes swirling around
her like a pool of blood. She walked to the window, her
-movements stiff, weary. She gazed out at the desolate
-landscape, the endless, unforgiving red of Mars.

The red dust, stirred by a perpetual wind, seemed to
-whisper of ancient battles, of empires risen and fallen.
Her own empire was falling. Her grip was indeed fading.
The power vacuum was growing. And Zara, like a vulture,
was circling, ready to feast on the corpse.

She felt a wave of utter, complete despair. She was alone.
Her allies had abandoned her. Her people were turning against
her. She was a queen without a kingdom, a leader without
-followers.

She had been so proud, so confident in her own abilities.
She had believed she could protect her people, that she
could lead them to a brighter future. But she had failed.
She had underestimated her enemy, overestimated her own
-strength. And now, she was paying the price.
