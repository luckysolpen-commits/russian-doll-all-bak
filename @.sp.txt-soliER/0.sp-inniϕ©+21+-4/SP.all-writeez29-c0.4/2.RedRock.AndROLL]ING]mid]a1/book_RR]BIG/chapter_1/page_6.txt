“The Carcazods,” Lucky said, his voice dropping, the name itself a
weapon. He saw Kaito flinch, a subtle, almost imperceptible reaction,
but it was there. The fear was still raw, even after all these years.

“They are contained, of course,” Lucky continued, his tone dismissive,
as if speaking of a minor inconvenience. “Confined to the surface
world, unable to reach us here in the deep. But we are fools if we
believe they are no longer a threat.”

He didn’t need to embellish the truth. The memory of the Carcazod
plague was seared into the collective consciousness of humanity.
He painted a vivid picture, his words sharp and precise. He spoke
of the screeching, chitinous hordes that had swarmed across the
continents, of the cities burned to ash, of the desperate, bloody
retreat to the oceans. He reminded Kaito of the refugee camps,
the plagues that had followed, the near-extinction of their species.

“We survived,” he said, his voice a low, intense whisper. “But we
are not safe. Our supply lines from the surface are fragile. A
single disruption, a single Carcazod resurgence, and our hydroponic
farms, with their reliance on surface-based resources, will fail.
And what happens then, Director?”

He leaned closer to the holographic display, his eyes locking with
Kaito’s. “A hungry populace is a restless populace. And a restless
populace is vulnerable. Imagine riots in the corridors of Atlantis
Prime. Imagine the city turning on itself, the fragile peace we
have built shattering into a million pieces. That is the true
threat, Director. Not the monsters outside, but the monster within.
The monster of desperation, of hunger, of fear.”

He watched Kaito’s face, saw the man’s composure crumble. The
bureaucratic mask was gone, replaced by the raw, primal fear of a
man staring into the abyss. Lucky had him. He had taken the
conversation out of the realm of spreadsheets and into the dark,
twisted landscape of Kaito’s own nightmares.

It was a cruel, calculated move. A manipulation of the highest
order. And it filled Lucky with a familiar, exhilarating thrill.
The same thrill he’d felt when outsmarting an enemy, when a
perfectly executed plan came together. It was the thrill of control,
of power. The thrill he had been missing, the thrill he craved more
than anything else. He was not just a biotech entrepreneur. He was
a warrior, and this was his battlefield.
