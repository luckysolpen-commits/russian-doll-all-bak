The hum was the first thing you noticed.

A deep, resonant thrum that vibrated through the soles
of your shoes, up your spine, and settled in the base
of your skull. It was the sound of life support, of a million
tons of water held at bay by engineering and sheer will.
It was the sound of Triton Tower, and for Lucky Starr, it was
the constant, soothing lullaby that had replaced the chaotic
symphony of his past. He sat behind a desk carved from a single,
Bioluminescent coral, its polished surface swirling with faint,
internal light, like a captured galaxy.

Sunlight, having fought its way down through a hundred meters
of the Atlantic, was further filtered by the reinforced acrylic
of the panoramic window. It painted shifting, aqueous patterns
of turquoise and silver across the room, dancing over the minimalist
decor and the sharp creases of Lucky’s bespoke suit. The air
tasted clean, recycled, with the faint, sharp tang of ozone from
the humming electronics that powered his world. This high up,
in the apex of the city he had helped build, the silence between
the thrum of the hydro-filters was profound. It was a silence
bought with blood, credits, and lies.

He adjusted the cuff of his left sleeve, a nervous habit he’d
never quite shaken. The fabric was a silk-alloy blend, cool
and smooth against his skin, a world away from the rough, sweat-
stained synth-leather of his former life. On his desk, next to
the shimmering holographic display that was his primary connection
to the world of legitimate business, sat a single object that didn’t
belong. It was a small, silver data chip, scarred and pitted,
with the insignia of a defunct mercenary guild barely visible.
He kept it as a reminder. A touchstone to the man he was, or
perhaps, the man he still feared he might be.

Outside, the vast, crushing pressure of the ocean was a constant,
unseen presence. A silent testament to the fragility of their
existence. Atlantis Prime, a miracle of human ingenuity, was a
bubble of light and life in a world of crushing darkness. A
gilded cage. And he, Lucky Starr, was its most successful, most
celebrated prisoner. He had built his empire on the ashes of the
old world, a legitimate enterprise that washed away the sins of
his past. Or so he told himself. But the hum of the filters
could not drown out the whispers of his own conscience, the ghosts
of deeds done in the shadows, far from the filtered sunlight of
his new reality.

He took a slow, measured breath, composing his features into a mask
of calm, confident assurance. The holographic display flickered,
the face of a UN official materializing in the air before him.
The performance was about to begin.
