From his vantage point, high in Triton Tower, Atlantis Prime
sprawled beneath him, a breathtaking spectacle of light and life.
Gleaming spires, grown from bio-luminescent coral and woven with
iridescent alloys, pierced the deep, their tips disappearing into
the murky twilight above. It was a city that breathed, that lived,
a testament to humanity’s stubborn refusal to fade into extinction.
Transport tubes, glowing with the soft, internal light of the
mag-lev vehicles within, crisscrossed the cityscape like the veins
of a living organism. He could see the faint shapes of submersibles,
from bulky cargo haulers to sleek, personal craft, gliding silently
between the spires.

Schools of genetically engineered fish, their scales flashing with
bioluminescent light, moved in synchronized, shimmering clouds,
illuminating the city’s plazas and thoroughfares. Giant manta rays,
their wingspans as wide as ancient aircraft, glided majestically
past his window, their dark forms silhouetted against the city’s
glow. It was a view that should have inspired awe, a sense of
accomplishment. He had, after all, played a significant role in
its creation. His company, Triton Biotech, had developed the very
coral that formed the city’s foundations.

But as Lucky gazed upon the scene, he felt a profound sense of
detachment. He saw not a home, but a beautiful, intricate cage.
He saw the immense pressure domes, the complex network of energy
shields and defense systems, and knew, with a certainty that chilled
him to the bone, how easily it could all be shattered. The city’s
beauty was a fragile veneer over a terrifying vulnerability. The
vibrant life that pulsed within its walls was utterly dependent on
the technology that kept the crushing weight of the ocean at bay.

His office, in contrast, was a sterile, silent sanctuary. The
air, scrubbed and recycled, held no scent of the sea, no hint of
the teeming life just beyond the acrylic. It was a controlled
environment, a place where he could orchestrate his moves, manipulate
his pawns, and maintain the illusion of control. He was a master
of this game, the game of politics and commerce, of whispered
deals and calculated risks. But it was a game that left him feeling
hollow, a ghost in his own life.

He turned his attention back to the holographic display, the face
of the UN official now fully resolved. The man’s expression was
stern, his eyes filled with a weary skepticism. Lucky knew the
type. A bureaucrat, a man who lived by rules and regulations, who
saw the universe not as a place of infinite possibility, but as a
series of boxes to be ticked. A man who, in Lucky’s experience,
was always the most susceptible to the right kind of pressure.
The kind of pressure that couldn’t be quantified in a spreadsheet.
