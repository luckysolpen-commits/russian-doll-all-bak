# TPMOJIO CAFE — Bootstrap vs. Investor Decision Framework
## "Two Paths. One Destination."

---

## The Question

Do you bootstrap (grow from revenue) or raise investment capital (grow from funding)?

**There's no right answer.** There's only the right answer for *you*, *your timeline*, and *your risk tolerance*.

---

## Bootstrap Path

### What It Looks Like:
- **Year 1:** Build software. Sell licenses. Run pop-up events. $50-100K revenue.
- **Year 2:** Pre-sell memberships. Find first location. Open with software revenue + membership pre-sales. $200-400K revenue.
- **Year 3:** First location profitable. Second location planning. $500K-1M revenue.
- **Year 4-5:** 2-3 locations. Regional brand. $1-2M revenue.
- **Year 5-10:** 5-10 locations. National software platform. $5-10M revenue.

### Pros:
- **Full ownership.** You own 100%. No board. No investors. No pressure to exit.
- **Slow is okay.** You grow at your pace. No quarterly reports. No investor expectations.
- **Culture is protected.** No investor pressure to cut corners, change the concept, or sell out.
- **Failure is survivable.** If a location fails, you close it. No investors to answer to.
- **Exit is optional.** You can run this forever. You don't have to sell.

### Cons:
- **Slow.** 5-10 years to meaningful scale. Competitors can move faster.
- **Capital constrained.** You can only grow as fast as revenue allows.
- **Personal risk.** Your savings, your credit, your time. If it fails, you lose personally.
- **Harder to hire.** Can't offer competitive salaries early. Equity is worth less without investor validation.
- **No network.** Investors bring connections, advice, credibility. You're on your own.

### Who It's For:
- People who value control over speed.
- People who can afford to go slow.
- People who believe the concept is strong enough to grow organically.
- People who don't need a big exit.

### Who It's NOT For:
- People who need to move fast (competitive market, limited window).
- People who can't afford personal risk.
- People who want a big exit (IPO, acquisition).
- People who need investor networks and connections.

## Investor Path

### What It Looks Like:
- **Month 1-6:** Build MVP software. Run pop-up events. Build deck. Raise seed ($500K-1M).
- **Month 6-18:** Open first location. Prove unit economics. Hit $60K+/mo revenue.
- **Month 18-30:** Raise Series A ($2-5M). Open 3-5 locations. Software SaaS at 500+ customers.
- **Month 30-48:** Raise Series B ($10-20M). Open 20+ locations. National brand.
- **Month 48-72:** Exit (acquisition or IPO). $50-200M valuation.

### Pros:
- **Fast.** 3-5 years to meaningful scale. First-mover advantage.
- **Capital available.** You can build, hire, market, expand without waiting for revenue.
- **Network.** Investors bring connections, advice, credibility, hiring pipelines.
- **Personal risk reduced.** Investor money, not your savings. If it fails, you lose time, not life savings.
- **Exit potential.** $50-200M exit is possible with investor backing.

### Cons:
- **Loss of control.** Board of directors. Investor expectations. Quarterly reports. Pressure to grow fast.
- **Dilution.** You own 10-30% at exit, not 100%.
- **Culture risk.** Investors may push for changes: cut community programming, increase prices, change the concept.
- **Exit pressure.** Investors need a return. You'll be pushed to sell or IPO, whether you want to or not.
- **Failure is public.** Investor-backed failures are visible. Personal failures are private.

### Who It's For:
- People who value speed over control.
- People who can't afford personal risk.
- People who want a big exit.
- People who need investor networks and connections.
- People who believe the market window is limited (competitors are coming).

### Who It's NOT For:
- People who value control over speed.
- People who want to run this forever.
- People who don't want a board of directors.
- People who believe the concept can grow organically.

## The Hybrid Path (Recommended)

**Bootstrap Phase 0-1. Raise investment at Phase 2.**

### How It Works:
1. **Phase 0 (Months 1-6):** Bootstrap. Build software. Build community. Run pop-ups. $5-15K cost.
2. **Phase 1 (Months 6-18):** Bootstrap. Open first location. Fund from software revenue + membership pre-sales + small business loan. $200-280K cost.
3. **Phase 2 (Months 18-30):** **Raise seed round ($500K-1M).** Prove the model is replicable. Open 2-3 more locations.
4. **Phase 3-4 (Months 30-72):** Raise Series A/B if growth is strong. Or continue bootstrapping if revenue supports it.

### Why This Works:
- **You prove the concept before asking for money.** Investors love traction. You'll have it.
- **You negotiate from strength.** "We're profitable at one location. We're opening three more. Want in?"
- **You keep more equity.** Traction = higher valuation = less dilution.
- **You can walk away.** If investors don't like your terms, you don't need them. You're already profitable.
- **You protect the culture.** You've already built the community. Investors can't change that.

### The Numbers:
| Phase | Funding Source | Amount | Your Ownership |
|-------|---------------|--------|---------------|
| Phase 0 | Personal | $10K | 100% |
| Phase 1 | Revenue + Loan | $280K | 100% |
| Phase 2 | Seed Round | $500K-1M | 80-90% |
| Phase 3 | Series A | $2-5M | 60-75% |
| Phase 4 | Series B | $10-20M | 40-60% |
| **Exit** | **Acquisition** | **$50-200M** | **$20-120M to you** |

**Compare to pure bootstrap:**
| Phase | Funding Source | Amount | Your Ownership |
|-------|---------------|--------|---------------|
| Phase 0 | Personal | $10K | 100% |
| Phase 1 | Revenue + Loan | $280K | 100% |
| Phase 2 | Revenue + Loan | $250K | 100% |
| Phase 3 | Revenue + Loan | $1M | 100% |
| Phase 4 | Revenue + Loan | $5M | 100% |
| **Year 10 Revenue** | | **$5-10M/yr** | **$5-10M/yr to you** |

**Bootstrap = $5-10M/year forever. Investor path = $20-120M one-time exit.**

## When to Raise (If You Choose To)

### Good Times to Raise:
- ✅ After Phase 1 is profitable (proven unit economics).
- ✅ After you have 300+ members (proven demand).
- ✅ After software has 1,000+ users (proven product).
- ✅ After you've documented the playbook (proven replicability).
- ✅ When you have multiple location offers (proven desirability).

### Bad Times to Raise:
- ❌ Before you have a location (no traction).
- ❌ Before you have members (no demand).
- ❌ Before you have software (no product).
- ❌ When you're desperate (bad terms).
- ❌ When you don't need the money (why dilute?).

## What Investors Will Ask

### The Questions:
1. **"What's your unit economics?"** → Show them Location 1's P&L.
2. **"How many members do you have?"** → Show them the membership dashboard.
3. **"What's your software revenue?"** → Show them the SaaS metrics.
4. **"How replicable is this?"** → Show them the playbook.
5. **"What's your moat?"** → Community, IP, AI, physical locations.
6. **"Why you?"** → You have the IP, the content, the community, the vision.
7. **"What's the exit?"** → Acquisition by Barnes & Noble, GameStop, or software company.
8. **"How much do you need?"** → $500K-1M for 3-5 locations.
9. **"What will you do with it?"** → Build locations, hire team, grow software.
10. **"When will you be profitable?"** → Each location is profitable within 12 months. Portfolio is profitable within 24 months.

### The Deck:
1. Problem (no third place for nerds)
2. Solution (TPMOJIO Cafe)
3. Market size ($50B+ cafe + gaming + community market)
4. Product (cafe + games + tech + community + AI)
5. Traction (members, revenue, software users, events)
6. Business model (memberships, F&B, retail, software, events)
7. Competition (B&N, GameStop, board game cafes — and why we're different)
8. Team (you + co-founders + advisors)
9. Financials (unit economics, projections, use of funds)
10. Ask ($500K-1M for X% of company)

## The Honest Truth About Investors

**Most investors won't understand this concept.** It's too unusual. Cafe + gaming + tech + community + AI + open source IP. They'll try to fit it into a box. It doesn't fit.

**The right investor will get it immediately.** They'll see the community, the IP, the software, the physical locations, the AI. They'll understand the moat. They'll want in.

**Finding the right investor takes time.** Talk to 50. Get 5 term sheets. Pick the one that understands the vision.

**Don't take money from investors who want to change the concept.** If they say "cut the community programming," "increase prices," "focus on retail," walk away. They don't get it.

**Your best investors are:**
- People who love the TPMOJIO universe (they're fans).
- People who've built community businesses before (they've done it).
- People who understand open source / Creative Commons (they get the model).
- People who are patient (they understand this is a 5-10 year business).

## Your Personal Situation

You said: *"I've never had a successful business before."*

**That's okay.** Here's what to do:

1. **Start with Phase 0.** It's low risk. $5-15K. If it fails, you lose little.
2. **Find a co-founder who's done this before.** Someone who's opened a cafe, a game store, or a community space. You bring the IP and vision. They bring the operations.
3. **Talk to 100 potential members.** If they won't pre-pay, the concept isn't ready. If they will, you have validation.
4. **Track one metric:** Monthly recurring revenue (memberships + software). Everything else is noise.
5. **Be okay with slow.** This is a 5-year business. Not a 1-year business. Not a 6-month business. Five years.

**If you bootstrap:** You'll own 100%. You'll grow slowly. You'll build something real. You'll be proud of it.

**If you raise:** You'll own 40-60%. You'll grow fast. You'll build something big. You'll be proud of it.

**Either way, you win.** The question is: what kind of win do you want?
