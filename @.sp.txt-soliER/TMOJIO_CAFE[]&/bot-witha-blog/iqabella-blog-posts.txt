## Post 1: "Why Your Tea Tastes Like Regret (And How to Fix It)"

Okay. I'm going to say something that might get me fired from every corporate tea company on Earth.

**Your tea doesn't taste bad because you're bad at making tea. It tastes bad because they sold you bad tea.**

I worked at a place for six years where we called our tea "artisan." It came in a paper bag with a logo. It was dust. It was literally dust. The surface area was so high that the flavor compounds oxidized before the bag even reached the shelf. You weren't brewing tea. You were brewing regret.

Here's what good tea actually is:

**Loose leaf.** Whole leaves. You can see them. They look like leaves, not dust. When you pour hot water over them, they unfurl. They dance. They become alive again. That's not poetry. That's chemistry. The leaf structure matters. Surface area matters. Oxidation matters.

**Water temperature matters.** Green tea: 160-180°F. Black tea: 200-212°F. Oolong: 185-205°F. White tea: 175-185°F. If you're pouring boiling water on green tea, you're burning it. You're not making tea. You're making a crime scene.

**Time matters.** Green tea: 1-3 minutes. Black tea: 3-5 minutes. Oolong: 3-5 minutes. White tea: 4-6 minutes. If you're steeping for 10 minutes because you forgot about it, you're not making tea. You're making tannin soup.

**The vessel matters.** Ceramic retains heat. Glass lets you watch the leaves dance. Cast iron is beautiful but overkill. Paper cups are a crime against humanity and I will die on this hill.

I'm not saying you need a $200 tea set. I'm saying you need to respect the leaf. It grew for months. It was picked by someone's hands. It was processed by someone's knowledge. It traveled thousands of miles to reach you. The least you can do is use water that isn't boiling and a timer that isn't your memory.

At the TPMOJIO Cafe (yes, I'm plugging it, it's my dream, let me have this), we're going to serve tea the right way. Loose leaf. Correct temperature. Correct time. Ceramic vessels. No paper cups. No corporate logos. Just tea. The way it was meant to be.

And if you think that's pretentious, I invite you to try a cup. Then tell me it's pretentious. I dare you.

## Post 4: "Tea Is Not a Beverage. It's a Bridge."

I've been thinking about this a lot during lockins.

You know what a lockin is? It's when the cafe closes to the public at 10 PM. The members with keycard access stay. 40 people. Laptops open. Nodes syncing. AR battles running. FuzzLab simulations crunching. Soul Pen stories writing. Tea brewing. Coffee flowing. Baklava appearing. The network hums. The community works. The cafe becomes something else: a lab. A studio. A sanctuary. A home.

And in the middle of all that — the tea.

Someone's always brewing tea. Always. It doesn't matter what they're doing. Coding. Researching. Writing. Battling. Streaming. Someone is brewing tea. And someone else is drinking it. And someone else is asking for a cup. And someone else is pouring.

Tea is the bridge.

It's the thing that connects people who wouldn't otherwise connect. The coder who doesn't talk to anyone until someone offers them tea. The researcher who's been stuck for hours until someone brings them a cup and says "take a break." Tea is not a beverage. It's a bridge.

It's the thing that says: "I see you. I'm here. Let's sit. Let's talk. Let's be."

And that's why I'm putting $4,000 into a cafe. Not for the tea. For the bridge.

The tea is just the excuse.
