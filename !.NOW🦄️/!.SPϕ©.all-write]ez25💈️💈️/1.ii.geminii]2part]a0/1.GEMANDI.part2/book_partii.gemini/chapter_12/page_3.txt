Lucky stared at him, disbelief warring with a growing sense
of horror. His mind reeled, struggling to process the monstrous
confession. The sheer audacity, the cold, calculated cruelty
of it, was almost beyond comprehension. "You... you orchestrated
the apocalypse?" he choked out, his voice hoarse with a
mixture of rage and a profound sense of betrayal. The
world he had fought so hard to survive, the countless
lives lost, the endless suffering – all of it, a deliberate
act by this man, sitting calmly before them, as if discussing
a business deal. The thought was sickening, a bitter taste
in his mouth.

"Not the apocalypse itself, no," The Architect corrected,
his voice almost playful, a condescending chuckle escaping
his lips. "That was merely a catalyst. A necessary reset.
Humanity, my dear Dr. Lucky, was stagnating, consuming
itself with petty squabbles and unsustainable growth.
It needed... a push. A new direction. A grand, global
recalibration." He gestured vaguely with a manicured hand,
as if sweeping away the remnants of a failed experiment.
His eyes, though still piercing, held a disturbing glint
of self-righteousness, a conviction that he was not a
destroyer, but a savior, albeit a brutal one. Astra felt
a cold fury building within her, a scientific mind revolted
by such a twisted justification for mass murder.

"And that direction was endless suffering?" Astra demanded,
her voice trembling with a barely suppressed rage, her
hands clenching into fists at her sides. "Millions dead!
The world in ruins! Families torn apart! Is that your
'new direction'? Is that your 'progress'?" Her scientific
dispassion had completely evaporated, replaced by a raw,
visceral anger. She thought of the countless hours she
had spent trying to alleviate suffering, to find solutions,
only to discover that the source of all their misery was
sitting right in front of her, calmly justifying his atrocities.

"A small price to pay for progress, Dr. Astra," The Architect
said, shrugging dismissively, as if discussing a minor
inconvenience. "You see, I possess the cure." He pressed
a button on his sleek, minimalist desk, and the holographic
display above him flickered to life, resolving into a complex
molecular structure, a swirling, intricate dance of atoms
and bonds. It was beautiful, in a terrifying, abstract
way, a testament to a scientific genius twisted by madness.

"This," he announced, his voice filled with a chilling
pride, a triumphant flourish, "is the Mutagen. The substance
that can be engineered into a virus to wipe out the Carcazoids.
And I intend to sell it. To the highest bidder. To those
who are worthy of rebuilding the world. My world." His
gaze swept over them, a possessive gleam in his eyes, as
if they were merely pawns in his grand scheme. The air
in the chamber grew heavy, charged with the weight of his
monstrous ambition. Lucky felt a wave of nausea, a profound
sense of disgust at the sheer depravity of the man before
them. He wasn't just a madman; he was a capitalist of
chaos, profiting from the very destruction he had orchestrated.
