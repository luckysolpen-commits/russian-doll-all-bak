The helicopter’s skids touched down on the grated metal of
the helipad with a final, jarring shudder. The whine of the
turbines began to spool down, but the oppressive humidity of the
African jungle rushed in to fill the void, a tangible
presence that clung to their skin like a second layer of
clothing. Chara and Giana moved with the fluid economy of
motion that came from years of practice, unclipping their harnesses and
grabbing their gear in a single, seamless sequence. They stepped out
onto the platform, the setting sun casting their long shadows
across the rusting deck of the oil strut. A lone figure
awaited them, a man whose form was rendered indistinct by the
brilliant glare of the sun at his back. He was a
man of average height and build, clad in sterile, non-descript
fatigues that bore no insignia, no mark of allegiance. He was
a blank slate, a ghost in a world of shadows.

As they approached, the man stepped forward, his features resolving out
of the glare. He had a face that was instantly forgettable,
a collection of features that seemed designed to slide from memory.
His eyes, however, were another matter. They were a pale,
washed-out blue, and they held a chilling emptiness, the look
of a man who had seen too much and felt too
little. “Welcome to Mothra,” he said, his voice a dry,
rasping whisper that seemed to be swallowed by the vastness of
the jungle surrounding them. “I’m ANON. Your liaison.” He offered
no handshake, no further pleasantries. He simply turned and began
to walk, expecting them to follow.

He led them away from the helipad, his boots clanging a
rhythmic dirge on the metal gangway. The oil strut was a
relic, a skeletal monument to a forgotten age of fossil
fuels, now repurposed as a clandestine outpost on the fringes of
civilization. It was a world unto itself, a self-contained ecosystem
of spies and soldiers, all living under a veil of
secrecy and paranoia. ANON guided them to a standard shipping
container, its corrugated steel sides painted a dull, unassuming gray.
A keypad was mounted next to the door, and he
tapped in a code with practiced ease. The door hissed
open, revealing an interior that was a stark contrast to the
decaying exterior. The container had been converted into a high-tech
briefing room, the air humming with the sound of servers
and cooled by a powerful climate control system. The walls
were lined with monitors, displaying satellite imagery, data streams, and
encrypted communications.

ANON gestured to a holographic projector in the center of the
room. With a tap on a console, it flickered to
life, projecting a three-dimensional, rotating image of a sprawling facility.
“Your target,” he announced, his voice devoid of inflection. “A
rogue mech development and manufacturing plant, hidden deep in the
jungle. It’s run by a PMC, the kind of soldiers
who salute the highest bidder. They’re well-armed, well-trained, and have
no qualms about collateral damage.” He zoomed in on a
central, heavily fortified building within the complex. “This is the
data core. Your objective is to infiltrate the facility, access
that core, and extract all of their operational data. This
is a stealth mission. You will have no support. You will
leave no trace. You are ghosts. Am I understood?” Chara
and Giana exchanged a single, knowing glance. The mission was
a suicide run, a one-way ticket into the heart of
darkness. But it was the job. And they were the
ones to do it.
