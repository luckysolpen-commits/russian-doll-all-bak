The holographic image of the facility pulsed with a soft,
ethereal light, casting dancing shadows on the walls of the
container. Chara’s eyes traced the intricate lines of the complex,
her mind a whirlwind of calculations and tactical assessments. She
absorbed every detail, every potential entry point, every blind spot,
every choke point. The facility was a puzzle, a complex and
deadly equation, and she was the variable that would solve
it. Giana, ever the pragmatist, broke the silence, her voice
sharp and focused. “What’s the opposition’s strength? Numbers, hardware, patrol
patterns?”

ANON’s pale eyes flickered towards her, a flicker of something
that might have been respect in their depths. “The PMC
is a company-sized element, approximately one hundred and twenty strong.
They’re equipped with standard infantry weapons, but we’ve also detected
the presence of heavy machine gun emplacements and at least
two light armored vehicles. Their patrol patterns are regular, but
they’re also known for their random, unscheduled sweeps. They’re disciplined,
but they’re also predictable. Their weakness is their reliance on
technology. They have a state-of-the-art surveillance system, but it’s
centralized. Take out the command and control center, and you’ll
be invisible.”

He manipulated the holographic image, highlighting a small, unassuming building
on the perimeter of the facility. “This is the C&C
center. It’s your first objective. Neutralize it, and you’ll have
free rein of the facility. But be warned, it’s a hard
target. It’s reinforced, and it’s heavily guarded.”

Chara and Giana exchanged another look. The mission was getting
more complicated by the second, the odds stacking up against
them. But they were professionals, and they had faced worse.
They had a saying, a mantra that had seen them
through a hundred impossible situations: “The only easy day was
yesterday.”

ANON handed them each a small, encrypted datapad. “This contains
all the intel we have on the facility, including the
schematics, the patrol routes, and the profiles of the key
personnel. Memorize it, then wipe it. There can be no
trace of our involvement.” He then produced a small, cylindrical
device, no bigger than a cigar. “This is a high-frequency
emitter. It will disable their communications for a short period
of time. Use it wisely. You’ll only have one shot.”

He turned to leave, his part in the briefing apparently
over. “One more thing,” he said, his hand on the
door. “The jungle is as much of an enemy as
the PMC. It’s full of things that want to kill
you. Watch your step.” The door hissed shut, leaving them
alone in the cool, sterile silence of the container. The
weight of the mission settled upon them, a tangible thing,
a physical presence in the room. They were on their
own, two women against a small army, in a place
where the very environment was hostile. The odds were long,
the risks were high, but the stakes were even higher.
The fate of the world, or at least a small part
of it, rested on their shoulders. It was a familiar
feeling, a burden they had carried many times before. And
as always, they would bear it without complaint, without hesitation.
They were Chara and Giana. And this is what they did.
