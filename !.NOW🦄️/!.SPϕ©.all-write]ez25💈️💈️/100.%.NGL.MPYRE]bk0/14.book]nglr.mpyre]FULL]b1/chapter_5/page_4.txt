The turning point came in the form of a child. An Apexian fledgling who had been born after the song had begun, a child who had never known a single moment of sadness, of fear, of doubt. He was a perfect, beautiful, and utterly empty vessel. He had no personality, no desires, no dreams of his own. He was a mirror, reflecting the blissful, unthinking contentment of the world around him. And he was dying. His body was healthy, his mind was at peace, but his soul, his very life force, was slowly, surely, fading away.

Luna, her face a mask of grim, horrified understanding, realized the terrible truth. The song was not just a pacifier; it was a poison. It was a slow-acting neurotoxin that was erasing the very capacity for individual thought, for personal growth, for the messy, beautiful, and necessary struggle that is the essence of life. The Apexians were not just being pacified; they were being erased. Their utopia was not a paradise; it was a beautiful, gilded tomb.

The Anglers knew they had to act, and act quickly. Their campaign of subtle disruption was not enough. They had to shatter the illusion, to force the Apexians to see the terrible truth of their perfect, beautiful, and soul-crushing dream. They had to find the source of the song, the psychic parasite that was feeding on the soul of a species, and they had to destroy it. It was a desperate, dangerous, and utterly necessary plan. They had to become the serpent in the garden, the bearers of a terrible, liberating truth. They had to teach a world of angels how to fall.
