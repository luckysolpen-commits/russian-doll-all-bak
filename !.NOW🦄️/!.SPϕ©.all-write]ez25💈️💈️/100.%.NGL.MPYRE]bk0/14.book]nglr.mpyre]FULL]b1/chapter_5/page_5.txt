The source of the song was not a machine, or a creature, but a place. A vast, crystalline cathedral at the heart of the Apexian capital city. It was a place of pilgrimage, a place where the Apexians would go to bask in the pure, unfiltered radiance of the song of hope. It was the focal point of their blissful, unthinking contentment, the heart of their beautiful, gilded tomb. And it was guarded, not by soldiers, but by the most devout, the most blissfully unaware, the most hopelessly lost of the Apexian people.

The Anglers knew they could not fight their way through the sea of serene, smiling faces. To do so would be to become the monsters they were fighting. They had to find another way. It was Jureth, the young cartographer, who provided the answer. He had been studying the cathedral, not as a fortress, but as a piece of music. He had analyzed the song of hope, its rhythms, its harmonies, its subliminal messages. And he had found a flaw. A single, dissonant note that was hidden deep within the symphony of blissful contentment.

It was a note of pure, unadulterated fear. A tiny, almost imperceptible echo of the psychic parasite’s own terror of being discovered, of being starved, of being alone. It was a single, tiny crack in the perfect, beautiful, and soul-crushing dream. And Jureth knew how to amplify it. He had created a counter-song, a melody of his own, a song of doubt, of struggle, of the messy, beautiful, and necessary imperfection of life. It was a song that would not shatter the dream, but would introduce a new, discordant note into the symphony. It was a song that would give the Apexians a choice.
