The Anglers’ first challenge was one of simple communication. How do you convince a people they are in danger when they are convinced they are in paradise? Their initial attempts at contact were met with a serene, uncomprehending indifference. The Apexians listened to their warnings with polite, smiling faces, their minds so saturated with the song of hope that the words simply washed over them, meaningless ripples on a sea of blissful contentment. They were a people who had forgotten how to fear, how to question, how to even conceive of the possibility that something might be wrong.

It was Zara who finally broke through the wall of blissful apathy. She didn’t try to warn them, to scare them. Instead, she used her empathic abilities to sing a different song. It was a quiet, simple melody, a song of a single, bittersweet memory from her own past – the memory of a scraped knee, of a childhood pet that had died, of a first, clumsy, and ultimately heartbreaking crush. It was a song of small, ordinary, and beautifully imperfect moments. It was a song of life.

The effect was subtle, but profound. A few of the Apexians, their minds momentarily jarred by this strange, new, and unfamiliar emotion, faltered in their graceful flight. A flicker of confusion, of curiosity, appeared in their serene, untroubled eyes. They had heard a new note in the symphony of their perfect world, a note that was both sad and beautiful, a note that hinted at a reality beyond their blissful, unthinking contentment. It was a start. A tiny crack in the crystal cage of their perfect, beautiful, and soul-crushing dream.
