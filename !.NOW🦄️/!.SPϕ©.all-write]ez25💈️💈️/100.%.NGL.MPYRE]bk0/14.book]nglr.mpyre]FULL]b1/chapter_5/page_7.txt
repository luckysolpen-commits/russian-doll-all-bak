The psychic parasite that had taken root in the heart of the cathedral was not a subtle creature. It was a being of pure, unadulterated optimism, and it did not tolerate dissent. As Jureth’s counter-song began to spread, as the seeds of doubt began to take root in the minds of the Apexians, the parasite fought back. The song of hope intensified, its blissful, unthinking contentment becoming a crushing, suffocating weight. It was a battle of ideas, a war of songs, and the fate of a civilization hung in the balance.

The Anglers stood their ground, their minds shielded by Zara’s empathic abilities, their wills fortified by Jax’s unshakeable resolve. They were a small island of doubt in a sea of blissful contentment, a single, defiant note of imperfection in a symphony of soul-crushing perfection. But they were not alone. The Apexians, their minds awakened by Jureth’s counter-song, were beginning to choose. Some clung to the familiar comfort of the song of hope, their faces serene, their eyes closed, their minds lost in a beautiful, gilded dream. But others, their curiosity piqued, their souls stirred by the promise of a different kind of hope, began to hum along with Jureth’s song.

The cathedral itself began to react to the growing dissonance. The pure, white light of its crystalline walls began to flicker, to pulse with a sickly, yellow light. The psychic parasite was being starved, its food source, the blissful, unthinking contentment of the Apexians, tainted by the poison of doubt. It was a slow, agonizing death, a death of a thousand tiny questions, a thousand small acts of intellectual defiance.
