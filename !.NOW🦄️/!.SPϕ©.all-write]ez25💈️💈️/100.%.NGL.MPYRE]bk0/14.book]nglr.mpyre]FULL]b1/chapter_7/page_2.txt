The Anglers’ attempts to reason with the Pyrians were met with a silent, uncomprehending indifference. They were a bug in the system, a glitch in the perfect, orderly code of their crystalline universe. The Pyrians did not try to destroy them, but to correct them, to absorb them into the silent, harmonious whole. They were subjected to a constant, low-level psychic pressure, a gentle, insistent pull towards the silent, beautiful, and utterly lifeless order of the crystalline consciousness.

It was a battle of attrition, a slow, grinding war against an enemy that was not a monster, but a concept. The Anglers’ minds, their very sense of self, began to fray at the edges. They found themselves losing their individuality, their messy, chaotic emotions being smoothed away, replaced by a cool, calm, and utterly terrifying sense of peace. They were being erased, not with a bang, but with a whisper.

It was Jack, the mechanic, the man of action, who finally broke the stalemate. He was not a philosopher, or a mystic, or a scientist. He was a man who understood machines, and he saw the Pyrian consciousness for what it was: a perfect, beautiful, and utterly fragile machine. And he knew how to break it. He created a virus, a piece of code that was not designed to destroy, but to disrupt, to introduce a single, tiny element of chaos into the perfect, orderly system.

The virus was a simple one: a single, unanswerable question. A question that had no logical answer, no place in the perfect, orderly universe of the Pyrians. The question was: *Why?*
