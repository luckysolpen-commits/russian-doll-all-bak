The docking procedure was executed with a tense, surgical precision. The
‘Stargazer’s’ airlock connected with the XVS outpost’s with a soft
hiss, a sound that was unnervingly loud in the profound silence.
Jax, Hana, Jack, and Luna prepared for boarding, their faces grim.
Kael would remain on the bridge, monitoring their vital signs and the
ship’s sensors. Zara would stay with him, her empathic senses a
vital early-warning system against any psychic attack. Kato, as always,
had already disappeared, his task to scout the station through its
ventilation systems and maintenance shafts, a ghost in the machine.

“Comms check,” Jax’s voice crackled in their ears, a grounding presence.
“Stay sharp. We don’t know what we’re walking into. Standard sweep
and clear formation. Report anything, and I mean anything, out of
the ordinary.” The airlock door slid open, revealing a corridor that
was immaculate, sterile, and utterly normal. The lights were on, the
air was breathable, and a faint, pleasant hum of the station’s
life support system filled the air. There were no signs of a
struggle, no bodies, no blood. It was this very normality that
set their teeth on edge.

They moved through the silent corridors like phantoms, their boots barely
making a sound on the polished deck plates. They cleared the
mess hall, the crew quarters, the science labs. Each room was
the same: empty, orderly, and unnervingly quiet. It was as if
the crew had simply vanished into thin air. Finally, they reached
the station’s central hub, a large, circular room with a panoramic
view of the swirling, pastel clouds of Aether. And it was
here that they found the crew.

They were all there, all twenty of them. They were not dead,
not injured, not even restrained. They were simply standing or sitting
at their posts, their eyes open but unfocused, their faces a
chilling tableau of intense, private emotion. Some wore expressions of
rapturous bliss, their lips curved into serene smiles. Others were
contorted in masks of pure terror, their hands clenched, their bodies
rigid with fear. A few wept silently, tears streaming down their
cheeks. Each crew member was surrounded by a faint, shimmering aura,
a personal bubble of light that pulsed in time with their unseen
dreams.

Luna approached one of the scientists, a young woman whose face was
alight with ecstatic joy. She raised her tricorder, its soft whirring
the only sound in the room. “Their brain activity is off the
charts,” she whispered, her voice a mixture of scientific fascination and
dread. “They’re not comatose. They’re… experiencing something. A hyper-realistic, shared
-hallucination. They’re trapped in a dream.” At that moment, Kato’s voice
materialized in their comms, a low, urgent whisper from the shadows.
“The dream is the trap,” he said. “But it’s also the source.
There’s a focal point, a psychic broadcast emanating from the planet
below. It’s calling to them. It’s calling to all of us.”
