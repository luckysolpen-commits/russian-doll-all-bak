The first crack in the carefully constructed facades of the
guests appeared, as it so often does, in a moment
of weakness, a moment of vulnerability, a moment when
the mask slipped and the true face was revealed.
For Seraphina Monet, that moment came in the dead
of night, in the form of a hushed, frantic
phone call. Madame Dupont, on her way to the
kitchen for a glass of warm milk, heard the
muffled sounds of a desperate, pleading voice coming from
the Rose Room. She paused, her hand on the
door, her heart filled with a mixture of concern
and curiosity. She knew she shouldn't listen, that it
was a violation of her guest's privacy, but she
also knew that something was terribly wrong.

"He can't know I'm here," Seraphina whispered, her voice
a mixture of fear and desperation. "I did everything
you told me to. I took a different name.
I paid in cash. I didn't tell a soul."
There was a pause, and then, her voice dropped
to a barely audible whisper. "If he finds me,
he'll kill me." The words hung in the air,
a chilling confession that sent a shiver down Madame
Dupont's spine. Seraphina was not just hiding from an
obsessed fan; she was hiding from a man who
wanted her dead.

For Silas Blackwood, the crack appeared in the form
of a small, mud-stained shovel that Madame Dupont found
hidden in the back of his closet. It was
a simple, ordinary shovel, the kind that could be
bought at any hardware store. But it was the
mud on the shovel that intrigued her, a rich,
dark soil that was not from her garden. It
was the soil of a freshly dug grave, the
soil of a secret that was buried deep in
the earth. And as she looked at the shovel,
she had a sudden, chilling thought. What if Silas
Blackwood was not searching for a treasure? What if
he was searching for a body?

For Mr. Smith, the crack appeared in the form
of a single, tear-stained page from a leather-bound journal.
Madame Dupont found it on the floor of the
library, a page that had been ripped out in
a moment of anger or despair. On it was
a single, cryptic sentence: "I saw her again today,
in the garden, tending to the roses. She has
not aged a day. And she still does not
know who I am." The words were a cry
of anguish, a confession of a love that had
been lost, a secret that had been kept for
years. And as Madame Dupont read the words, she
knew that Mr. Smith was not just a reclusive
author; he was a man in love with a
ghost, a man who had come to the Chateau
Tivoli to be close to the woman he had
lost, the woman who still haunted his dreams.

The secrets were beginning to unfurl, one by one,
each one more shocking, more dangerous, than the last.
The Chateau Tivoli was no longer just a B&B;
it was a confessional, a house of secrets, a
place where the past and the present were on
a collision course. And as the fog rolled in
from the bay, blanketing the city in a thick,
gray shroud, Madame Dupont knew that the game had
just begun.
