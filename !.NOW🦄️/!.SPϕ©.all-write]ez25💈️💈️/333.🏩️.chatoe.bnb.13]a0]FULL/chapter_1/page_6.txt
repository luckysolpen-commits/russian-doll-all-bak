The doorbell, a grand, old-fashioned thing with a deep,
resonant chime, seemed to echo the anticipation that filled
the air. Madame Dupont, her hand resting lightly on
the ornate brass doorknob, took a final, steadying breath.
She loved this moment, this threshold between the known
and the unknown, the ordinary and the extraordinary. It
was a moment pregnant with possibility, a moment where
anything could happen. And at the Chateau Tivoli, it
often did.

She opened the door, and there she was. The
first guest. A woman who was not just a
woman, but a phenomenon. Seraphina Monet. The actress.
The star. The legend. She stood on the doorstep,
a vision in a cloud of white silk and
oversized sunglasses, her famous face a mask of cool,
composed beauty. But there was a fragility about her,
a vulnerability that her glamorous facade couldn't quite conceal.
Her eyes, when she finally removed the sunglasses, were
the color of a stormy sea, and they held
a universe of secrets, a galaxy of untold stories.

"Welcome to the Chateau Tivoli," Madame Dupont said, her
voice a warm and welcoming embrace. "We are so
honored to have you with us." Seraphina offered a
small, almost imperceptible, smile, a flicker of warmth in
the cool, blue depths of her eyes. "Thank you,"
she said, her voice a low, husky whisper that
was as famous as her face. "I was told
I would find peace here." "We will do our
very best to see that you do," Madame Dupont
replied, her words a solemn promise, a sacred vow.

She led the actress into the foyer, the grand
staircase rising before them like a promise of a
journey to another world. Seraphina looked around, her
eyes taking in the rich, dark wood, the stained-glass
windows, the grinning gargoyle on the newel post. A
small, genuine smile touched her lips. "It's even more
beautiful than the pictures," she said, her voice filled
with a sense of wonder. "It's a house with
a soul." "It is indeed," Madame Dupont replied, her
heart swelling with pride. "And it is a house
that knows how to keep a secret."

The words hung in the air, a silent understanding
passing between the two women. Seraphina was not just
looking for a beautiful house; she was looking for
a sanctuary, a fortress, a place where she could
be safe from the prying eyes of the world,
from the relentless glare of the spotlight, from the
demons that haunted her. And in Madame Dupont, she
saw not just a proprietor, but a kindred spirit,
a woman who understood the power of secrets, the
importance of discretion, the value of a safe harbor
in a stormy sea.

As Madame Dupont led Seraphina to the Rose Room,
the most luxurious suite in the house, she knew
that this was not just another guest. This was
a woman on the run, a woman with a
story to tell, a woman who would change the
course of the Chateau Tivoli in ways that she
could not yet imagine. The first player had arrived.
The game was afoot.
