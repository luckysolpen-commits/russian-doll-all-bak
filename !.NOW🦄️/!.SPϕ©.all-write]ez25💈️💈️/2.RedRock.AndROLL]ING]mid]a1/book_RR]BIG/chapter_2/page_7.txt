As she walked through the gleaming, sterile corridors of
Atlantis Prime, Gem’s mind was a whirlwind of activity.
Her cybernetic eye, no longer scanning for threats, was now
-projecting data streams into her field of vision. Encrypted
-comm channels, corporate schematics, personnel files. She
was already gathering intelligence, laying the groundwork
for the operation to come.

She accessed a secure, back-channel comm line, one that
-bypassed the UN’s official networks. It was a relic from
her past, a tool of a trade she had tried to leave behind.
But old habits, like old skills, never truly died. They
just lay dormant, waiting for the right moment to be
-reawakened.

She sent a single, encrypted message to a private comm
-unit she knew belonged to Lucky Starr. It was a simple
-message, just a string of numbers and symbols. A code
they had used years ago, during their time as mercenaries.
It was a message that would be meaningless to anyone else,
but to Lucky, it would be a ghost from his past, a whisper
from a life he had tried so hard to forget.

She knew it was a gamble. He might ignore it. He might
-have even forgotten the code. But she was counting on his
-curiosity, his paranoia, his inability to resist a puzzle.
She was planting a seed, a seed of doubt and intrigue, in
the carefully cultivated garden of his new life.

Next, she accessed the mercenary guild’s private network.
It was a dark corner of the net, a place where contracts
were posted, allegiances were forged, and betrayals were
-commonplace. She searched for Astra’s call sign, a stylized
-phoenix rising from the flames. She found it, attached to
a series of low-level, high-risk contracts. Escort duty,
-bodyguard work. The kind of jobs you took when you were
-desperate, when the real wars had dried up.

She sent another encrypted message, this one more direct.
“Zara. Mutagen. High-stakes. You in?” She knew Astra
-wouldn’t be able to resist. The mention of Zara’s name
-alone would be enough to get her attention. The promise
of a real fight, a chance to take down a corporate tyrant,
-would be irresistible.

She had cast her lines. Now, all she could do was wait.
She felt a familiar thrill, a surge of adrenaline that she
-hadn’t felt in years. It was the thrill of the game, the
-dance on the edge of a knife. She had been living in the
-shadows for too long, playing by the rules of a society
she didn’t fully trust. Now, she was back in her element.
