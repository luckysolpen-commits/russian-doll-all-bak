Director Kaito’s face was pale, his carefully constructed composure
shattered. He swallowed hard, his Adam’s apple bobbing in his throat.
He tried to maintain his official tone, but his voice was strained,
a mere shadow of its former authority.

“A… a valid point, Mr. Starr,” he stammered, his eyes darting around
as if searching for an escape from the horrifying images Lucky had
painted. “The… the security of our food supply is, of course, of
paramount importance. Your… your proposal will be reviewed with
renewed urgency. We will… we will be in touch. Within the week.”

The holographic call dissolved, the image of the flustered Director
vanishing into thin air, leaving Lucky alone in the silent hum of
his office. The sudden emptiness was jarring. The performance was
over, the adrenaline began to fade, and he was left with the cold,
hard reality of what he had just done.

He had manipulated a good man, a man trying to do his job, by
exploiting his deepest fears. He had played on the trauma of a
generation to get what he wanted. It was a victory, but it tasted
like ash in his mouth.

The hum of the hydro-filters, usually a soothing presence, now
seemed loud, oppressive. The shimmering light filtering through the
water felt cold, clinical. He was a master of this game, but the
cost of playing was a piece of his own soul. A piece he had been
selling off for years, until he wasn’t sure how much was left.

He pushed himself away from the desk, the motion stiff, weary.
The weight of his own facade, the constant performance of being
Lucky Starr, the respectable entrepreneur, was exhausting. He walked
across the room, his footsteps echoing in the silence. He needed a
drink. Not the expensive, synthetic wine he served to his guests,
but something real, something that would burn away the lingering
taste of his own deception.

He was a pillar of the community, a respected visionary, a savior
of humanity. He was also a liar, a manipulator, a man haunted by
the ghosts of his past. The two identities were becoming increasingly
difficult to reconcile. He was living a double life, and the strain
was beginning to show, not on his face, but in the quiet, desperate
moments when the performance was over and he was alone with his
thoughts. The silence of his office was no longer a sanctuary; it
was a cage, and the only prisoner was himself.
