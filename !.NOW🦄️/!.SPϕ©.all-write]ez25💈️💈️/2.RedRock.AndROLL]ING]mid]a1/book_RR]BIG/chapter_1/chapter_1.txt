
Chapter 1: The Entrepreneur's Facade

The hum of the hydro-filters was a constant, soothing lullaby in Lucky Starr’s
office, a stark contrast to the chaotic symphony of his past. Sunlight,
filtered through layers of reinforced acrylic and the shimmering turquoise of
the Atlantic, painted shifting patterns across his polished desk. From this
vantage point, high in Triton Tower, Atlantis Prime sprawled beneath him, a
miracle of human ingenuity. Gleaming spires of bio-luminescent coral and
iridescent alloys pierced the deep, a testament to humanity’s resilience.

He adjusted the cuff of his bespoke suit, a nervous habit he’d never quite
shaken, even after years of playing the part. On the holographic display
flickering before him, a stern-faced UN official droned on about grant
allocations for sustainable deep-sea agriculture. Lucky nodded, offered
reassuring smiles, and interjected with precisely timed, insightful questions.
He was good at this. Too good, perhaps. The man who once navigated asteroid
fields and dodged laser fire now charmed bureaucrats with spreadsheets and
projections.

“Mr. Starr, your proposal for the ‘Abyssal Bloom’ initiative is… ambitious,”
the official said, a hint of skepticism in his voice. “The projected yields
are quite high, even for your… innovative methods.”

Lucky’s smile didn’t waver. “Innovation is the cornerstone of survival, Director.
Especially when facing an existential threat like the Carcazods.” He gestured
vaguely towards the window, as if the monstrous, land-dwelling creatures were
just beyond the glass, their chitinous forms pressing against the city’s
defenses. They weren’t, of course. The Carcazods couldn’t swim, a fact that
had allowed humanity to retreat to the ocean depths and rebuild. But the
memory of their relentless advance, the screams of the surface world, still
haunted his quieter moments.

He’d built Triton Biotech from the ground up, a legitimate enterprise, a
beacon of hope in a world scarred by war and plague. He’d poured his
considerable intellect and a surprising amount of his ill-gotten gains into
it. It was a penance, a way to atone for the sins of his mercenary past. Or
so he told himself. Sometimes, late at night, when the city lights twinkled
like fallen stars outside his window, he wondered if it was just another
elaborate disguise.

The official cleared his throat. “Indeed. The Carcazod threat remains…
contained, thanks to the efforts of the UN and, of course, the natural
barrier of the ocean. However, resources are finite, Mr. Starr. We must
ensure every credit is spent wisely.”

Lucky leaned forward, his voice dropping to a conspiratorial whisper. “And
what could be wiser than ensuring our food supply, Director? A hungry populace
is a restless populace. And a restless populace is vulnerable.” He let the
implication hang in the air, the unspoken threat of internal strife, of
humanity turning on itself. It was a card he played often, and it rarely
failed. Fear, he’d learned, was a far more potent motivator than hope.

The official’s expression tightened. “A valid point, Mr. Starr. Very well.
We will review your proposal with renewed urgency. Expect a decision within
the week.”

The holographic call dissolved, leaving Lucky alone in the silent hum of his
office. He walked to the panoramic window, pressing a hand against the cool
acrylic. Outside, a school of bioluminescent fish darted past, their scales
flashing like scattered jewels. Below, the city pulsed with life, a vibrant,
fragile ecosystem. He was a part of it now, a pillar of the community, a
respected entrepreneur.

But the facade was heavy. He missed the thrill of the chase, the sharp,
uncomplicated clarity of a mission. He missed the camaraderie of his old
crew, the easy banter, the shared danger. He missed the feeling of being
truly alive, not just playing a part. He sighed, the sound lost in the
vastness of the ocean. The Carcazods were contained, but the world was
still a powder keg, and he, Lucky Starr, was sitting on the fuse, pretending
to be a gardener.
