He walked back to his desk, the whiskey glass still in his hand.
His eyes fell on the scarred, silver data chip, the memento from
his past. He picked it up, its familiar weight a small comfort in
his hand. He ran his thumb over the faded insignia of the ‘Crimson
Comets,’ his old mercenary crew. A lifetime ago.

He set the glass down and, with a sense of grim reluctance, opened
a hidden drawer in his desk. Inside, nestled amongst a few other
personal items he kept hidden from the world, was a small, framed
holographic photo. He picked it up, his fingers trembling slightly.
He hadn’t looked at it in years.

He activated the photo, and the image flickered to life. It was
his old crew, frozen in a moment of raucous celebration after a
successful raid. A younger, wilder Astra was laughing, her arm
thrown around the shoulders of a hulking brute named Jax, her
customized sniper rifle slung over her back. Gem was there, too,
before the cybernetics, her eyes sharp and intelligent as she argued
with a wiry scout named Kael. And there he was, in the center of
it all, grinning, covered in grime, a fresh scar on his cheek.
They were a family, forged in the crucible of shared danger and
unquestioning loyalty.

He traced the outline of their faces, a wave of profound loss
washing over him. He missed the simplicity of their lives, the easy
camaraderie, the sharp, uncomplicated clarity of a mission. He
missed the feeling of being truly alive, not just playing a part.
He wondered where they were now. Was Astra still out there, a
-lone wolf in the darkness? Had Gem found the peace she was always
searching for? What would they think of him now, the respectable
Mr. Starr, the man in the bespoke suit?

He knew the answer. They would laugh. They would call him a sellout,
a corporate stooge. And they would be right. He had traded the
thrill of the chase for the comfort of a gilded cage. He had traded
his loyalty to them for a seat at the table with the very people
they used to prey on. The thought was a knife in his gut.

He stared at the photo, at the ghost of the man he used to be, and
felt a profound, aching loneliness. He had everything he had ever
thought he wanted. Wealth, power, respect. But he had lost the only
thing that had ever truly mattered. He had lost himself.
