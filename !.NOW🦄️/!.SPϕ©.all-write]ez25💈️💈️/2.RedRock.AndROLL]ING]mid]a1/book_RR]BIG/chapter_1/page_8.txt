He bypassed the elegant, well-stocked bar in the corner of the
office, the one he used for entertaining clients and dignitaries.
Instead, he walked to a non-descript panel in the wall, pressing
his thumb against a hidden sensor. The panel slid open with a soft
hiss, revealing a small, refrigerated compartment. Inside, nestled
among a few other relics of his past, was a bottle of cheap,
surface-brewed whiskey. The label was faded, the contents a harsh,
amber liquid. It was the kind of drink you bought in a dive bar
on a forgotten asteroid, the kind that burned all the way down.

He poured a generous measure into a heavy, crystal glass, the
contrast between the vessel and its contents a perfect metaphor for
his own life. He swirled the liquid, the scent of it, raw and
unrefined, a stark departure from the sterile air of his office.
He took a long, slow sip, the burn in his throat a welcome, real
sensation. It was a taste of his past, a reminder of a time when
life was simpler, more honest, even in its brutality.

He walked to the panoramic window, the glass cool against his hand.
He stared at his own reflection, a ghostly image superimposed over
the glittering cityscape of Atlantis Prime. He saw the expensive
suit, the perfectly coiffed hair, the mask of calm, controlled
success. He didn’t recognize the man staring back at him. The man
in the reflection was a stranger, a carefully constructed fiction.

He felt a wave of self-loathing, so intense it almost made him
gasp. The suit felt like a cage, its fine fabric a suffocating
shroud. He remembered the feel of his old combat gear, the worn
leather, the heavy plates of armor. It had been uncomfortable,
restricting, but it had also been honest. It was the uniform of a
man who knew who he was, what he was capable of. This suit… this
was a costume. And he was an actor, playing a part he was no longer
sure he wanted.

The facade was not just heavy; it was crushing him. The weight of
the lies, the deceptions, the constant performance. He had built
his new life on a foundation of secrets, and the cracks were
beginning to show. He took another sip of the whiskey, the burn a
welcome distraction from the cold, empty feeling in his chest. He
was a king in his castle, but the castle was a prison of his own
making. And he was utterly, completely alone.
