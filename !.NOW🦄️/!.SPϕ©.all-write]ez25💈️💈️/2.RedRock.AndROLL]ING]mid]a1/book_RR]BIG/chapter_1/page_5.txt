Lucky leaned forward, his expression shifting from earnest
entrepreneur to concerned visionary. He began to paint a picture
of ‘Abyssal Bloom,’ his voice filled with a carefully constructed
passion. He spoke of genetically engineered kelp, its fronds woven
with bioluminescent algae, capable of converting the ocean’s
tremendous pressure into energy. He described a system of protein
synthesis, of accelerated growth cycles that could produce a
-harvestable crop in a matter of weeks, not months.

“Imagine, Director,” he said, his voice a conspiratorial whisper.
“Vast, underwater forests, glowing with their own internal light,
providing not just food, but power. A completely self-sustaining
ecosystem. We’re not just talking about a new kind of farm; we’re
talking about a new kind of city. One that is truly independent,
truly secure.”

He used technical jargon, words like ‘piezoelectric conversion’
and ‘symbiotic nutrient cycling,’ knowing they would be both
impressive and slightly baffling to the bureaucrat. He was selling
a dream, a vision of a future free from the anxieties that plagued
the UN’s every decision. A future where humanity was not just
surviving, but thriving.

His performance was flawless. He believed it himself, in that
moment. The project was brilliant. It was his brainchild, a testament
to his intellect and his ambition. It was also his penance, a way
to atone for the sins of his past. He had poured his considerable
intellect, and a surprising amount of his ill-gotten gains, into
Triton Biotech. It was a legitimate enterprise, a beacon of hope
in a world scarred by war and plague. But even as he spoke the
words, he knew that the project, like everything else in his life,
had a dual purpose. It was a tool, a means to an end. And the end,
as always, was power.

He saw a flicker of interest in Kaito’s eyes, the first crack in
his skeptical facade. The Director was a man who dealt in facts
and figures, but even he could be swayed by a compelling narrative.
Lucky had given him one. A story of hope, of progress, of a brighter
future. Now, it was time to introduce the villain. The monster
that would make his vision not just appealing, but essential.

“But this is not just about creating a utopia, Director,” he
continued, his tone shifting, becoming more somber. “It’s about
preventing a dystopia. It’s about ensuring that we are prepared
for the threats that still linger, just beyond the reach of our
lights.” He gestured vaguely towards the window, towards the
crushing darkness of the abyss. The stage was set.
