As Kaito droned on about resource allocation and logistical
challenges, Lucky’s mind began to drift. He wasn’t listening to
the words, but to the rhythm, the cadence of the Director’s speech.
He heard the underlying fear, the weariness of a man burdened by
the immense responsibility of keeping a fragile civilization alive.
He was analyzing the man, not the argument, searching for the
cracks in his bureaucratic armor.

A memory, sharp and unwelcome, surfaced. A different kind of
negotiation, years ago, in the dimly lit cargo bay of a derelict
freighter. The air had been thick with the smell of ozone and fear.
He’d been facing a rival mercenary captain, a hulking brute with
a plasma pistol aimed at his chest. There had been no spreadsheets,
no budget reports. Just the cold, hard calculus of survival. He’d
talked his way out of that one, his words a weapon as potent as
any blaster. He’d found the man’s weakness, his greed, and had
exploited it without mercy.

He felt a pang of something that might have been nostalgia, quickly
suppressed. That life was over. He was a different man now. A
better man. Or so he told himself. But the skills he’d honed in
the shadows were still there, sharp and ready. He was still that
same man, just wearing a more expensive suit.

He thought about how much easier it had been then. The threats
were physical, tangible. You could see them, fight them, kill
them. Now, the threats were buried in sub-clauses and budget
reports, hidden in the nuances of diplomatic language. It was a
different kind of war, a war fought with words and influence, but
the stakes were just as high. Higher, even. The fate of an entire
city rested on his ability to play this game.

He brought his focus back to the present, to the holographic face
of Director Kaito. The man was winding down, his arguments exhausted.
It was time for the counter-attack. Time to shift the battlefield
from the sterile world of logistics to the raw, primal realm of
fear. He knew, with a cold certainty, that it was a battle Kaito
was not equipped to win.

“Director,” he interjected, his voice soft, yet firm. “Your points
are all valid. The risks are real. But you are failing to consider
the greatest risk of all. The risk of inaction.” He paused,
letting the weight of his words settle. He was no longer just a
biotech entrepreneur. He was a survivor, a man who had looked into
the abyss and had not flinched. And he was about to show the
Director a glimpse of that abyss.
