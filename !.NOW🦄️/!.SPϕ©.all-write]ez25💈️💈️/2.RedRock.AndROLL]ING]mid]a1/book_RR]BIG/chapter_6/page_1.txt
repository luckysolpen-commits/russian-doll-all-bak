The UN detention cell was a study in minimalist discomfort.
Polished durasteel walls, a single, unyielding bench, and a
faint, antiseptic scent that did little to mask the underlying
tension. It was a cage, and Lucky, Gem, and Astra were the
-unwilling occupants. The echoes of the Martian envoy’s
-desperate warning still rang in their ears. A plague. Zara’s
-twisted ambition. It was a nightmare unfolding in slow motion,
and they were trapped in the opening act.

A UN guard, impassive and heavily armed, stood sentinel
-outside the transparent force field that served as their cell
door. He was a statue, a symbol of the bureaucratic inertia
that was their greatest enemy. Lucky tried to engage him, to
-glean any information, but the guard remained stoic, a wall
of official silence.

“This is a waste of time,” Astra finally grumbled, pacing the
-small confines like a caged tiger. Her energy was a palpable
-force in the small room, a stark contrast to the sterile
-calm of their prison. “We should be out there, doing
-something. Not sitting here like common criminals.”

“We are common criminals, Astra,” Gem reminded her, her voice
-flat, devoid of emotion. “At least, in the eyes of the UN.
An unregistered mercenary, a rogue biotech entrepreneur, and
a… well, a clone with a questionable past. Not exactly a
-shining example of civic responsibility.”

Lucky sighed. “She’s right. Our best bet is to play by their
-rules, for now. We need to see the Queen. She’s the key to
-understanding Zara’s true intentions.” He was a man who
-preferred to be in control, and this powerlessness was a
-bitter pill to swallow.
