Book 0:YE OLDE SOUL PEN:  Accidental Witch - Chapter 1: The Ink of Dawn
Page 1The village of Quillhaven clung to the edge of the Shatterveil Cliffs, where the sea roared below like a beast that never slept. Elara Veyn, barely nineteen, stood at the cliff’s edge, her cloak snapping in the salt-streaked wind. Her fingers clutched a worn leather satchel, its contents a secret even to her. The sky was bruised purple, dawn still an hour away, but she couldn’t wait. Not today.The elders called this the Hour of Whispers, when the world held its breath and the old magics stirred. Elara’s grandmother used to say the cliffs listened, but Elara wasn’t here for whispers. She was here for answers.In her satchel was a relic her mother had left her—a pen, its wood black as midnight, its nib glinting like a shard of starlight. She’d never seen it used, never dared touch it until last night, when the dreams came. Vivid, jagged dreams of ink spilling into rivers, of words carving mountains, of her own hands writing truths she didn’t understand.She knelt, opened the satchel, and drew out the pen. It was heavier than it looked, warm against her calloused fingers. The villagers thought her a dreamer, a girl too fond of stories to be practical. But this pen wasn’t a story. It was real, and it terrified her.“What are you?” she whispered, holding it to the faint light. The nib shimmered, and for a moment, she swore it pulsed.  
Page 2Elara’s breath caught. She glanced over her shoulder, half-expecting to see one of the village wardens patrolling the cliffs. No one. Just the wind and the distant cry of gulls. She tucked the pen into her sleeve and stood, her boots crunching on the gravel path back to Quillhaven.The village was a patchwork of stone cottages, their roofs thatched with sea-reed. Lanterns hung from iron posts, casting pools of amber light. Elara slipped through the alleys, avoiding the main square where the market would soon wake. She needed to reach the Scribe’s Hollow, the old archive where her mother once worked. If answers existed, they’d be there.The Hollow was a crumbling tower at the village’s edge, its walls etched with runes no one could read anymore. Elara’s mother, Lirien, had been its last keeper, cataloging tales of the Shatterveil’s lost magics. When Lirien vanished five years ago, the elders sealed the Hollow, claiming it cursed. Elara didn’t believe in curses, but she believed in her mother.She reached the tower’s iron door, its surface cold under her palm. The lock was rusted, but she’d come prepared. From her satchel, she pulled a hairpin and a thin blade, working them into the keyhole. Her hands shook—not from cold, but from the weight of what she was doing.The lock clicked. The door groaned open.  
Page 3Inside, the air was thick with dust and the scent of old parchment. Elara lit a small lantern from her satchel, its flame revealing shelves that stretched into shadow. Books, scrolls, and loose pages lay in disarray, as if abandoned mid-thought. She stepped forward, her boots silent on the stone floor.The pen in her sleeve felt heavier now, almost pulling her toward a spiral staircase at the room’s center. She climbed, the lantern casting jagged shadows on the walls. At the top was a small chamber, its only furnishing a wooden desk carved with the same runes as the tower’s exterior.Elara set the lantern down and pulled the pen from her sleeve. Its warmth had grown, almost burning. She placed it on the desk, half-expecting it to move on its own. It didn’t, but the air seemed to hum, a low vibration she felt in her bones.She found a scrap of parchment and, without thinking, pressed the pen’s nib to it. Ink flowed, black as void, though she’d added none. Her hand moved, writing words she didn’t choose: The Soulpen wakes when the world breaks.She dropped the pen, heart pounding. The words glowed faintly, then faded into the parchment.  
Page 4Elara stared at the blank page. Had she imagined it? No—the pen still lay there, its nib wet with ink. She reached for it again, hesitating. Her mother’s stories flooded back: tales of a pen that wrote truths no one could bear, that shaped fates and unraveled them. Lirien had called it the Soulpen, a relic from before the Shatterveil was named, when the cliffs were a bridge between worlds.Elara’s fingers closed around the pen. This time, she wrote deliberately: Who am I? The ink flowed again, and words formed: Elara Veyn, daughter of Lirien, bearer of the unbroken thread.She frowned. Unbroken thread? It sounded like one of her mother’s riddles, the kind that made Elara’s head ache as a child. She tried again: Where is my mother?The pen scratched: Beyond the veil, where ink meets bone.Elara’s stomach twisted. The veil was a myth, a story of a barrier between the living world and whatever lay beyond. No one crossed it and returned. Or so the elders said.  
Page 5A noise below—a creak of wood, a shuffle of feet. Elara froze, slipping the pen into her sleeve and dousing the lantern. She crept to the staircase, peering into the darkness. A shadow moved in the lower chamber, too tall to be a stray animal.“Who’s there?” she called, her voice sharper than she felt.No answer, but the shadow stilled. Elara’s hand tightened on the blade in her satchel. She descended slowly, each step deliberate. The shadow shifted, revealing a figure cloaked in gray, their face hidden by a hood.“Elara Veyn,” the figure said, their voice low, almost a growl. “You shouldn’t be here.”“How do you know my name?” She kept the blade hidden but ready.The figure stepped into a sliver of moonlight from a cracked window. Their hood fell back, revealing a man with sharp features, his eyes like chipped obsidian. “The Soulpen calls to those who listen,” he said. “And I’ve been listening a long time.”  
Page 6Elara’s grip on the blade tightened. “Who are you?”“Call me Torren,” he said, raising his hands to show they were empty. “I knew your mother. She was… reckless, like you.”Elara bristled. “Don’t talk about her like you knew her better than me.”Torren’s lips twitched, not quite a smile. “I didn’t say that. But I know what you’re holding. The Soulpen isn’t a toy, girl. It’s a key, and keys open things best left locked.”She took a step back, her shoulder brushing a shelf. A scroll fell, unraveling across the floor. “If you want it, you’ll have to take it,” she said, voice steady despite her racing pulse.Torren laughed, a dry sound like breaking twigs. “I don’t want it. I want it gone. Destroyed, before it wakes what it shouldn’t.”  
Page 7Elara’s mind raced. Destroy the Soulpen? Her mother had guarded it, maybe died for it. “Why should I trust you?”“You shouldn’t,” Torren said. “But you’ll want to hear me out. The Soulpen doesn’t just write. It binds. It takes. Every word you write with it costs something—your will, your memories, your soul, if you’re not careful.”Elara’s hand drifted to her sleeve, where the pen’s warmth pulsed against her skin. “You’re lying.”“Am I?” Torren stepped closer, his eyes glinting. “Write something now. Something small. See what happens.”She hesitated, then pulled the pen out, grabbing another scrap of parchment from the desk. She wrote: The lantern lights.The extinguished lantern flared to life, its flame blue and unnatural. Elara gasped, dropping the pen. Her head throbbed, a sharp pain behind her eyes.  
Page 8Torren’s expression darkened. “You feel it, don’t you? The cost.”Elara pressed a hand to her temple, the pain fading but leaving a hollow ache. “What was that?”“The Soulpen’s price,” he said. “It draws from you to make the words real. Small truths cost little. Big ones…” He trailed off, glancing at the glowing lantern. “Your mother wrote something big, Elara. Too big.”She glared at him, her fear turning to anger. “If you know so much, tell me where she is.”Torren’s gaze softened, just for a moment. “I don’t know. But I know she used the Soulpen to cross the veil. And I know it’s calling you to do the same.”Elara’s throat tightened. The words she’d written—beyond the veil, where ink meets bone—echoed in her mind.  
Page 9Before she could respond, a bell tolled outside, sharp and urgent. The village alarm. Torren cursed under his breath. “Wardens. They must’ve seen the light.”Elara grabbed the pen and parchment, stuffing them into her satchel. “Come with me,” she said, not waiting for his answer. She bolted down the stairs, Torren’s footsteps heavy behind her.They slipped out a side door just as lantern beams swept the tower’s entrance. Voices shouted, boots pounding the gravel. Elara led Torren through the alleys, her knowledge of Quillhaven’s twists giving them an edge.They ducked into a narrow gap between two cottages, crouching in the shadows. Elara’s heart hammered as the wardens passed, their lanterns casting long fingers of light.“Who are you, really?” she whispered to Torren.  
Page 10He leaned close, his breath warm against her ear. “Someone who’s seen what the Soulpen can do. Someone who’s lost people to it.”Elara wanted to press him, but the wardens’ voices faded, and she motioned for him to follow. They crept toward the cliffs again, where the darkness would hide them. The pen in her satchel seemed to hum, a faint vibration that matched her pulse.At the cliff’s edge, Torren stopped. “You can’t keep it, Elara. It’ll consume you.”She faced him, the wind tearing at her cloak. “It’s all I have left of her. I’m not giving it up.”He studied her, his eyes unreadable. “Then you’ll need to learn to use it. But not here. Not now.”  
Page 11Elara’s mind swirled with questions, but the bell tolled again, closer this time. “Where, then?”“Beyond Quillhaven,” Torren said. “There’s a place, an old scriptorium in the Ashen Wastes. It’s where the Soulpen was made. If you want answers, that’s where they’ll be.”The Ashen Wastes. Elara had heard stories—barren lands where nothing grew, where the air tasted of cinders. No one went there willingly.“Why should I believe you?” she asked again.“Because I’m not asking you to give me the pen,” he said. “I’m asking you to survive it.”Elara’s fingers brushed the satchel. The pen’s warmth was a comfort and a warning.  
Page 12The wardens’ voices grew louder, their lanterns flickering closer. Elara made her choice. “Lead the way,” she said.Torren nodded, pulling his hood up. They moved along the cliff path, staying low. The sea roared below, its rhythm steadying Elara’s nerves. She didn’t trust Torren, not fully, but he knew things—things about the Soulpen, about her mother. That was enough for now.They reached a hidden trail, one Elara had used as a child to sneak out at night. It wound down the cliffs, narrow and treacherous. Torren moved with the ease of someone who’d walked it before.Halfway down, he stopped, pointing to a cave mouth barely visible in the rock. “In there,” he said. “We’ll wait out the wardens.”Elara hesitated, then followed. The cave was damp, its walls slick with moss.  
Page 13Inside, Torren lit a small fire, its light revealing carvings on the walls—runes like those in the Hollow, but older, worn smooth by time. Elara traced one with her finger, feeling the pen hum in her satchel.“What are these?” she asked.“Wards,” Torren said. “From when the Soulpen was guarded, not hidden. This cave was a refuge for its keepers.”Elara’s chest tightened. “My mother came here, didn’t she?”Torren nodded. “She was the last true keeper. Before she… changed things.”“Changed how?” Elara’s voice was sharp, demanding.  
Page 14Torren met her gaze. “She wrote something that shouldn’t have been written. Something that broke the veil. I don’t know what, but it’s why she’s gone—and why the Soulpen chose you.”Elara’s hand closed around the satchel. The pen’s warmth was a pulse now, steady as a heartbeat. She wanted to write, to demand more answers, but Torren’s warning echoed: Every word costs something.Outside, the wardens’ voices faded. Dawn was breaking, painting the cave mouth gold. Elara stood, slinging the satchel over her shoulder. “We go to the Ashen Wastes,” she said. “But I’m not destroying the pen. Not until I know what it did to her.”Torren’s jaw tightened, but he nodded. “Then we’d better move. The wardens won’t stop looking, and neither will the ones who want the pen for themselves.”Elara stepped into the dawn, the Soulpen’s weight both a burden and a promise.
