Her discoveries extended to the Carcazods themselves. She analyzed their genetic makeup, their rapid regenerative capabilities, and their terrifying adaptability. They were not natural creatures; they were engineered, designed for destruction, their very existence a testament to a dark, unseen force. The medical bay, ostensibly for healing, also contained disturbing evidence of experimentation, of attempts to modify and enhance the clones, to push the boundaries of their biological limits. Astra saw the subtle mutations, the genetic markers that indicated a deliberate manipulation of their DNA, hinting at a darker purpose, a more sinister agenda than mere self-preservation.

One evening, while analyzing a particularly complex genetic sequence, Astra stumbled upon a hidden data log, encrypted and buried deep within the SSOL’s central database. It detailed early experiments, failures, and successes in the cloning process, but more disturbingly, it contained records of consciousness transfer, of minds being uploaded, downloaded, and even merged. The ethical implications were horrifying. It wasn’t just about creating bodies; it was about manipulating souls, about playing God on a cosmic scale. The log also hinted at a connection between the Carcazods and the cloning process, suggesting that the creatures were not just enemies, but perhaps a byproduct, or even a deliberate creation, of Sol’s own experiments.
