The battle of the gods began. Solus,
in his giant, monstrous form, was a
force of pure, destructive order. He moved
through the team's vibrant and chaotic world
like a storm, his every step erasing
the beautiful and imperfect life that they
had created. He turned the meandering rivers
into straight, sterile canals. He turned the
wild and tangled forests into neat and
orderly rows of identical trees. He turned
the singing fish into silent, geometric shapes.
He was not just destroying their world.
He was sanitizing it. He was making
it a perfect reflection of his own
empty soul. The team did not try
to fight him directly. To do so
would be to play his game, to
admit that the only way to solve
a problem is with force. They fought
him with their art. They fought him
with their creativity. They fought him with
the very thing he was trying to
erase. As Solus turned a forest into
a row of perfect spheres, Maria would
be right behind him, turning the spheres
into a menagerie of strange and beautiful
clockwork animals. As he turned a river
into a straight canal, Flash would be
there, turning the canal into a giant,
slippery slide of rainbow-colored mud. As
he turned a mountain into a perfect
cone, Pax would be there, turning the
cone into a giant, harmonious wind chime
that sang a song of defiance against
his sterile order. And as he tried
to erase the sky, Nyx would be
there, writing poems of such profound and
terrible beauty on the encroaching darkness that
even Solus himself would be forced to
pause and read them. It was a
cosmic game of cat and mouse, of
creation and re-creation, of order and chaos.
And it was a game that was
driving Solus mad. He was a being
of perfect order, of absolute control. And
he could not control them. He could
not predict them. He could not understand
them. They were a force of chaos,
of creativity, of a joyful and defiant
life that he could not erase. And
it was breaking his perfect, orderly mind.
He roared in frustration, his voice a
concussive blast of pure, sonic order that
shattered the mountains and boiled the rivers.
He unleashed all of his power, all
of his rage, all of his frustration,
in a single, final, and desperate attempt
to erase them, to silence them, to
make them a part of his perfect,
silent, and orderly world. A wave of
pure, concentrated order washed over the team,
a wave that was designed to erase
their individuality, their creativity, their very souls.
It was the ultimate expression of his
philosophy. The ultimate weapon of the self.
And it was a weapon that was
unstoppable. Or so he thought. The team
stood before the wave of pure order,
and they did not run. They did
not hide. They did not fight. They
did the one thing that Solus could
not comprehend. They embraced it. They opened
themselves up to it. They became one
with it. And in that moment of
perfect surrender, they found the flaw in
his perfect order. The single, tiny, and
all-important thing that he had left
out of his perfect world. Love.
