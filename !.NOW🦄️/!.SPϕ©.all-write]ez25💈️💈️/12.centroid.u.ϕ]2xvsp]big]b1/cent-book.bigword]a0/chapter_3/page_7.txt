While Lucky and Astra were learning to
let go of their creation, another student
was struggling with the opposite problem. Maria
Blochroachez, a shy and talented artist from
Lethari House, had also created something special
for the competition. It was a small,
intricate sculpture of a clockwork bird, its
feathers made of spun silver and its
eyes of tiny, glittering jewels. It was
a work of breathtaking beauty, a testament
to her incredible skill and patience. But
it was also just a sculpture. It
was not alive. It did not move.
It did not sing. It was a
perfect, beautiful, and utterly lifeless thing. Maria
was a master of the technical aspects
of creation, but she struggled with the
more intangible qualities of soul, of passion,
of life. She could create a perfect
body, but she could not give it
a soul. And it was a source
of great frustration for her. She would
spend hours in her room, staring at
the clockwork bird, willing it to move,
to sing, to come to life. She
would use her Soul Pen, her Tear
It Cards, all the tools at her
disposal. But nothing worked. The bird remained
a beautiful, silent, and heartbreaking reminder of
her own limitations. Lucky and Astra noticed
her struggle. They would see her in
the Great Hall, staring at their storybook
world with a look of longing and
envy in her eyes. They knew that
she was a talented artist, and they
felt a sense of empathy for her
struggle. They decided to reach out to
her, to offer their help, their friendship,
their support. They found her in her
room, surrounded by a menagerie of beautiful
but lifeless creations. There were clockwork animals,
porcelain dolls, and glass flowers, all of
them perfect, all of them empty. Maria
was shy and hesitant at first, unused
to the attention of the popular kids
from Luminari. But Lucky and Astra were
kind and genuine, and she soon opened
up to them. She told them about
her struggle, her frustration, her fear that
she would never be a true creator.
Lucky and Astra listened with patience and
understanding. They did not offer any easy
answers, any magical solutions. They simply shared
their own experiences, their own struggles, their
own journey of discovery. They told her
about the flower, the sphere, the storybook
world. They told her about the art
of letting go, of trusting the process,
of allowing the creation to create itself.
They told her that the soul of
a creation comes not from the creator,
but from the universe itself. The creator
is just a channel, a vessel, a
midwife. Maria listened, her eyes wide with
a new understanding. She had been trying
to force life into her creations, to
will them into existence. But she had
been going about it all wrong. She
had to let go. She had to
trust. She had to become a channel.
It was a new and radical idea,
and it was a little bit scary.
But it also filled her with a
sense of hope. A sense of possibility.
A sense of a new beginning.
