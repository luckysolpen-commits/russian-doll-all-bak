The Proctor's disappointment was a death sentence, and you know it. You have to get off this moon.

You spend three cycles planning your escape. You slice into the maintenance schedules, create a blind spot in the hangar bay's surveillance network, and divert a small amount of fuel to a "decommissioned" orbital shuttle. It's a relic, barely space-worthy, but it's your only shot.

Under the guise of a routine maintenance check, you slip into the hangar bay. The shuttle is there, dark and silent. You have a window of exactly seven minutes before the next patrol passes. As you're powering up the shuttle's primary systems, you hear voices. A security team has arrived early. They haven't seen you yet, but they're closing in on your position.

Your escape plan has just gone sideways. You can try to hide in the shuttle's service crawlspace and hope they miss you. Or you can try to finish the pre-flight sequence and blast your way out of the hangar, a move that is as loud as it is suicidal.
