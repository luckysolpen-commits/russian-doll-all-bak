You're a clone of the Empire, and your best chance of survival is with the Empire. Whatever you've seen or done, a rescue is your only viable option.

You set to work. Your Digger is a wreck, but its comms array is mostly intact, just offline. You need to reroute power from a secondary system. The environmental controls are a good candidate; you can survive in your suit for a few days. The clown ship is another source of parts. You spend hours stripping wires, rerouting power conduits, and bypassing fried circuits. It's a slow, grueling process.

Finally, after what feels like an eternity, you manage to get a signal. It's weak, but it's there. You send out a distress call on a coded military channel, giving your designation and a brief, heavily edited version of events: "Engaged and neutralized two hostile vessels. My craft is disabled. Requesting immediate evac."

The reply comes almost instantly. "Acknowledged, 700. A recovery team is en route. ETA is four hours. Do not leave your location. And congratulations on the kills."

Relief washes over you. You are saved. But as you wait in the silence, you can't help but wonder if you've made the right choice. You think about the data chip in your pocket, or the location of the Underground Xircus, or the dying clown's warning. Your rescue is also a return to the fold, a return to the unquestioning loyalty that now feels so alien.

When the rescue ship arrives, you will be debriefed. You will have to tell them something.
