The Architect's journal was not a dry scientific log, but a descent into madness.
It began with meticulous observations of stellar phenomena, theories on energy manipulation,
and the ambitious dream of creating a self-sustaining artificial sun.
Dr. Aris Thorne, as he was then, wrote with a detached brilliance,
his intellect undeniable, his ambition boundless.

But as Astra read on, the tone shifted.
The entries became more personal, more obsessive.
He wrote of the limitations of humanity, its inherent flaws, its self-destructive tendencies.
He saw chaos where others saw freedom, weakness where others saw compassion.
He believed humanity was a disease, and he, Aris Thorne, was the cure.

He detailed his experiments, increasingly reckless and unethical.
He spoke of manipulating consciousness, of creating a collective mind,
a perfect unity where individual thought was subsumed for the greater good.
He saw himself as a benevolent god, guiding his flock to enlightenment,
whether they wanted it or not.

Then came the entries about the "lab accident" that Maya had mentioned.
Thorne described it not as a failure, but as a necessary sacrifice.
A small moon, a testing ground for his early Metal Sun prototypes,
had been consumed in a catastrophic energy surge.
He wrote of the beauty of the destruction, the raw power he had unleashed.
He felt no remorse, only a chilling sense of triumph.

"The universe is a chaotic symphony," he wrote, his words chilling Astra to the bone.
"And I am its conductor. I will bring harmony, even if it means silencing a few discordant notes."

He spoke of his former colleagues at Aevum Dynamics with contempt,
calling them shortsighted, weak, incapable of grasping his true vision.
He saw their attempts to stop him not as ethical objections, but as petty jealousy.
He was a prophet, and they were merely mortals, clinging to their outdated beliefs.

As Astra read, she felt a profound sense of dread.
This wasn't just a man with a god complex; this was a man who had truly transcended humanity.
He had shed all empathy, all compassion, all connection to the very species he claimed to save.
He was a machine, cold and calculating, driven by a singular, terrifying purpose.

She found entries detailing the early stages of the Icarus's construction,
hidden in the deepest reaches of the Kuiper Belt.
He had worked in secret, meticulously building his new world, his new sun,
while the rest of humanity squabbled and fought amongst themselves.
He saw their blindness as a confirmation of his own superiority.

She also found references to a "key," a failsafe mechanism built into the Metal Sun.
It was a contingency, a way to shut down the entire system if it ever went rogue.
But the entries were vague, cryptic, as if Thorne himself was hesitant to commit the full details to writing.
He was a control freak, even over his own creations.

Astra's heart pounded in her chest.
This was it. The weakness. The flaw in his perfect design.
He had built a god, but he had also built a kill switch.
And she was going to find it.

She continued to read, searching for any clue, any hint of the key's location or activation.
She knew it wouldn't be easy.
Thorne was too intelligent, too paranoid, to leave such a vital piece of information lying around.
But she was a survivor. She was a fighter.
And she would not rest until she found a way to bring down the Supreme Emperor Sol.
