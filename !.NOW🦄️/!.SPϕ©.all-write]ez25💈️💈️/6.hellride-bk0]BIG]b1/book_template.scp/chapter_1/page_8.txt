The decision was made.
A fragile consensus born of desperation and defiance.
Jax, ever the pragmatist, began plotting a course.
A careful, roundabout route that would keep them in the shadows of moons and asteroid belts as much as possible.
Every jump, every burn, had to be calculated to avoid the direct gaze of the Metal Sun.
It was like being a mouse trying to cross a brightly lit room, scurrying from one piece of furniture to the next, hoping the cat wasn't watching.

Maya was a whirlwind of silent activity.
Her domain was the invisible world of data, and she moved through it with a grace that was almost physical.
She was crafting a message, a digital flare to be sent into the darkest corners of the net.
It couldn't be a direct call to arms; that would be too easily traced.
It had to be a whisper, a rumor, a breadcrumb trail for the right kind of people to follow.
She used old codes, forgotten backdoors, layers of encryption that would make a corporate security chief weep.
It was a work of art, a symphony of paranoia and hope.

Lucky watched them, a sense of profound gratitude washing over him.
He was the captain, the one who was supposed to have all the answers.
But he was nothing without them.
Jax, the steady hand that kept their world from falling apart.
Maya, the sharp mind that saw the patterns in the chaos.
They were a trinity, a three-legged stool.
Take one leg away, and the whole thing collapses.

He turned his attention back to the viewport.
The Metal Sun was still there, a constant, silent rebuke to their fledgling rebellion.
It was so vast, so powerful, so absolute.
Their plan felt like a child's fantasy in the face of it.
A wooden sword against a dragon.

Doubt, a cold and insidious serpent, began to coil in his gut.
Were they fools? Were they just deluding themselves?
Were they leading themselves to a quicker, more painful death?
He thought of the Architect's serene, smiling face.
The face of a man who had remade the universe in his own image.
A man who had declared himself a god.
What chance did a broken-down freighter and its three-person crew have against a god?

He closed his eyes, forcing the doubt down.
He couldn't afford it. It was a luxury he didn't have.
Hope was a muscle. You had to exercise it, even when it was sore, even when it felt like it was about to tear.
If he let the doubt win, they were already dead.

He opened his eyes and looked at the Metal Sun again.
But this time, he didn't see its power, its perfection.
He saw its arrogance.
He saw the flaw in all tyrants, in all gods of their own making.
The belief in their own invincibility.
The Architect, for all his genius, was still a man.
And men could be broken.

"Ready when you are, Captain," Jax said, his voice pulling Lucky back from the brink.

"Message is away," Maya added, a note of triumph in her voice.
"Let's see who bites."

Lucky took a deep breath, the recycled air of the cockpit filling his lungs.
It tasted of oil and ozone and a faint, lingering fear.
But it also tasted of hope.
"Take us out, Jax," he said, his voice clear and strong.
"Let's go fishing."

The *Stray Dog* eased out from behind the asteroid, its engines burning a faint, defiant blue.
It was a tiny ship, a fragile vessel in a vast and hostile sea.
But it was moving.
It had a purpose.
And in a world that had just been brought to its knees, that was everything.
They were no longer just running.
They were hunting.
Hunting for a spark of rebellion in the all-consuming light of the new, false sun.
