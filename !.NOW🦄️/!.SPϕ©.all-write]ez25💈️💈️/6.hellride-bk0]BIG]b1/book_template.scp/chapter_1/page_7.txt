The *Stray Dog* drifted in the shadow of a large, pockmarked asteroid.
It was a temporary reprieve, a moment of stolen invisibility.
But they all knew it couldn't last.
The Metal Sun's light, even reflected off the asteroid's surface,
was a constant reminder of their predicament.
It was a cold, analytical light, and it seemed to see right through the rock, right through the hull of their ship, right into their fearful hearts.

"We need a plan," Jax said, breaking the long silence.
He was cleaning his tools, a nervous habit, his hands needing something to do, something to fix.
"We can't just float here forever."

"What kind of plan?" Maya shot back, her voice sharp with frustration.
"A plan to do what? Tickle the Architect to death?"
Her sarcasm was a shield, but Lucky could see the fear behind it.
She was a creature of the net, of logic and order.
And they were facing something that defied all logic, an order imposed by a madman.

"A plan to survive," Lucky said, his voice calm and steady.
He was the eye of their small, personal storm.
If he lost his calm, they would all be swept away.
"That's the only plan that matters right now."

He looked at the star chart on the secondary screen.
A map of a world that no longer existed.
All the old routes, the safe harbors, the hidden corners of the system...
they were all meaningless now.
They were all under the glare of the Metal Sun.

"We need allies," he said, thinking aloud.
"We can't do this alone."

"Allies?" Jax scoffed. "Who? The corporations? They'll be lining up to make deals with the Architect.
The governments? They're probably already drafting surrender terms."

He was right, of course. The powerful would flock to the powerful.
That was the way of the world.
But Lucky wasn't thinking of the powerful.
He was thinking of the powerless.
The ones who, like them, had been caught in the gears of the Architect's grand design.
The smugglers, the pirates, the outcasts.
The ones who lived in the shadows.

"There are others," Lucky said, his gaze sweeping across the star chart.
"People who won't bow down. People who value their freedom more than their safety."

"You're talking about the fringe," Maya said, her voice a mixture of skepticism and hope.
"The criminal element."

"I'm talking about survivors," Lucky corrected her.
"People who know how to live on the edges.
People who know how to fight back when they're cornered."

He pointed to a sector on the chart, a notorious haven for pirates and smugglers.
"We start there," he said.
"We put out feelers. We see who's willing to listen."

It was a long shot, a desperate gamble.
But it was better than waiting to die.
It was a move.
A step forward in the darkness.

"It's risky," Jax said, but there was a new light in his eyes.
A spark of something that might have been hope.
"But I'm tired of running."

"I'll see what I can find on the shadow nets," Maya added, her fingers already dancing across her console.
Her shield of sarcasm was gone, replaced by a focused intensity.
She had a purpose now, a problem to solve.

Lucky nodded, a grim smile touching his lips.
It wasn't much of a plan.
It was a whisper of defiance in the face of a hurricane.
But it was their whisper.
And they would make it heard.
Even if it was the last sound they ever made.
