The silence of space was not empty.

It was a thick, velvet blanket, smothering and vast.
Lucky had always found a strange comfort in it.
Out here, floating in the cockpit of the *Stray Dog*,
the universe felt both infinite and intimate.
He could see the curve of Earth, a swirl of blue and white,
a marble of impossible beauty hanging in the void.
His knuckles were white where he gripped the controls,
not from tension, but from a deep, bone-deep weariness.
They had been on the run for three cycles,
and the constant looking over his shoulder was a weight.

"Anything?" a voice crackled through the comms. It was Jax,
his voice a low rumble, always calm, always steady.
Jax was the anchor of their small, ragtag crew.
A former corporate engineer who’d seen too much,
he was the one who kept the *Stray Dog* from falling apart at the seams.

Lucky leaned forward, his face close to the viewport.
The glass was cool against his forehead.
"Just the usual cosmic dust bunnies," he murmured back.
"And a whole lot of nothing."
His gaze swept across the starfield, a familiar tapestry of diamonds on black.
But something felt different today. A prickle on his skin.
A hum that wasn't coming from the ship's aging life support.

"I've got a bad feeling," Maya’s voice joined in,
her tone sharp, laced with the anxiety that always seemed to cling to her.
She was their slicer, their ghost in the machine,
her fingers able to dance through firewalls that would fry lesser talents.
"The net is… quiet. Too quiet."

Lucky’s eyes narrowed. Maya was never wrong about the net.
If it was quiet, it meant something big was hiding in the silence.
He adjusted the ship's sensors, pushing their range,
feeling the engines strain with the effort.
The hum grew louder, a low thrum that vibrated through the deck plates,
through the soles of his worn boots.
It wasn't a sound, not really. It was a presence.

Then he saw it.

At first, it was just a distortion, a ripple in the fabric of space.
Like heat haze on a summer road.
But it grew, expanding, warping the starlight around it.
A pinprick of impossible light appeared in the center of the ripple.
It wasn't the familiar, warm glow of a star.
This was a cold light, a sharp, metallic brilliance that hurt to look at.

"What in the hells…?" Jax breathed over the comm.

The pinprick of light intensified, blooming like a time-lapse flower of death.
It pulsed, a steady, rhythmic beat that seemed to mock the frantic thumping of Lucky’s own heart.
From the heart of that impossible light, a shape began to emerge.
A silhouette against the blinding glare.
It was vast, a mountain of metal and darkness,
its lines too sharp, too perfect to be natural.
It was a ship. But it was unlike any ship Lucky had ever seen.
It didn't just move through space; it commanded it,
parting the void like a blade.

The Icarus had returned.
And it had brought something terrible and beautiful with it.
The hum was a roar now, a symphony of power that drowned out all thought.
Lucky could only stare, his breath caught in his throat,
as the ship and its impossible cargo swam into view.
The universe, it seemed, was about to change forever.
He felt a cold dread snake its way up his spine.
This wasn't just a new ship. It was a new god.
And they were floating right in its path.
He gripped the controls, his knuckles aching.
The weariness was gone, replaced by a surge of adrenaline.
The silence of space was about to be broken.
And he had a feeling it would be screaming.
