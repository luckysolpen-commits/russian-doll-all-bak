The journey was a tense, nerve-wracking affair.
They moved in a series of short, calculated jumps, a cosmic game of leapfrog.
Each jump was a blind gamble, a moment of utter vulnerability as they tore a hole in spacetime.
They would emerge into the harsh glare of the Metal Sun, exposed and naked for a few terrifying seconds,
before their sensors could get a lock and Jax could pilot them into the next shadow.

Lucky spent most of the time at the viewport, his eyes scanning the void.
He wasn't just looking for threats.
He was looking for signs of life.
For the flicker of an engine burn, the glint of a hull.
Anything to show that they weren't the only ones foolish enough to be moving in this new, dangerous world.
But the void was empty.
The shipping lanes were silent.
The usual chatter of the comms was replaced by the Architect's looping broadcast, a constant, maddening mantra of peace and unity.
It felt like they were the last free people in the solar system.

Inside the cockpit, the tension was a palpable thing.
Jax was a coiled spring, his hands never straying far from the controls.
Maya was a ghost, her face illuminated by the glow of her console, her eyes red-rimmed from lack of sleep.
She was sifting through mountains of data, looking for a response to her message, a needle in a haystack of digital noise.

Lucky felt the weight of their fear, their exhaustion.
It was his weight to carry.
He was the captain. The leader. The one who had to be strong, even when he felt like he was crumbling inside.
He moved through the small space of the cockpit, a quiet, reassuring presence.
A fresh cup of synth-coffee for Jax.
A hand on Maya's shoulder, a murmured word of encouragement.
Small gestures, but they were all he had to offer.
They were a family, a dysfunctional, bickering, but fiercely loyal family.
And they were all they had left.

Days bled into one another, marked only by the changing shadows on the cockpit floor.
The initial adrenaline had worn off, replaced by a dull, grinding fatigue.
The hope that had fueled their departure was beginning to flicker, to gutter like a candle in the wind.
They were alone.
Utterly, completely alone.

Then, it happened.

"I've got something," Maya said, her voice a hoarse whisper.
She sat bolt upright, her eyes wide, her face a mask of disbelief.

Lucky and Jax were at her side in an instant.
"What is it?" Lucky asked, his heart pounding in his chest.

"A response," Maya said, her voice trembling slightly.
"It's heavily encrypted, bounced through a dozen dead-drops.
But it's a response."

She worked her magic, her fingers a blur across the console.
Lines of code scrolled across the screen, a waterfall of green and black.
Then, a single message appeared.

**WHERE?**

Just one word.
But it was the most beautiful word Lucky had ever seen.
It was a spark in the darkness.
A hand reaching out in the void.
They weren't alone.

"They're cautious," Jax said, his voice tight with excitement.
"They want to know where to meet."

"They're smart," Lucky corrected him, a slow smile spreading across his face.
"They're survivors."

He looked at Maya. "Can you trace it?"

She shook her head. "No. They're too good.
But I can send a reply."

"Do it," Lucky said, his voice ringing with a newfound confidence.
"Send them the coordinates to the Ghost Nebula.
It's neutral ground. A place where shadows have shadows."

Maya's fingers flew across the console again.
The message was sent, a single breadcrumb cast into the vast, digital ocean.
Now, all they could do was wait.
And hope.

But it was a new kind of hope.
A hope that was no longer just a desperate fantasy.
It was a tangible thing.
A single word, a single question, that had changed everything.
The game was afoot.
And for the first time since the Metal Sun had risen,
Lucky felt like they had a chance to win.
