The Ghost Nebula was a graveyard of stars.
A swirling chaos of dust and gas, illuminated by the faint, dying light of ancient suns.
It was a place where ships went to disappear, a blind spot in the all-seeing eye of the system.
And now, it was their rendezvous.

The *Stray Dog* hung in the heart of the nebula, its engines silent, its lights dimmed.
They were a ghost among ghosts, waiting.
The tension in the cockpit was a living thing, a fourth member of the crew.
Every flicker on the sensor screen, every stray bit of cosmic radiation, sent a jolt of adrenaline through their veins.

"How long do we wait?" Jax asked, his voice a low rumble that did little to hide his anxiety.
He was a man of action, of engines and conduits.
This waiting, this helplessness, was eating away at him.

"As long as it takes," Lucky said, his gaze fixed on the swirling clouds of gas outside the viewport.
He was a hunter, and he knew the value of patience.
You didn't rush the prey. You let it come to you.

"What if it's a trap?" Maya wondered aloud, her voice a small, worried sound.
"What if the Architect is on the other end of that message?"

"It's a risk we have to take," Lucky said, his voice gentle but firm.
"Freedom is always a risk."

He looked at his crew, at their tired, fearful faces.
He had led them here, to this place of ghosts and shadows.
He had asked them to trust him, to follow him on this desperate, foolhardy quest.
He owed them more than just courage.
He owed them honesty.

"Listen to me," he said, his voice drawing their attention.
"What we're doing... it's not just about survival anymore.
It's about sending a message.
That humanity doesn't just roll over and die.
That we don't trade our souls for safety.
That even in the face of a god, we can still say no."

He let the words hang in the air, a small testament to their defiance.
He didn't know if they would succeed.
He didn't even know if they would survive the day.
But he knew that they had to try.
That in the trying, they would find their own kind of victory.

And then, a flicker on the screen.

"We have a ship," Maya said, her voice a sudden, sharp intake of breath.
"It's old, a modified freighter. No transponder. It's running silent."

A silhouette emerged from the swirling dust of the nebula.
It was a battered, ugly ship, scarred and pitted from a hundred battles.
But to Lucky, it was the most beautiful thing he had ever seen.

"Open a channel," he said, his voice steady, his heart hammering against his ribs.
"Let's see who came to our party."

The comms crackled to life, and a face appeared on the screen.
A woman with a shaved head, a network of cybernetic tattoos covering one side of her face, and eyes that had seen too much.
She looked at them, her expression a mixture of suspicion and curiosity.

"You're the ones sending out invitations?" she asked, her voice a low, gravelly rasp.

Lucky met her gaze, a slow smile spreading across his face.
"We were hoping to start a revolution," he said.
"You in?"

The woman stared at him for a long moment, her cybernetic eye whirring softly.
Then, a slow, dangerous smile spread across her own face.
"You're either crazy or stupid," she said.
"But you're the first interesting thing to happen since the sky fell."

She leaned forward, her eyes glinting in the dim light of her cockpit.
"The name's Raven," she said.
"And I'm always in the mood for a little chaos."

Lucky laughed, a sound of pure, unadulterated relief.
It was a start.
A small, fragile, but very real start.
The first spark of a fire that would, with any luck, burn the new god from his throne.
The revolution had begun.
