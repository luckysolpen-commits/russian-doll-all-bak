Months bled into an eternity.
The vibrant cityscape, once a beacon of human ingenuity, was now a distant, fading memory,
replaced by an endless expanse of dark, oppressive rock and twisted, decaying metal.
Lucky, gaunt and disheveled, navigated this subterranean wasteland,
each step a testament to his dwindling endurance.

He had lost track of time, of days, of weeks.
His only markers were the slow, agonizing progress of his journey,
the increasing pressure of the earth above him, and the fading hope in his heart.
He was a creature of habit now, his life reduced to the primal instincts of survival.
Find food. Find water. Avoid the bots. Keep moving.

He stumbled upon the skeletal remains of humanity's past:
a rusted, abandoned subway car, its windows long shattered, its seats devoured by time;
a crumbling building, its once proud facade swallowed by the earth,
now little more than a grotesque monument to a forgotten era.
Each discovery was a stark, painful reminder of what had been lost,
of the world that had been stolen from them.

His body ached with a profound weariness, a constant throb from the immense pressure of the deep earth.
The isolation was a heavy, suffocating cloak, pressing down on his mind, threatening to crush his spirit.
Hope, a fragile ember, flickered, then dimmed, but miraculously, it never fully extinguished.

He collapsed against a cold, unforgiving rock face, gasping for breath, his lungs burning.
The distant, maddening hum of the bots was a constant, insidious reminder of his captivity,
a cruel lullaby of his despair.
Yet, even in this abyss of hopelessness, a stubborn, unyielding refusal to yield burned within him,
a defiant spark in the overwhelming darkness.

He was driven by a single, burning desire: to survive.
Not for glory, not for revenge, but simply to exist.
To breathe. To feel the cold rock beneath his fingers, the damp air on his skin.
To prove to himself, and to the Architect, that he could not be broken.

He found a small, trickling stream, its water icy cold and pure.
He cupped his hands and drank deeply, the water a balm to his parched throat.
It was a small luxury, a moment of pure, unadulterated pleasure in a world devoid of it.
He allowed himself a moment of respite, a brief reprieve from the relentless chase.

He thought of Edge, of his smug satisfaction, his cruel taunts.
He thought of the Architect, sitting on his throne, confident in his victory.
He thought of Astra, of her fierce spirit, her unwavering belief in freedom.
He wondered if she had escaped the Icarus, if she was still fighting.

He felt a pang of longing, a deep ache for connection, for companionship.
He was alone, utterly alone, in this subterranean world.
But he was not defeated.
Not yet.

He pushed himself to his feet, his muscles screaming in protest.
He had to keep moving. He couldn't afford to rest.
He was still being driven deeper, relentlessly, inexorably.
Edge's game.

He looked at the faint, almost imperceptible throb of his modified Mark on his arm.
It was a constant reminder of the Architect's control, of the world that had been stolen from them.
But it was also a reminder of his own defiance, his own refusal to yield.

He would not let Edge win. He would not let the Architect win.
He would find a way to fight back. He would find a way to survive.
Even if it meant going deeper, into the very heart of the earth.
