The isolation was a heavy, suffocating cloak, pressing down on Lucky's mind.
He had always been a man of action, of movement, of connection.
Now, he was a prisoner of silence, his only companions the echoes of his own thoughts.
He spoke aloud sometimes, just to hear the sound of a human voice,
even if it was his own, raspy and unfamiliar.

He found himself talking to the skeletal remains of humanity's past,
the rusted subway cars, the crumbling buildings.
He would tell them stories of the world above, of the sun, of the sky,
of the vibrant, chaotic life that had once thrived there.
He knew they couldn't hear him, but it helped to keep the madness at bay.

His body, though gaunt, had adapted to the harsh realities of the deep earth.
His eyes, once accustomed to the bright glare of the sun, now saw in the dimmest light.
His hearing, once dulled by the constant hum of city life, now picked up the faintest whisper of a distant drip, the scuttling of unseen creatures.
He was becoming a creature of the shadows, a denizen of the underworld.

He found a small, hidden chamber, its entrance obscured by a rockfall.
Inside, he discovered a cache of ancient supplies: dusty tins of nutrient paste,
a few bottles of purified water, and a tattered blanket.
It was a treasure trove, a lifeline in the abyss.
He rationed the supplies carefully, making them last as long as possible.
Each bite, each sip, was a victory, a small act of defiance against the crushing despair.

He spent hours exploring the labyrinthine passages, mapping them in his mind.
He found ancient ventilation shafts, long-forgotten maintenance tunnels,
and even a few crude shelters, built by those who had come before him.
He was a ghost in a ghost town, a survivor in a world of the dead.

He thought of Edge, of his smug satisfaction, his cruel taunts.
He thought of the Architect, sitting on his throne, confident in his victory.
He thought of Astra, of her fierce spirit, her unwavering belief in freedom.
He wondered if she had escaped the Icarus, if she was still fighting.

He felt a pang of longing, a deep ache for connection, for companionship.
He was alone, utterly alone, in this subterranean world.
But he was not defeated.
Not yet.

He pushed himself to his feet, his muscles screaming in protest.
He had to keep moving. He couldn't afford to rest.
He was still being driven deeper, relentlessly, inexorably.
Edge's game.

He looked at the faint, almost imperceptible throb of his modified Mark on his arm.
It was a constant reminder of the Architect's control, of the world that had been stolen from them.
But it was also a reminder of his own defiance, his own refusal to yield.

He would not let Edge win. He would not let the Architect win.
He would find a way to fight back. He would find a way to survive.
Even if it meant going deeper, into the very heart of the earth.
