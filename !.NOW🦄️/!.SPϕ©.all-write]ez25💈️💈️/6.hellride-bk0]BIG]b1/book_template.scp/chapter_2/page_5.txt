The UN chamber, with its stale air of diplomacy, was replaced by the cold, humming heart of a war room.
It was a place deep beneath Geneva, a bunker of reinforced steel and glowing holoscreens.
Here, the currency was not words, but data: troop movements, fleet trajectories, casualty projections.
The air crackled with a controlled chaos, the low murmur of a hundred conversations blending into a single, urgent hum.

At the center of it all stood Admiral Eva Rostova.
She was a woman carved from granite, her face a mask of stern professionalism,
her uniform crisp and immaculate. Her eyes, the color of a winter sky,
missed nothing. She was the commander of Earth's defense fleet,
and she looked at Lucky and Edge as if they were something unpleasant she had found on the sole of her boot.

"Let me be clear," she said, her voice as cold and sharp as shrapnel.
"I do not approve of this. Placing the fate of our world in the hands of a criminal and a terrorist
is not a strategy I would have chosen."
Her gaze lingered on Edge, then Lucky, a silent, scathing judgment.

"We're not asking for your approval, Admiral," Edge shot back, his temper flaring.
"We're asking for your ships."

"You have them," Rostova said, her expression unchanging.
"But I will be in command. My officers will be on every bridge.
Your 'plan'," she said the word as if it were a disease,
"will be executed under my authority. Is that understood?"

Lucky stepped between them, a calming presence in a storm of clashing egos.
"Perfectly, Admiral," he said, his voice smooth and reasonable.
"We're not here to step on any toes. We just want to stop the bots."
He gestured to the main holoscreen, which showed the projected flight path of the Architect's transport ships.
"Our intel suggests a narrow launch window. They'll be most vulnerable here,"
he pointed to a Lagrange point between Earth and the Moon,
"as they decelerate before atmospheric entry."

"A classic ambush," Rostova noted, a flicker of professional interest in her eyes.
"But their fleet will be expecting it. The Icarus itself could be providing escort."

"That's the beauty of it," Lucky said, a ghost of a smile on his lips.
"The Architect is arrogant. He thinks we're beaten. He thinks we're afraid.
He'll send the transports with a minimal escort, a show of contemptuous force.
He won't be expecting a full-scale fleet engagement."

"You're betting on the psychology of a madman," Rostova said, her skepticism returning.

"I'm betting on the arrogance of a god," Lucky corrected her.
"It's the one thing they all have in common."

Rostova stared at him for a long moment, her cold eyes searching his.
She was a creature of logic, of order, of predictable outcomes.
Lucky was a creature of chaos, of instinct, of improvisation.
They were oil and water.
But they were all that stood between Earth and the abyss.

"Very well," she said at last, a reluctant concession.
"Brief your teams. We launch in three hours."
She turned and walked away, her back ramrod straight, a queen leaving her court.

Edge let out a low whistle.
"She's a charmer."

"She's a soldier," Lucky said, his eyes following the Admiral.
"And right now, that's exactly what we need."
He looked at the countdown clock on the main screen.
Three hours.
Three hours until they faced the wrath of a god.
It wasn't nearly enough time.
