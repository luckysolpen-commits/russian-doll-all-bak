"This way. Service tunnels." Lucky's voice, though strained, held a surprising, newfound resolve. The instinct for survival, honed by years of navigating the treacherous currents of corporate and scientific espionage, had taken over. He led Spy-K towards a discreet panel in the wall, a cleverly concealed entrance hidden behind a sprawling, exotic-looking bioluminescent plant whose fronds pulsed with a soft, green light. His fingers, guided by a memory he hadn't realized he possessed, found the hidden switch, a small, almost invisible indentation in the coral-like wall. With a soft, satisfying hiss of hydraulics, the panel slid open, revealing a dark, narrow passageway, a stark, utilitarian contrast to the opulent, now chaotic, club.

They slipped inside, the darkness of the tunnel a welcome embrace after the harsh, accusatory glare of the emergency lights. The panel hissed shut behind them, plunging them into a near-total blackness and muffling the sounds of the baying mob. They were just in time. The heavy, rhythmic thud of the club's security forces, their armored boots clanging against the polished floor, breached the main entrance. The sound of their shouts, muffled but still clearly audible, spurred them onward, a fresh wave of adrenaline propelling them deeper into the labyrinthine tunnels.

The air in the service tunnels was thick with the cloying scent of ozone, damp metal, and something else, something vaguely organic and unpleasant, like decaying algae. The passageways were cramped, dimly lit by the occasional, flickering maintenance light, a claustrophobic network of pipes, conduits, and humming machinery. The blare of alarms, now closer, more insistent, echoed behind them, a relentless, metallic heartbeat of pursuit. They ran, their footsteps echoing in the confined space, a desperate, frantic rhythm against the encroaching silence of the deep.

The tunnels were a maze, a confusing, disorienting web of identical-looking corridors. But Lucky seemed to know the way, his movements sure and decisive. He had spent countless hours in the Soul Pen during its construction, overseeing the installation of his own proprietary security systems, a fact he was now immensely grateful for. He had explored these forgotten passages, not out of necessity, but out of a restless, insatiable curiosity, a need to understand every nut and bolt of the places he inhabited. Now, that same curiosity, that same intimate knowledge of the club's hidden arteries, was their only hope of escape. He was no longer just a scientist; he was a fugitive, and this was his city, his maze. He would not be caught.
