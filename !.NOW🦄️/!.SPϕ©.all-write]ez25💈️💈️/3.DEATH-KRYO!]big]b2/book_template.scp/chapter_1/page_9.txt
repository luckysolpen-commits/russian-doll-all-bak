**Page 9**

"They're rerouting power, trying to seal the emergency exits," Astra reported, her calm voice a stark contrast to the rising tension. Her eyes were glued to a datapad she held in her hand, its screen displaying a schematic of the tunnel network. Red lines, like angry, spreading veins, were appearing across the map, indicating locked-down sections, cutting off their potential escape routes one by one. "We're running out of time."

"Almost there," Lucky muttered, pushing himself harder, his lungs burning, his muscles screaming in protest. He skidded to a halt at a junction, the tunnels branching off into three seemingly identical paths. He closed his eyes for a moment, shutting out the distractions, the alarms, the fear. He concentrated, sifting through the memories of his childhood explorations, trying to recall the subtle details, the faint hum of distant machinery, the almost imperceptible shift in the air currents. He was a scientist, a man of logic and reason, but now he was relying on something more primal, on instinct, on a gut feeling that he couldn't explain but had learned to trust. Then, with a decisive, almost violent gesture, he pointed to the tunnel on the right.

"This one."

They followed him without question, their trust in him absolute, a silent, unwavering bond forged in the crucible of shared danger. The tunnel narrowed, the ceiling grew lower, forcing them to stoop. The air grew colder, heavier, the scent of the open ocean becoming more pronounced. Soon, the tunnel ended in a solid wall of rock and reinforced concrete, a dead end. The hope that had sustained them for the last few minutes threatened to evaporate.

"Dead end?" Gem's voice held a rare hint of despair, her usual fiery confidence momentarily extinguished. She placed a hand on the cold, rough surface of the wall, as if trying to will it to disappear.

Lucky, however, smiled faintly, a ghost of his usual mischievous, confident grin. "Not quite."

He ran his hand along the rough, cold surface of the wall, his fingers tracing an almost imperceptible seam, a faint line that was all but invisible in the dim light. He found a small, almost unnoticeable indentation, a hidden button disguised as a natural imperfection in the rock. He pressed it. For a moment, nothing happened. The silence was absolute, broken only by their ragged breathing. Then, with a low, protesting groan of ancient, protesting metal, a section of the wall began to slide open, revealing not more rock, but the vast, silent, inky blackness of the open ocean. The pressure differential was immense, yet the hidden door held, a testament to its forgotten, masterful construction.
