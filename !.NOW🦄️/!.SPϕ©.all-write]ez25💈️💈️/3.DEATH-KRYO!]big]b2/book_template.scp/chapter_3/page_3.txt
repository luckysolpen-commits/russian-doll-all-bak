They stopped in front of a large, imposing building, its facade stark and unadorned, a brutalist monolith that seemed to absorb the light around it. A sign, etched in glowing, crimson neon, proclaimed its identity: "Sub-Korea Research Institute." The name resonated with Lucky, a faint, distant bell in the vast library of his mind. He had heard whispers of Sub-Korea, a powerful, enigmatic tech conglomerate, a rising, aggressive force in the underwater economy. But he had always dismissed them as just another corporate giant, another shark in a sea of sharks.

"Sub-Korea?" Lucky questioned, a frown creasing his brow. "I thought they were just a tech company. A competitor. I never saw them as a threat."

Dorchester's lips curled into a cynical, knowing smile. "They're more than that," she said, her voice a low, dangerous purr. "They're a front. A very sophisticated, very well-funded front. They're using their technology, their influence, their wealth, to take control of the city, one sub-robot, one compromised politician, one corporate acquisition at a time." Her words sent a chill down Lucky's spine. The idea of a city being subtly, insidiously infiltrated, its autonomy eroded from within by an unseen, unknown enemy, was far more terrifying than the prospect of an outright, open invasion.

They found a vantage point overlooking the institute, a dilapidated, abandoned building across the street that offered a clear, unobstructed view of the entrance and the heavily fortified perimeter. From their hidden, shadowy perch, they observed the scene below. Small, spider-like sub-robots, their multi-jointed, metallic legs scuttling across the ground with an unnerving, insect-like efficiency, patrolled the perimeter. Their optical sensors, glowing with a menacing, predatory red light, constantly swept the area, their movements precise, coordinated, and utterly relentless. Security was tight, suffocatingly so, a clear, unambiguous indication that whatever was happening inside the institute, it was meant to be kept secret, hidden from prying eyes.

"How do we get in?" Gem whispered, her hand instinctively going to the hilt of her energy blade, her knuckles white. Her warrior's instinct was screaming at her, telling her that this place was a nexus of danger.

"We don't. Not yet," Dorchester replied, her eyes narrowed in thought, her mind a whirlwind of calculations and strategies. "First, we need to know what we're walking into. We need intelligence. We need to see inside the belly of the beast before we try to slay it. Raven, you're up." She turned to her younger sister, a mischievous, almost predatory, glint in her eye.
