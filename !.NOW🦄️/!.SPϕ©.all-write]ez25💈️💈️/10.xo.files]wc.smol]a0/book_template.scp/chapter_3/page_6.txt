Lucky stood at the edge of the clearing, a silent observer to a ritual he could not comprehend. He felt a profound sense of isolation, a chasm of cultural and experiential difference that separated him from the Afink people. They were united in their fear, in their faith, in their shared history. He was alone, a man of science in a world that defied all scientific explanation. He watched as they swayed to the rhythm of the chant, their eyes closed, their faces turned towards the sky. They were a single, unified entity, a collective consciousness that was bracing itself for the coming storm. He was an outsider, a spectator, a ghost at a funeral. He wanted to join them, to share in their ritual, to find some comfort in their collective strength. But he couldn’t. He didn’t know the words to their chant, the steps to their dance, the meaning behind their ritual. He was a stranger in a strange land, and he had never felt more alone. He felt a surge of anger, of frustration, of… jealousy. They had something he didn’t, something he had lost long ago. They had faith. They had a belief in something greater than themselves, a sense of purpose that was woven into the very fabric of their being. He had only his science, his logic, his reason. And in the face of this… this overwhelming, irrational, impossible reality, his science was useless. His logic was a flimsy shield against a tidal wave of cosmic power. His reason was a flickering candle in a hurricane of ancient magic. He was a man who had built his entire life on a foundation of facts and evidence. And now, that foundation was crumbling beneath his feet. He was lost. He was adrift. And for the first time in his life, he had no idea what to do.
