He found Lyra by the stream, her hands still, her gaze fixed on the water’s surface. The vibrant tapestry she was always weaving lay forgotten in her lap. She didn’t seem to notice his approach. Her focus was entirely inward, her brow furrowed in concentration. “Lyra?” he said softly, not wanting to startle her. She blinked, her dark eyes slowly coming back into focus. A flicker of something – fear? recognition? – passed through them before her expression settled into a familiar, placid calm. “Lucky,” she acknowledged, her voice a little strained. “Is something wrong?” “You tell me,” he said, sitting beside her on the smooth, moss-covered stones. “The whole village is on edge. This… hum. This feeling in the air. It’s getting worse.” He tried to frame it in scientific terms, a desperate attempt to impose order on the encroaching chaos. “It could be a geological phenomenon. Subsonic frequencies from deep within the planet’s crust. Or perhaps a form of atmospheric interference, some kind of electromagnetic…” He trailed off. The words sounded hollow, meaningless, even to his own ears. Lyra looked at him, and for the first time, he saw a hint of pity in her eyes. It was the look of an adult listening to a child’s naive explanation for a complex and terrifying reality. “It is not the earth that sings, Lucky,” she said, her voice barely a whisper. “And it is not the sky. It is them.” “Them?” he asked, though he already knew the answer. The image of the floating city, of the golden-eyed beings, flashed in his mind. “The Extrmophiles,” she confirmed, her voice trembling slightly. “They are waking up. And they are calling to us.”
