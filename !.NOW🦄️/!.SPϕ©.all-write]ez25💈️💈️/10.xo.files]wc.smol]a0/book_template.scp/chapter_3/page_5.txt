They were not words, not in any language he knew. They were a torrent of raw, unfiltered emotion, a cacophony of alien thought and sensation. He felt a surge of immense, ancient power, a consciousness so vast and so old that it made him feel like an insignificant insect. He felt a cold, calculating intelligence, a mind that was as sharp and as deadly as a shard of glass. He felt a hunger, a ravenous, insatiable hunger for… something. Something he couldn’t name, but that filled him with a primal dread. He snatched his hand back from the wall as if he had been burned, his heart hammering against his ribs. He stumbled back, his breath coming in ragged gasps. He looked at Lyra, his eyes wide with a mixture of terror and awe. “I… I heard them,” he stammered, his voice barely a whisper. Lyra nodded, her expression grim. “Now you understand,” she said softly. The hum in the air intensified, and the villagers began to gather in the central clearing, their faces a mixture of fear and reverence. They moved with a slow, deliberate grace, their movements almost ritualistic. Gorn emerged from his hut, his ancient face etched with a deep, profound sadness. He held a staff in his hand, a gnarled, black piece of wood that seemed to absorb the light. He began to chant, his voice a low, mournful sound that seemed to be the only thing that could stand against the encroaching hum. The other villagers joined in, their voices rising and falling in a hypnotic, otherworldly chorus. It was a sound of immense beauty and profound sorrow, a lament for a world that was about to change forever. It was the sound of a people facing their destiny. And it was a sound that would haunt Lucky for the rest of his days.
