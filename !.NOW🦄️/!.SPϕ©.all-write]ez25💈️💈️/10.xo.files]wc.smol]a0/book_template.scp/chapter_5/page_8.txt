Lyra, with a surge of courage that Lucky found both terrifying and admirable, stepped forward. Her hands were held out in a gesture of peace, a universal sign of non-aggression that she hoped would translate across species. Lucky followed, his guard still up, his heart hammering against his ribs. He was a scientist, not a diplomat. He was trained to observe, to analyze, to understand. But here, in this alien marketplace, he was just a man, trying to survive. And he was willing to follow Lyra, even if it meant walking into the lion’s den. As they emerged from the shadows, the Extrmophile women turned, their faces mirroring the curiosity of the tall one who had first noticed them. Their single, golden eyes, usually so cold and calculating, held a flicker of something akin to surprise, then a more focused, assessing gaze. The tall Extrmophile woman, taller than the others, with intricate, glowing patterns etched into her obsidian-like scales, looked up. Her single eye narrowed, her gaze piercing as she surveyed the shadows. She saw them. She had seen them all along. Her gaze lingered on Lyra, then shifted to Lucky. He felt her eye like a physical touch, assessing, probing, dissecting. He resisted the urge to flinch, to break eye contact, to show any sign of weakness. He stood his ground, his face a mask of carefully constructed neutrality. Lyra, her voice surprisingly steady, spoke in a language that was a mixture of clicks and guttural sounds, a language that Lucky had only just begun to understand. “We mean no harm,” she said, her hands still held out in that universal gesture of peace. “We are just travelers seeking shelter and trade.” The tall Extrmophile woman’s gaze lingered on Lyra, then shifted back to Lucky. He felt her eye like a physical touch, assessing, probing. He resisted the urge to flinch. He was a scientist, a man of logic and reason. But here, in this alien marketplace, he was just a man, trying to survive. And he was willing to do whatever it took.
