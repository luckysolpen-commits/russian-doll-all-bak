Lucky followed Lyra, his mind a whirlwind of confusion and terror. The hut, once a symbol of warmth and safety, was now a beacon of something ancient and terrifying. As they approached, the glowing patterns on the mud-brick wall began to writhe, to twist, to pulsate with an inner light that was both beautiful and unsettling. And then, from the very heart of the glowing patterns, tendrils of a dark, viscous, ink-like substance began to ooze. Slowly at first, like thick, black blood seeping from a wound, then with increasing speed. They snaked out, thick and glistening, like living vines, their surfaces shimmering with an eerie, internal violet light. The air grew heavy, thick with the scent of ozone and something else, something metallic and ancient, like rust and forgotten machinery. Lucky’s scientific mind, even in the face of such impossible phenomena, tried to categorize, to analyze. Was it a non-Newtonian fluid? A highly advanced form of nanotechnology? A biological construct? But the questions were meaningless. This defied all known laws of physics, of chemistry, of biology. This was… magic. “The Ink of the Ancients,” Lyra breathed, her voice filled with a mixture of awe and dread. “It has begun.” She reached out a hand, as if to touch the pulsating tendrils, but Lucky pulled her back, his heart hammering against his ribs. “Don’t touch it!” he yelled, his voice raw with fear. But Lyra just looked at him, her eyes wide and unblinking. “It is not meant to harm us, Lucky,” she said, her voice calm, almost serene. “It is meant to… take us.”
