The hound he had hit with the club was the first to react. It leaped at him, its jaws snapping, its golden eyes burning with a cold, predatory light. But Lucky was ready. He sidestepped the attack, bringing the rock down on the creature’s back with all his strength. The hound howled, a sound of pure agony, and collapsed, its spine shattered. The other hound, seeing its companion fall, hesitated for a moment, its golden eyes wide with what looked like surprise. It was a fatal mistake. Lucky didn’t give it time to think. He charged, his makeshift club a blur of motion. He swung for the head, the gnarled branch connecting with a sickening thud. The hound’s skull caved in, and it fell to the ground without a sound. He stood over the two dead creatures, his chest heaving, his body trembling with the aftershocks of the fight. He was covered in their blood, a thick, black liquid that smelled of ozone and rust. He had done it. He had survived. He had killed. He felt a wave of nausea, and he leaned against the rock formation, vomiting. The taste of bile and fear filled his mouth. He was a scientist, a man of peace. He had never killed anything in his life. But this world… this world was changing him. It was stripping away the layers of civilization, revealing the primal core beneath. And he wasn’t sure he liked what he was seeing. He looked at the two dead hounds, their bodies already beginning to attract the attention of the jungle’s scavengers. He felt a pang of something that wasn’t quite remorse, but close to it. They were just animals, hunting for food. They were a part of this world, and he was the intruder. He had no right to be here. He had no right to kill them. But he had no choice. It was them or him. And he had chosen himself. He took a deep breath, the alien air filling his lungs. He was alive. That was all that mattered. He would mourn the man he used to be later. Right now, he had to keep moving. He had to find shelter. He had to find a way home.
