One of the hounds lunged, a blur of black fur and golden eyes. Lucky swung his club, the gnarled branch connecting with the creature’s skull with a sickening crack. The hound yelped, a high-pitched sound of pain and surprise, and stumbled back. But the other one was already on him. It slammed into him with the force of a freight train, its claws digging into his shoulders, its hot breath on his face. He could feel its teeth, sharp and needle-like, grazing his cheek. He cried out, a raw, primal scream of pain and rage. He brought his knee up, slamming it into the creature’s soft underbelly. The hound grunted, its grip loosening for a fraction of a second. It was enough. He pushed the creature off him, scrambling back, his heart hammering against his ribs. The first hound, the one he had hit with the club, was circling him again, its golden eyes narrowed, its lips curled back in a snarl. The other one was getting to its feet, shaking its head, its eyes burning with a renewed fury. He was trapped. They were going to tear him apart. He felt a wave of despair wash over him, so powerful it almost brought him to his knees. But then he saw it. A large, loose rock, just within his reach. He didn’t hesitate. He grabbed the rock, its rough surface digging into his palm. It was heavy, solid. He felt a surge of adrenaline, a primal energy that coursed through his veins. He was not going to die here. He was not going to be food for these alien beasts. He was a man. And he was going to fight like one. He let out a roar, a challenge to the jungle, to the planet, to the universe. He was Lucky. And his luck had not run out yet. He charged, the rock held high, a primitive weapon in a world of impossible technology. He was a caveman fighting monsters. And he was going to win.
